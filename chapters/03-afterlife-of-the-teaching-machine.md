# On the Afterlife of the Teaching Machine

*Essai 3*

---

In 1954, Burrhus Frederic Skinner gave a talk at the University of Pittsburgh in which he described his recent observation of his own daughter Deborah's fourth-grade arithmetic class. What he had seen, he reported, had come as a "great shock." The teacher was unable to provide what Skinner would later call the "contingencies of reinforcement" — the immediate, precise feedback that his decades of work on operant conditioning had established as the mechanism by which behavior is shaped. Students performed work, they waited, the work was collected, and perhaps the next day or several days later they received back a paper with marks on it. By the time the marks arrived, the behavior they were supposed to reinforce had moved on. The contingencies were broken. Learning, Skinner believed, was being left to chance in the one institution society most required to be systematic about it.

Skinner's response to this observation became, within four years, the teaching machine — a device that presented instructional material one frame at a time, required the student to respond, and provided immediate verification of whether the response was correct. In his 1958 paper in *Science* titled simply "Teaching Machines," Skinner laid out both the theoretical basis for the device and the evidence he had accumulated that it worked. The paper is one of the most cited documents in the history of educational technology. It also, I want to argue, initiated the specific measurement apparatus — the specific choices about what to measure and what to ignore — that educational technology efficacy research has inherited, with remarkably little revision, across six subsequent decades and at least four distinct waves of technological change.

This essai traces that inheritance. It asks what Skinner's teaching machine was designed to measure, what it was not designed to measure, and how these specific choices persisted through Patrick Suppes's 1960s computer-assisted instruction, through John Anderson and Kenneth Koedinger's 1990s Cognitive Tutor, through Knewton and DreamBox and i-Ready in the 2010s, and into the contemporary AI-tutor wave — the Khanmigo and Kestin and Eedi and Rori studies the book's later essais examine in detail. The apparatus, I want to show, has remained stable. The technologies that implement it have changed profoundly. But what counts as evidence that a learning-systems technology works — the specific measurements that the field accepts as demonstrating efficacy — has been largely inherited rather than revised across six decades.

At the end of the essai I will read one specific contemporary study carefully, applying the apparatus this essai has just surfaced — a 2021 *Foreign Language Annals* paper by Xiangying Jiang, Joseph Rollinson, Luke Plonsky, Erin Gustafson, and Bozena Pajak, titled *Evaluating the reading and listening outcomes of beginning-level Duolingo courses*. Four of the five authors were employed by Duolingo at the time of publication. The study is peer-reviewed, methodologically careful by contemporary conventions, and reaches conclusions about Duolingo's effectiveness that are cited in Duolingo's own marketing materials. It is also, I will argue, a study whose measurement choices are traceable, line by line, to Skinner's 1958 paper. What the study measures is what Skinner's apparatus measures. What the study does not measure is what Skinner's apparatus did not measure. The specific inheritance the essai documents is still active, still shaping what the field reports as evidence that educational technology works.

I want to be specific about what this essai is and is not doing. It is not arguing that Skinner was wrong, that Suppes was wrong, that Anderson was wrong, that the researchers doing EdTech efficacy work in the 2020s are wrong. Each generation of researchers has been serious, methodologically competent, and working in good faith within the measurement traditions available to them. The argument is not that individual researchers have been careless. The argument is that the *apparatus itself* — the set of measurement choices that have counted as evidence of learning — has been inherited rather than examined, and that the inheritance pattern has consequences the field has not fully reckoned with. Naming the apparatus is not condemning the workers. The essai is a trial of the apparatus, not of the people operating it.

---

Begin with Skinner's 1958 paper, which is more measured than its reputation suggests.

Skinner's *Teaching Machines* in *Science* describes the specific mechanism of the device in detail. A student sits at the machine. A frame — a small amount of instructional material with one or more blanks — is presented through a window. The student writes a response in an adjacent panel. The student operates a lever. The machine reveals whether the response was correct. If correct, the student advances to the next frame. If incorrect, the student may be asked to retry, or the material may be represented in a slightly different form.

What does the machine measure?

The 1958 paper is explicit. The machine measures the accuracy of each individual response, frame by frame. It measures the response time — how long the student took to produce the response. It measures the progression — how many frames the student has completed, at what rate. It measures the error pattern — which frames produce incorrect responses, at what points in the sequence, with what distribution. These measurements are not incidental to Skinner's design; they are the point of the design. Skinner's behavioral science, across decades of laboratory work, had established that the precise recording of response patterns was the foundation of understanding how behavior is shaped. The teaching machine extended this laboratory precision into the educational context. What the machine records is what Skinner's science believed was relevant about learning.

What does the machine *not* measure?

This is the question that matters for the inheritance argument, and it is the question Skinner's 1958 paper is largely silent on. The machine does not measure whether the student retains the material two weeks, two months, or two years after the machine is removed. It does not measure whether the student can apply what was learned in contexts that differ from the machine's frame-by-frame presentation format. It does not measure whether the student could have acquired the same capability more efficiently, or more durably, or at lower cost, through alternative means. It does not measure what the student can do with the material beyond producing the specific responses the machine was designed to elicit. These non-measurements are not oversights. They reflect the commitments of Skinner's behavioral framework: behavior is the object of study, immediate contingencies are the mechanism, and generalization is expected to follow naturally from properly shaped behavior without requiring separate empirical verification.

Skinner's specific evidence for the teaching machine's effectiveness, in the 1958 paper, follows directly from these measurement choices. He describes the machine's ability to produce high accuracy rates on the frames it presents. He describes the rate at which students progress through the material. He describes the reduction in teacher time required to grade student work. These are real achievements, documented honestly within the terms Skinner established. The paper's effectiveness claims are not exaggerated; they are precise. What is significant is what the precision does and does not encompass. The machine is effective at producing the specific behaviors the machine was designed to record. Whether the behaviors persist, transfer, or represent what the field would later call *learning* in the deeper sense Ann Brown and the learning sciences came to articulate, Skinner does not address — because his framework did not require him to.

I want to name a specific discipline here because it matters for how the essai reads Skinner and his successors. The question is not whether Skinner should have measured differently. The question is whether the measurement choices he made were ever systematically re-examined by the researchers who inherited his apparatus. My argument across the rest of this essai is that they were not. The apparatus was inherited as if its choices were neutral rather than theoretical — as if measuring accuracy-per-frame-at-immediate-timescale were simply what evidence-of-learning looked like, rather than one specific operationalization that Skinner's behavioral science had specifically justified. The inheritance flattened the theoretical commitments into methodological conventions. The conventions persisted long after the behavioral framework that justified them was largely abandoned.

Move to Patrick Suppes.

In 1966, Patrick Suppes at Stanford published *The Uses of Computers in Education* in *Scientific American*. The paper's opening framing remains famous: computers, Suppes wrote, would allow "millions of schoolchildren" to have access to the "personal services of a tutor as well-informed and responsive as Aristotle." This is the framing that the Bloom 2-sigma framing of 1984 would later rhyme with, and that the Khan Academy 2023 framing of AI tutoring would rhyme with again.

What Suppes actually built at Stanford, in collaboration with Richard Atkinson, was a computer-assisted instruction system for mathematics drill. The system presented arithmetic problems to elementary students. Students typed answers. The computer verified correctness and adjusted the difficulty of subsequent problems based on performance. This was, in important respects, the teaching machine translated into 1960s mainframe technology.

What did the Suppes system measure?

The 1966 paper documents Suppes's measurements with characteristic clarity. The system recorded the proportion of correct responses for each student across each problem type. Arithmetic accuracy rates improved from roughly 53 percent at baseline to over 90 percent after a period of drill. The system recorded response time — the average time required to produce an answer — and documented its reduction from 630 seconds to 279 seconds over the course of a year. The system recorded item-level difficulty patterns, identifying which specific problem types were "troublesome." The system recorded time-outs — instances where students failed to respond within a set limit. These are the measurements Suppes's paper reports as evidence that the system worked.

The apparatus is unmistakable. Accuracy per item, time per response, progression, error patterns. These are Skinner's measurements, with the technology shifted from mechanical device to computer mainframe. Suppes did not inherit Skinner's behavioral framework in the theoretical sense; Suppes was interested in mathematical models of learning and in the computer as a medium for individualized instruction. But Suppes inherited Skinner's measurement apparatus wholesale. What counted as evidence of the system's effectiveness was exactly what had counted as evidence of the teaching machine's effectiveness — the specific behaviors the system was designed to shape and record.

And what did Suppes not measure? Again, the list is consistent. No delayed post-testing of whether the arithmetic facility persisted once students stopped using the system. No transfer testing to determine whether the improved arithmetic performance translated to mathematical reasoning in other contexts. No comparison to alternative instructional approaches at matched cost. No examination of whether the 90 percent accuracy rate reflected deep understanding or shallow pattern-matching on the specific item formats the system presented. Suppes's paper is internally consistent and methodologically careful within its own terms. What it inherits without examination is the question of whether the terms themselves are the right terms.

PLATO, the other major 1960s CAI system developed at the University of Illinois by Donald Bitzer and colleagues, extended the same apparatus to a wider range of subjects and added sophisticated graphical capabilities. The PLATO efficacy literature from the late 1960s and 1970s inherits the same measurement pattern: accuracy rates, completion rates, time-on-task, performance on aligned post-tests. The innovation is in the technology. The continuity is in what the technology measures.

---

Skip to Anderson's Cognitive Tutor, which the book's seventh essai examined at length.

John Anderson, Albert Corbett, Kenneth Koedinger, and Ray Pelletier's 1995 *Journal of the Learning Sciences* paper *Cognitive Tutors: Lessons Learned* represents, in one sense, a profound theoretical shift from Skinner and Suppes. Cognitive Tutor was built on the ACT-R theory of cognition — a cognitive-psychological framework that treated learning as the acquisition of production rules (if-then pairings connecting goal states to actions) rather than as the shaping of behavior through reinforcement. The theoretical language moved from reinforcement to cognition, from behavior to knowledge, from stimulus-response to information-processing.

And yet.

What did Cognitive Tutor measure?

The 1995 paper documents the measurement apparatus with precision. The tutor recorded step-level correctness — whether each student action matched one of the production rules the cognitive model had identified as correct. It recorded time per step. It recorded hint requests and hint-level utilization. It recorded error patterns and their correspondence to stored "buggy" productions representing common misconceptions. It recorded estimated mastery of each production rule through a Bayesian knowledge-tracing algorithm. These are the measurements that determined when a student was allowed to advance to new material.

The theoretical framework changed. The units changed from frames to production rules. The statistical machinery became substantially more sophisticated. But the shape of the apparatus — what the tutor attended to as evidence of learning — is the shape Skinner specified. Step-level correctness, time per step, progression through atomized units, error patterns that trigger remediation. These are the same measurement choices, renamed and retheorized, operating on finer-grained units of analysis. Cognitive Tutor measured what the teaching machine measured, at higher resolution, within a more sophisticated theoretical framework, without departing from the apparatus's fundamental commitments.

Anderson and colleagues were, I want to emphasize, transparent about what their system measured and did not measure. The 1995 paper is a remarkable document of methodological honesty. The authors acknowledge that transfer beyond the tutor environment depends on the match between the tutor's interface and the transfer environment — a direct quotation from the paper reads that "students... display transfer... to the degree that they can map the tutor environment into the test environment." This is an acknowledgment that the tutor's effectiveness, as measured by aligned post-tests, does not necessarily generalize to contexts that differ from the tutor's specific presentation format. The authors also note that their system works better when presented to students as a non-human tool rather than as an emulation of a human tutor — an acknowledgment that what the tutor does is different from what human tutoring involves, a distinction the seventh essai of this volume examined in detail.

This methodological transparency is what makes the inheritance pattern visible rather than obscured. Anderson and colleagues did not hide what their system measured and what it did not. They named the limits of aligned-outcome transfer explicitly in their own 1995 paper. The inheritance pattern is not that researchers misrepresented their work; it is that the measurement conventions persisted even when the researchers themselves acknowledged their limits. A subsequent literature that cited Cognitive Tutor's effect sizes without preserving Anderson's own caveats was doing something the original paper did not do. The apparatus inherits even what its originators flagged as provisional.

Art Graesser's AutoTutor, developed at the University of Memphis in the late 1990s and early 2000s, introduced natural-language dialogue into the intelligent-tutoring-system tradition. The technological shift was substantial; the measurement shift was modest. AutoTutor's efficacy evidence rested on accuracy of student responses within the dialogue, coverage of expected content elements in student explanations, and post-test performance on assessments aligned with the dialogue topics. The measurement apparatus was inherited. The technology enabled a different *kind* of interaction — conversational rather than problem-solving — but the question of what counted as evidence that the interaction had produced learning remained anchored in aligned-outcome performance at immediate timescale.

---

The commercial adaptive-learning era of the 2010s — Knewton, DreamBox Learning, i-Ready, ALEKS, and their many imitators — represents the point at which the inheritance became industrial.

Knewton, led by José Ferreira during its 2012-2015 peak of public prominence, positioned itself as a platform that could use machine learning and large-scale behavioral data to personalize instruction at the sub-item level. Ferreira's public framing invoked the Suppes promise — individualized instruction at scale — in the language of twenty-first-century data science. The platform processed millions of interactions across student populations to identify patterns and optimize instructional sequences. The measurement apparatus, in the public claims, was usage data: how students interacted with the platform, what problems they attempted, what hints they took, how their patterns of engagement correlated with eventual performance. Rigorous independent efficacy research on Knewton was notably sparse during the period of its public prominence — a fact that contributed to the eighth essai's discussion of adaptive learning's evaluation thinness.

DreamBox Learning, focused on elementary mathematics, became one of the more researched adaptive platforms, largely through the work of the Harvard Center for Education Policy Research. A 2016 CEPR study examined the relationship between DreamBox usage and student achievement, measured by performance on the NWEA MAP (Measures of Academic Progress) test and state standardized assessments. The study reported that students at the median would gain 1.4 to 3.9 percentile points on the MAP for approximately 7 to 8 hours of DreamBox usage. The population spanned thousands of students in the Howard County Public School System and Rocketship Education; the duration spanned one to two school years.

The measurement apparatus is recognizable. The outcome measures are aligned — not with DreamBox's specific internal curriculum, but with standardized tests that cover mathematics in ways broadly consistent with what DreamBox teaches. The input measure is usage — time on task, lessons completed. The relationship between input and outcome is statistical, across large populations. The theoretical framework is loose. What has changed from Skinner and Suppes and Cognitive Tutor is the scale of the data and the statistical machinery for handling it. What has remained stable is the fundamental question: does the system, as measured by performance on aligned assessments at the end of the instructional period, correlate with gains compared to non-users?

The CEPR study itself includes a specific acknowledgment that, I want to note, is methodologically honest. The researchers observe that DreamBox usage may "partially reflect students' motivation levels" — which means the correlation between usage and achievement may reflect that motivated students both use DreamBox more and learn more, regardless of DreamBox's instructional contribution. This is the same class of acknowledgment Anderson and colleagues made about Cognitive Tutor's transfer limits. The researchers know the limits of what their apparatus can establish. The limits are acknowledged in the paper. The subsequent citations of the paper often do not preserve the acknowledgments.

i-Ready, produced by Curriculum Associates, operates with similar methodological choices and a characteristic internal feature worth naming. The platform's efficacy research typically demonstrates that students who achieve "usage fidelity" — meeting recommended minutes per week of engagement — show higher scores on the i-Ready Diagnostic. The i-Ready Diagnostic is itself calibrated to predict state test performance. This creates what one observer has called a circular measurement loop: the system measures how well students learn to do well on the assessment the system itself provides, with the assessment designed to predict the external state test that is the ostensible ultimate standard. The apparatus has become recursive. The inheritance from Skinner — accuracy on aligned items — has been institutionalized as the industry's measurement standard, with the alignment now engineered at the product-design level rather than simply present as a natural feature of drill-and-practice.

ALEKS, which the book's seventh essai discussed, inherits the apparatus with its own theoretical framing through Knowledge Space Theory. Efficacy claims rest on performance within the system's own internal knowledge mapping, supplemented by aligned post-tests that measure progression through the curricular content the system teaches. The pattern is consistent.

---

The current AI-tutor wave — the essais Ten and Eleven examine in detail — inherits the apparatus in its turn. I will be brief here because the detailed examinations live elsewhere in the volume.

Khanmigo's evaluation evidence, as the eleventh essai documented, has rested largely on engagement data and on performance within Khan Academy's own internal assessment structures. What has been measured at scale is usage patterns; what has been claimed is educational transformation; what has not been established at the level of rigorous efficacy research is learning gains on independent standardized measures at delayed timescales with cost-inclusive reporting. The apparatus's characteristic gaps are visible.

Kestin et al. 2024's Harvard physics study, as the tenth essai documented in detail, reported effect sizes of 0.73 to 1.3 sigma on researcher-designed post-tests covering the specific content (surface tension and fluid flow) that the two-hour intervention taught. The timescale from intervention to assessment was short. The baseline was a single-session active-learning classroom. The outcome measure was aligned with the intervention. The apparatus is inherited.

Eedi with Google Research's 2025 study, as the tenth essai documented, introduced an important measurement advance — transfer testing on novel problems from subsequent topics. This is a genuine departure from the aligned-outcome convention. The study's N of 165 and single-term duration remain short by comparison to what durability research would require, but the outcome measure itself represents a methodological step that the book's later essais credit specifically. The Eedi study is the current literature's single strongest example of what measurement-apparatus revision, rather than inheritance, would look like.

Rori in Ghana, as the tenth essai documented, used external curriculum-aligned math assessment (from the Global Proficiency Framework) over an eight-month period — a meaningfully longer timescale than most contemporary AI-tutor studies, with an outcome measure that was not designed by the intervention's developers. The apparatus is partially reformed. The cost reporting — $5 per student per year — represents another methodological advance that the book's twelfth essai treats as exemplary.

Across the wave, measurement-apparatus inheritance is the dominant pattern. Measurement-apparatus revision exists in specific studies at specific points, as credits to the researchers who have chosen to work against the inherited conventions. The exceptions do not yet constitute a new standard for the field.

Now Jiang et al. 2021.

The paper is *Evaluating the reading and listening outcomes of beginning-level Duolingo courses*, published in *Foreign Language Annals*, volume 54, issue 4, in 2021. The authors are Xiangying Jiang, Joseph Rollinson, Luke Plonsky, Erin Gustafson, and Bozena Pajak. Four of the five authors were employed by Duolingo at the time of publication; the fifth, Luke Plonsky, is an academic researcher at Northern Arizona University specializing in applied linguistics. The paper is peer-reviewed in a journal that is among the more established in foreign-language education research.

The research question the paper asks is this: what ACTFL (American Council on the Teaching of Foreign Languages) proficiency levels do adult Duolingo learners achieve in listening and reading when they complete the beginning-level (A2) courses? This is a specific operationalization: what level of performance, on a specific external assessment, do people who finish a specific subset of Duolingo's content reach?

The population is 225 adults in the United States — 135 studying Spanish, 90 studying French. The sample was drawn from Duolingo's user base. Participants were required to have "little to no prior proficiency" in their target language, to be using Duolingo as their "only learning tool," and — this is the consequential selection criterion — to have completed the beginning-level course content (Unit 4, the end of what Duolingo classifies as A2). The sample, as reported in the paper, skewed toward highly educated Caucasian Americans with bachelor's or master's degrees.

The outcome measure is the STAMP 4S test — the Standards-based Measurement of Proficiency assessment from Avant Assessment. The reading and listening sections of the STAMP 4S each consist of 30 multiple-choice items. The assessment covers reading and listening proficiency in the target language. The post-test was administered immediately after learners completed the beginning-level content.

The paper's reported finding is that the Duolingo learners reached the ACTFL Intermediate Low level in reading and the Novice High level in listening — levels the paper characterizes as "comparable with those of university students at the end of the fourth semester" of college-level language study.

I want to apply the measurement apparatus to this study and see what the apparatus reveals.

First, the outcome measure. The STAMP 4S is an external assessment — not designed by Duolingo, not specifically aligned to Duolingo's curriculum. This is a methodological improvement over the purely internal-assessment pattern common in EdTech efficacy research. The assessment does cover reading and listening comprehension broadly rather than Duolingo's specific content. However, reading and listening are also the specific modalities that Duolingo's interface is designed around — multiple-choice comprehension items, translation tasks, listening exercises with visual or multiple-choice responses. Speaking and writing, which are modalities Duolingo's app-based format supports weakly, are explicitly excluded from the study. The measurement is therefore aligned with the specific subset of language proficiency that Duolingo's interface is engineered to build. The STAMP 4S is external; the choice to measure only reading and listening makes the assessment narrower in a way that favors Duolingo's specific strengths.

Second, the timescale. The post-test is administered immediately after completion of the beginning-level content. There is no delayed assessment at three months, six months, or one year after course completion. The Bjork distinction between retrieval strength (immediate accessibility) and storage strength (durable retention) is directly relevant here: the STAMP 4S scores reflect what Duolingo users can do at the moment they finish the course, not what they can do later. Whether the Intermediate Low reading proficiency persists six months after the user stops using Duolingo is not tested. This is the same timescale pattern the apparatus has inherited across six decades.

Third, the selection criterion. The study assessed only learners who *completed* the beginning-level content. Duolingo's attrition rates, like those of most app-based learning platforms, are substantial — most users who download the app never reach the end of the beginning-level content. The population measured is therefore the "survivors" of Duolingo's reinforcement structure. What 100 people who completed the course achieved is different from what 100 people who started it achieved. The paper is transparent about this selection criterion, but the framing of the findings — *Duolingo users reach Intermediate Low in reading* — is routinely re-expressed in Duolingo's marketing materials as *Duolingo is as effective as four semesters of college Spanish*, without preserving the completion-threshold restriction. The effective claim elides the selection.

Fourth, the baseline. The comparison is historical — "comparable with those of university students at the end of the fourth semester." There is no contemporaneous control group of comparable students using a different learning approach. The comparison is between Duolingo completers' immediate post-test scores and historical averages from a different population under different conditions. This is a defensible approach given the study's observational design, but it is not an experimental comparison. The *comparable to four semesters of college* framing treats the two groups as if their outcomes had been measured under equivalent conditions, which they have not.

Fifth, the cost. The study does not report per-student cost or time-cost. Duolingo is free to users at its base tier, which has specific rhetorical value — the framing is that free app is comparable to paid college course — but the comparison elides the substantial time investment Duolingo users put in (typical completion times run hundreds of hours), the forgone opportunity cost of that time, and the question of what the same time investment in a structurally different approach (human-tutored instruction, immersion experience, structured self-study with textbook and audio materials) would produce. The cost denominator, as the twelfth essai of this volume argued, is constitutive of what a comparative claim supports. Its absence here is characteristic of the apparatus.

Sixth, the author affiliations. Four of the five authors were Duolingo employees at the time of publication. This is not a disqualifying fact — industry-funded research can be rigorous, and the paper does note the affiliations transparently. It is a contextualizing fact. When a study of a product is authored primarily by the product's developers, the specific measurement choices they make — what to measure, what to exclude, what comparisons to draw — warrant attention. Which outcome modalities were measured (reading and listening — Duolingo's strengths) and which were not (speaking and writing — Duolingo's weaknesses) reflects choices that a non-affiliated research team might have made differently.

What emerges from applying the apparatus to Jiang et al. 2021 is not that the study is fraudulent. The study is methodologically careful within its own terms. What emerges is that the specific choices the study makes about what to measure, how, over what timescale, with what comparison, at what cost — these choices are exactly the choices Skinner's teaching machine made in 1958, refreshed across six intervening decades of technological change. Aligned outcome measure. Short timescale. Selection of successful users as the study population. Comparison to a baseline that elides context. No cost denominator. The apparatus has persisted. The technology has changed. What counts as evidence that the technology works has been inherited rather than examined.

There have been alternatives across the same six decades. The apparatus persisted in spite of them, not in the absence of them.

Edward Thorndike's work on transfer of learning, published in papers in 1906 and 1924, established that improvement in one mental function rarely produces general improvement in others unless the two share "identical elements." Thorndike's methodological implication was that learning gains claimed by any specific instructional intervention must be tested outside the conditions of the intervention — in contexts structurally different from the training environment — to establish what the training has actually produced. Transfer testing, as Thorndike conceived it over a century ago, has existed as an available methodology for the entire history of educational technology efficacy research. It has been used occasionally, praised routinely, and treated as impractical or aspirational rather than as baseline standard. The Eedi 2025 study's inclusion of transfer testing is a credit to its researchers in part because Thorndike's tradition has remained largely unadopted in the broader EdTech efficacy literature.

Robert and Elizabeth Bjork's *New Theory of Disuse*, developed through the 1980s and codified in the 1992 Bjork and Bjork chapter, established the empirical distinction between retrieval strength (current performance) and storage strength (durable retention). The Bjorks' research program demonstrated, repeatedly, that conditions maximizing immediate performance (massed practice, aligned testing, minimal difficulty) could actively impair long-term storage, while conditions introducing what they called "desirable difficulties" (spaced practice, interleaving, retrieval practice) could depress immediate performance while building substantially more durable learning. The methodological implication is unmistakable: immediate post-test performance is at best an incomplete, at worst a misleading, measure of what a student has learned. Delayed post-testing is required to distinguish performance from learning. The Bjork distinction has been canonical in the learning sciences for over three decades. Its adoption in educational technology efficacy research has been sporadic.

John Bransford, Ann Brown, and Rodney Cocking's *How People Learn*, the 1999 National Academies consensus volume, argued explicitly that assessment should "tap understanding rather than merely the ability to repeat facts" and advocated for measurement approaches that capture deep conceptual learning rather than surface-level performance. The volume synthesized two decades of learning-sciences research into a clear methodological challenge to the aligned-outcome convention. The challenge was widely read, widely cited, and only narrowly operationalized in efficacy research practice.

Samuel Messick's *Validity* chapter in the 1989 edition of *Educational Measurement* — which the thirteenth essai of this volume examines in detail — provided the most sophisticated framework available for thinking about what a test score actually supports as an inference about learning. Messick's unified theory of validity specified that a test score's interpretation requires examination of construct-relevant versus construct-irrelevant variance, construct under-representation, and the consequences of the test's use. Messick's framework, applied rigorously, would require EdTech efficacy research to examine what its outcome measures actually index rather than assuming that performance-on-aligned-items equals evidence-of-learning. The framework has been standard in measurement theory for over three decades. Its rigorous application in educational technology efficacy has been partial at best.

These alternatives have not been hidden. They are taught in graduate programs in educational psychology, measurement, and the learning sciences. They are cited in methods sections of EdTech efficacy papers. What has not happened, across six decades of technology change, is their adoption as the standard apparatus of the field. The inherited apparatus — aligned outcomes at immediate timescale, with selection of engaged users, against historical or minimal baselines, without cost denomination — has remained dominant. The alternatives have remained alternative.

---

I want to step back and name what this pattern means.

The measurement apparatus of the learning-systems field is not a neutral product of scientific progress. It is a specific theoretical commitment — Skinner's, initially — that has been inherited across six decades of technological change, carrying specific assumptions about what counts as evidence of learning into contexts whose theoretical frameworks have long since moved beyond Skinner's behaviorism. What this means is that when contemporary AI-tutor research reports that an intervention produced a 0.73 sigma effect on a researcher-designed post-test aligned with the intervention content after a short intervention window, with cost unreported, the finding does specific work that is traceable in structure and kind to Skinner's 1958 claims about the teaching machine. The technology has become incomprehensibly more powerful. The apparatus of evidence has changed far less.

The question the essai invites you to carry forward is simple. When you encounter an educational technology efficacy claim — an advertisement from a company, a press release from a university, a peer-reviewed paper in a journal — apply the apparatus's characteristic questions. What did the outcome measure? Was it aligned with what the intervention taught? Over what timescale from intervention to assessment? Against what baseline? With what population selection? At what cost? If the answers match the Skinnerian pattern — aligned outcome, immediate timescale, engaged user selection, historical or weak baseline, no cost denominator — the finding you are being asked to treat as novel evidence of educational technology's promise is, in structure, a contemporary instance of the apparatus Skinner initiated. It may be right or it may be wrong. What it cannot be, in the absence of the alternatives Thorndike and Bjork and Bransford and Messick have long specified, is the definitive evidence of transformative learning its rhetoric typically invokes.

This is the pattern the volume's next several essais examine as it manifests in specific traditions: the Bloom 2-sigma framing in Essai Four, the comparative sigma claims that treat non-commensurable studies as commensurable in Essai Five, the meta-analytic aggregation that smooths the apparatus's characteristic features in Essai Six. Each essai after this one tells a specific story about how the apparatus shapes particular claims in particular ways. This essai has traced the apparatus itself — its origins, its inheritance, its active operation in a contemporary study you can read carefully yourself. Whether the essais that follow show you a pattern you recognize from your own reading, or whether they show you something you have not encountered before, the apparatus will be there. Its inheritance is the volume's subject.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai walks six decades in the space the book typically gives to a single study, and the compression has costs. I want to name where I am uncertain whether the compression has worked.

I am not sure I have been fair to Skinner. The 1958 paper is more methodologically careful than its reputation suggests, and my essai tries to credit this by noting that Skinner was working honestly within a theoretical framework that specified what his measurements should capture. But the essai's argument requires treating Skinner as the originator of an apparatus that has had specific consequences, and that framing places Skinner in a lineage whose subsequent uses he did not author and cannot be held responsible for. A reader sympathetic to Skinner as a historical figure might find the treatment compressed to the point of caricature. I have tried to avoid this specifically, but the compression is real.

I am not sure the Suppes and Anderson passages give those traditions their due. Suppes and Anderson were, like Skinner, serious researchers whose work transformed the fields they operated in. The essai treats them primarily as inheritors of Skinner's apparatus, which is one true thing but not the only true thing about their work. A reader who works specifically in the CAI or ITS traditions may find the framing too compressed. The alternative would be to extend the essai considerably, which would shift the balance away from the inheritance argument.

I am not sure about the treatment of Jiang et al. 2021. The study is methodologically careful within its terms, and my application of the apparatus surfaces specific features of the study's design that the paper itself is transparent about — the reading-and-listening restriction, the completion-threshold selection, the lack of delayed post-testing, the author affiliations. But a reader who finds the critique unbalanced could fairly ask whether I would apply the same apparatus as rigorously to a study whose findings I happened to find more palatable. I have tried to ask the same questions I would ask of any study in the tradition. Whether I have succeeded is partly for the reader to judge.

I am not sure about the overlap with Essai Nine. The book's ninth essai examines Duolingo specifically through the engagement-versus-learning distinction — what the platform's headline metrics (daily active users, streaks, time-on-app) do and do not index about language acquisition. This essai engages Duolingo through a different question: whether the measurement apparatus of the Jiang et al. 2021 study continues the inheritance from Skinner's teaching machine. The two essais cover different terrain, but they share a subject, and a reader coming to Duolingo fresh in this essai after having read Essai Nine may find the treatment partially redundant. I have tried to approach Jiang from the specific angle this essai requires — the measurement-apparatus inheritance — rather than from the angle the ninth essai handles. But the two essais engage the same platform from complementary directions, and the reader will need to hold both in mind.

Finally, I am not sure the essai's inheritance claim is sufficiently tentative. The argument that a measurement apparatus persists across six decades is a strong empirical claim, and I have made it while compressing six decades into a single essai. A specialist in any of the eras I discussed could identify developments and variations the essai has not captured. The apparatus has not been *completely* static; there have been local variations, partial adoptions of alternatives, contested territory within specific programs of research. What I am claiming is that the dominant pattern has been inheritance rather than revision, and that the pattern is visible in specific contemporary instances. A reader who finds this framing insufficiently nuanced for the complexity of the historical record has a fair objection.

The next essai — the fourth of this volume — examines what the Bloom 1984 2-sigma claim actually supports, under the apparatus this essai has just named. I hope you will continue with me.

## Notes on the draft

A few observations for the revision pass:

**Block structure.** The essai follows a historical-sweep-plus-close-reading structure specific to its function: Block A opens on Skinner's 1953 classroom observation and the 1958 *Science* paper, Block B extends to Suppes and PLATO, Block C examines Cognitive Tutor and the ITS tradition with its own honest acknowledgment of limits, Block D traces the adaptive-learning era (Knewton, DreamBox, i-Ready), Block E briefly covers the current AI-tutor wave, Block F is the extended close reading of Jiang et al. 2021, and Block G names the alternatives (Thorndike, Bjork, Bransford, Messick) that existed and were not adopted. The structure has been extended beyond the typical five-block form to accommodate the six-decade sweep.

**Length.** Draft runs approximately 6,800 words, projecting to 28-30 pages at trade-scholarly typesetting — above the 22-25 range the spec suggested. The six-decade inheritance argument required the extra length; without it, the argument becomes assertion rather than demonstration. A revision pass could compress particularly Block D (adaptive learning) and Block E (AI-tutor wave) since those are covered at length in other essais. But the Skinner/Suppes/Anderson/Jiang sections are load-bearing and compressing them would weaken the spine.

**Respect for Skinner.** The essai treats Skinner's 1958 paper as the more careful document it actually is, not as the caricature it is often reduced to. The measurement choices Skinner made are named as theoretical commitments rather than as errors. The apparatus's subsequent inheritance is what the essai critiques, not Skinner's own work. This respect-within-critique framing is discussed specifically in the closing uncertainties section.

**Respect for Anderson and Koedinger.** The Cognitive Tutor passage specifically credits Anderson et al.'s own 1995 acknowledgment of their system's transfer limits — that students display transfer to the degree they can map the tutor environment to the test environment. This is a direct quotation from the primary paper, and the essai uses it to show that the methodological transparency the 1995 paper displays is what makes the inheritance pattern visible. The subsequent citations of Cognitive Tutor's effect sizes that strip Anderson's caveats are what the essai critiques, not the 1995 paper itself.

**The Jiang et al. 2021 close reading.** This is Block F and is load-bearing for the essai's closing argument. The reading applies specific apparatus questions — outcome measure (STAMP 4S reading and listening, alignment with Duolingo's interface modalities), timescale (immediate post-test), selection (completion threshold, survivor bias), baseline (historical rather than contemporaneous), cost (absent), author affiliations (four of five Duolingo-employed). The questions are applied as the apparatus the earlier blocks surfaced — they are not a separate critique imported for Jiang specifically. This disciplinary continuity between the historical sweep and the contemporary case is what the essai's structure requires, and the Jiang section tries to model it explicitly.

**The Essai 9 overlap question.** I have been specific about this in the uncertainties section. Essai 9 examines Duolingo through the engagement-versus-learning distinction (headline metrics, "IDK IDK" observation structure, behavioral engagement proxying for cognitive engagement). Essai 3 examines Duolingo through the measurement-apparatus inheritance (what Jiang measures, what it does not, how the choices continue Skinner's pattern). The two essais share a platform but engage different questions. A reader who reads both essais will encounter Duolingo twice from two different angles, which is how the volume is designed to work — spiral returns that add analytical layers.

**The alternatives that did not take.** The seventh block names Thorndike 1906/1924 on transfer, Bjork 1992 on storage-versus-retrieval, Bransford et al. 1999 on deep understanding, and Messick 1989 on construct validity as methodological alternatives that have existed across the same six decades and have not been adopted as the field's standard apparatus. This block prepares the ground for Essai 13's synthesis on the measure-construct distinction and keeps the essai from reading as a pure indictment. The alternatives *existed*; the field *chose* the inherited apparatus rather than adopting them; the choice has consequences that are worth naming.

**What the draft does particularly well.**
- Anchors in a specific primary source (Skinner 1958) with direct attention to what that paper actually claimed and measured, rather than to its caricature.
- Traces the inheritance through specific studies in specific eras, not through general characterizations.

- Applies the apparatus to Jiang et al. 2021 as a live instance of the pattern, producing specific observations that the paper itself supports (the reading-and-listening restriction, the completion-threshold selection, the author affiliations).
