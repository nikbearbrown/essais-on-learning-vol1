# On Intelligent Tutoring Systems

*Essai 7*

---

In 2014, John Pane, Beth Ann Griffin, Daniel McCaffrey, and Rita Karam published a RAND Corporation report titled *Effectiveness of Cognitive Tutor Algebra I at Scale*. The study they conducted was, by the standards of educational-technology evaluation, ambitious in every dimension. It was a cluster-randomized controlled trial across 147 school sites in seven states, encompassing roughly 18,700 high school students and 6,800 middle school students, pairing schools into matched groups and randomly assigning each pair to use Cognitive Tutor Algebra I or to continue with their existing textbook-based instruction. The outcome measure was a standardized algebra proficiency exam administered at the end of the academic year. The comparison was not ITS against no instruction; it was ITS against the actual algebra instruction American ninth and eighth graders typically received in schools. The scale, the rigor, the geographic range, and the real-world setting made this, to this day, one of the most credible evaluations of any intelligent tutoring system that has ever been conducted.

The findings were specific, and I want to state them carefully because the rest of this essai depends on reading them correctly. In the first year of implementation, Cognitive Tutor produced no statistically significant effect on algebra proficiency, in either the high school sample or the middle school sample. In the second year, a statistically significant positive effect emerged in the high school sample — approximately 0.20 standard deviations, sufficient to move a median student from the 50th to roughly the 58th percentile on the end-of-year assessment. The middle school sample showed an effect of similar magnitude in the second year (around 0.13 to 0.15 sigma), but the effect did not reach statistical significance. Pane and colleagues described this as an "implementation learning curve" and were explicit in their framing: "Decisionmakers should be prepared for the possibility that CTAI will not produce positive effects immediately and should allow time for the full effects to emerge."

This is a nuanced result. It is not a clean success and it is not a clean failure. It is the kind of result that resists the binary narratives educational technology typically generates. Interestingly, the study also documented that the second-year improvement was not primarily attributable to individual teacher learning — students of teachers who were new to Cognitive Tutor in the second year performed similarly to students of teachers who had used it for both years. The learning, Pane and colleagues concluded, seemed to happen at the level of the school site — scheduling, technology infrastructure, teacher-administrator coordination, student expectations — rather than at the level of individual practitioner skill. The schools that figured out how to implement Cognitive Tutor took a year to figure it out, after which the system produced the kinds of effects its developers had hoped for.

I want to sit with this finding before the essai goes further. Pane's 2014 study is what careful, independent, well-resourced evaluation of an intelligent tutoring system looks like. It is what the field's best evidence is. Cognitive Tutor, in this study, produced a genuine but modest second-year effect at high schools, no significant effect at middle schools, and required substantial institutional learning to produce any effect at all. The cost of implementation — RAND's documentation estimated approximately $97 per student per year for Cognitive Tutor versus approximately $28 per student for traditional textbook instruction — fell on school budgets. These numbers are what the best evidence for ITS, read honestly, looks like.

The narrative the field has constructed around results like these — across Cognitive Tutor's thirty-year history, across the AutoTutor and ALEKS and SimStudent and related research programs, across the sustained body of ITS research from the 1980s forward — is of *disappointment*. Intelligent tutoring systems were supposed to approach the effectiveness of human tutoring. They did not. The gap between the promise of early ITS work and the subsequent evaluation record has been narrated, across both proponents and critics, as evidence that ITS has somehow failed to deliver.

This essai examines that narrative. I want to argue that the disappointment framing rests on a specific category error — a comparison between what ITS measured and a construct, *human tutoring*, that ITS was never fully on the same axis with. Read through the apparatus the second essai of this volume built, ITS measured what it was built to measure, with striking methodological discipline. What it did not measure, and what Pane's 0.20 sigma does not index, is what human tutors do that is not step-level procedural guidance. The disappointment narrative confuses these two things: the measurement of procedural mastery that ITS delivered, and the measurement of something else — something that lives in a different set of interactional moves — that the comparison benchmark required.

This is not a defense of ITS against critics. Some ITS critiques are correct. This is an argument about what kind of comparison the field has been making, and what that comparison has been invited to obscure.

---

Before going further, I want to be specific about what this essai is and is not doing.

The ITS research tradition — John Anderson, Kenneth Koedinger, Albert Corbett, Art Graesser, Kurt VanLehn, and a generation of their students and collaborators — produced some of the most careful, theoretically grounded, methodologically serious work in the history of educational technology. These are not EdTech entrepreneurs pitching inflated claims to investors. They are researchers who built cognitive theories before they built software, tested their software against their theories, published their methodologies openly, and acknowledged their systems' limitations in the primary literature when they existed. When Anderson, Corbett, Koedinger, and Pelletier published *Cognitive Tutors: Lessons Learned* in 1995, they were writing, in the plural of "lessons learned," the specific things their decade of work had taught them, including the limitations. Their 1995 paper contains, in its own words, the acknowledgment that tutors work better when they present themselves to students as nonhuman tools rather than as emulations of human tutors. The researchers knew what their systems were. The disappointment narrative came later, from the field's uses of their work, not from the researchers themselves.

This essai's critique is therefore not a critique of ITS researchers. It is a critique of the measurement apparatus their systems inherited and operated under, and of the rhetorical move that compared their systems to a construct (human tutoring) whose full character was never on the same measurement axis. The researchers deserve credit for building what they built. The apparatus around their work, which placed their systems in comparison with human tutoring via summary sigma numbers, is what this essai examines.

---

Begin by examining what Cognitive Tutor was designed to do.

Cognitive Tutor is an embodiment of the ACT-R theory of cognition developed by John Anderson at Carnegie Mellon University. ACT-R posits that cognitive skill is acquired through the conversion of declarative knowledge (facts, concepts) into procedural knowledge (production rules, condition-action pairs). A production rule is specifically a statement of the form: *if the goal is X and the current state is Y, then take action Z*. Under ACT-R, becoming skilled at algebra means acquiring an increasingly sophisticated set of production rules for algebraic manipulation — recognizing that when the goal is to isolate a variable and the current state shows an equation with a coefficient, the next action is to divide both sides by that coefficient.

The instructional design principle this entails is specific: *instruction should be designed with reference to a cognitive model of the competence the student is being asked to learn*. If you can specify the production rules that constitute the target competence, you can instrument instruction to track whether each rule has been acquired. Cognitive Tutor does exactly this. Its engineering rests on a computational model of the problem-solving process the student is expected to develop. When a student works through an algebra problem, the tutor compares each step the student takes against its model of valid solution paths and responds accordingly.

The technology that results is built around two specific operations, both of which are worth naming precisely.

The first is *model tracing*. As the student works, the tutor monitors each individual step — each equation manipulation, each choice of operation — and matches that step against its internal representation of the production rules that constitute correct problem-solving. If the student takes a step that matches a valid production, the tutor proceeds. If the student takes a step that does not — or that matches one of the tutor's stored "buggy" productions representing common misconceptions — the tutor responds with immediate feedback, typically a short error message identifying what went wrong. If the student requests help, the tutor offers a sequence of hints calibrated to the specific production the student is currently struggling to fire. This is step-level instrumentation. The tutor knows, in real time, what the student did at every point in a problem, and responds to what the student did specifically.

The second is *knowledge tracing*. Across many problems, the tutor maintains a running estimate of whether the student has acquired each of the production rules underlying the domain. These estimates use a Bayesian framework, updating as the student succeeds or fails with items that exercise each rule. The tutor will not allow a student to advance to new material until its knowledge-tracing estimates cross a threshold indicating mastery of the current material. This is the *mastery learning* principle as applied inside a computational system: students do not move forward until the system's measurement apparatus says they are ready to.

Together, model tracing and knowledge tracing constitute the measurement apparatus of Cognitive Tutor. What the apparatus measures is specific: step-level correctness, time per step, requests for hints and the specific hint levels utilized, error patterns and their correspondence to stored misconceptions, and estimated mastery of each production rule in the domain. These are not arbitrary choices. They are the measurements ACT-R theory specifies as relevant to procedural skill acquisition. The design is internally consistent with the theory the system was built on.

What the apparatus does not measure is also specific, and the 1995 paper — along with later ITS literature — is forthright about the boundaries. Cognitive Tutor does not measure the student's affective state. It does not model whether the student is frustrated, bored, motivated, or emotionally disengaged. It does not measure conceptual confusion at levels above the production-rule grain (a student may execute productions correctly while failing to understand the domain at a deeper conceptual level, and the tutor cannot detect this). It does not measure transfer to unlike problems, to non-mathematical contexts, or to new domains. It does not measure the durability of learning beyond the unit or the semester. It does not measure the student's motivation for pursuing algebra, or their relationship to mathematics as a field, or the meaning they derive from mastering it. None of these dimensions are in the apparatus. The tutor was not built to measure them. The researchers who built it did not claim it measured them.

When Pane's RAND study evaluated Cognitive Tutor, the outcome measure — the standardized algebra proficiency exam — was aligned with the construct Cognitive Tutor was designed to build. The test measured procedural algebra competence, with items that could reasonably be decomposed into production rules the tutor had been designed to strengthen. The 0.20 sigma second-year effect reflects what Cognitive Tutor actually does: when implemented at high schools, over two years, with teachers who have figured out how to integrate it into their instruction, the system produces measurable gains on procedural algebra assessment. This is a real effect. It is what the tutor was built to produce.

---

Now consider what the ITS research tradition is typically compared against.

The comparison — explicit in VanLehn 2011 and throughout the meta-analytic literature, implicit in nearly every discussion of whether AI tutors have "achieved human-tutor effectiveness" — is with human one-to-one tutoring. The most frequently cited benchmark is Bloom's 2-sigma finding from 1984, which Essai 4 of this volume examined at length. But the comparison point is not only Bloom. Subsequent meta-analyses have found that expert human tutors, measured across many studies, produce effects in the range of roughly 0.4 to 0.8 sigma relative to classroom instruction. This is the number ITS is typically compared against when the field asks whether ITS has "matched" or "approached" human tutoring.

What, precisely, are these human-tutoring effect sizes measuring?

This is where the essai's argument lives. The human-tutoring construct, as characterized in the research literature on what expert human tutors actually do, involves a set of interactional moves that the ITS measurement apparatus does not index and was not built to index. These moves have been documented across several decades of careful research — much of it, interestingly, by researchers who also built ITS and who studied human tutoring in order to inform their systems' design. The researchers are not unaware of what they were not measuring. They were explicit about it. The field that inherited their work often was not.

Art Graesser, whose research program included the AutoTutor ITS and who therefore has particular credibility on both sides of this comparison, spent years analyzing videotaped tutoring sessions between expert human tutors and students. The goal was to characterize what expert tutors did, so that AutoTutor could be designed to do some of it. What Graesser's analyses documented was a specific set of interactional moves tutors routinely performed:

*Expectation and Misconception Tailored dialogue*, in which the tutor approaches a topic with a specific set of "expectations" — the components of a complete correct understanding — and a specific set of "misconceptions" — the common wrong ways students tend to think about the topic. The tutor probes the student's articulation, notes what components of the expectation are present and absent, identifies which misconceptions the student is exhibiting, and dynamically constructs the next move to address what the student specifically needs. This is substantially more flexible than matching a step against a stored production rule. It works with free-form student utterances, evaluates them against conceptual expectations rather than syntactic correctness, and adjusts the tutor's response based on what the utterance revealed.

*Comprehension checks*, in which the tutor continuously probes whether the student has understood what was just established. "Do you see why that works?" "Can you put that in your own words?" "What would happen if we changed this part?" These are not assessment items in the ITS sense; they are real questions that real tutors ask, whose answers real tutors then use to calibrate what to do next. The tutor is monitoring the student's understanding in real time, not as an external measurement but as part of an interaction whose purpose is the joint construction of understanding.

*Politeness and face management*, in which the tutor manages the student's emotional state and relationship to the material. Graesser documented that human tutors are often strategically imprecise about negative feedback, delivering it indirectly or softening it to protect the student's willingness to continue engaging. This is not a pedagogical flaw; it is a feature of interactions designed to sustain rather than damage the student's relationship to learning.

*Handling student-initiated questions*, in which the student asks something the tutor had not planned to address. Human tutors engage with these questions, sometimes following the student's interest to places the tutor had not intended to go. AutoTutor, Graesser noted with characteristic directness, has to use "diversionary tactics" to stay on its agenda when students ask questions it was not designed to handle.

Michelene Chi, whose ICAP framework distinguishes Interactive, Constructive, Active, and Passive forms of engagement, has made a related argument from a different angle. Chi documents that what makes human tutoring effective is not primarily the information the tutor delivers; it is the *interactivity* of the exchange, in which the tutor's questions and prompts elicit the student's own elaboration and self-explanation, and the student's attempts at articulation reveal gaps and misconceptions that the tutor then addresses through further interaction. Chi's research program has repeatedly shown that student self-explanation is a primary driver of conceptual change — and that expert tutors are specifically skilled at eliciting the right kinds of self-explanation from students through well-calibrated prompts and questions. An ITS can prompt for self-explanation — some systems do — but the prompt-to-prompt flexibility of a human tutor reading a student's partial answer and responding to that specific answer's specific weaknesses is not what production-rule-based systems do.

Wood, Bruner, and Ross, in a foundational 1976 paper, identified six functions that a tutor performs when scaffolding a learner through a task: recruitment of the learner's interest, reduction of degrees of freedom (simplifying the task), direction maintenance (keeping the learner focused on the goal), marking critical features (drawing attention to what matters), frustration control (managing the emotional risk of error), and demonstration (modeling the task for the learner). Of these six functions, Cognitive Tutor is specifically designed to perform the second — reduction of degrees of freedom — through its step-level scaffolding and hint system. The tutor makes the task more tractable by breaking it into smaller operations and providing graduated hints as needed. What the tutor is structurally blind to is recruitment (it cannot engage the student's interest), frustration control (it cannot read or respond to the emotional register of the interaction), and — in any rich sense — demonstration (it cannot stylize the solution in ways that make the expert move visible to the novice).

---

Here is the structural observation the essai wants to crystallize.

The ITS measurement apparatus, exemplified by Cognitive Tutor, was built to measure and support one specific dimension of what expert human tutors do: the reduction-of-degrees-of-freedom move in the Wood-Bruner-Ross framework, the step-level procedural scaffolding that constitutes one piece of what happens in a good tutoring interaction. Cognitive Tutor performs this piece with remarkable precision. Its production-rule model, its model tracing, its knowledge tracing, and its mastery-learning constraints are all optimized for ensuring that students acquire the procedural rules that constitute a specific domain of competence. When evaluated on measures aligned with this construct — standardized algebra assessments, for example — the system produces real effects. Pane's 0.20 sigma is real. It reflects what the system actually does.

Human tutoring, as documented in Graesser's research and Chi's and the broader line of work on actual tutoring interactions, involves that same move — reduction of degrees of freedom — alongside other moves: expectation-and-misconception dialogue, comprehension checks, politeness and face management, student-question handling, recruitment, frustration control, and demonstration. The interactional richness of what an expert tutor does is not a marketing claim or a sentimental projection; it is a documented empirical feature of the tutoring relationship, identified by researchers who studied it carefully in order to understand it. The effect sizes produced by expert human tutors in the research literature reflect this fuller set of moves operating together. The sigma number from a human-tutoring study measures the output of all these moves acting in concert, against whatever outcome measure the study used.

When these two numbers — the ITS effect size and the human-tutoring effect size — are placed on a single axis for comparison, the implicit claim is that they measure the same construct at different magnitudes. They do not. They measure overlapping but distinct constructs. ITS measures what a specific procedural-scaffolding technology produces on assessments that test procedural skills. Human tutoring measures what a full interactional relationship produces on assessments that, depending on the study, test some combination of procedural skills and broader constructs. The two numbers can be placed on the same axis only if we agree that the underlying outcome measures are the same — which in the research literature they often are not — and that the interactional moves the two interventions involve are comparable — which in the research literature they clearly are not.

This is the construct mismatch the essai points at. The ITS versus human-tutoring comparison has been doing field-level work for forty years. It has animated research funding, guided institutional adoption decisions, and underpinned the contemporary rhetoric that AI can "approach" the effectiveness of human instruction. What the comparison has obscured, from the beginning, is that the two things it is comparing were never fully on the same axis. ITS does, with real discipline, the procedural-scaffolding move. Human tutoring does that move plus a half-dozen others that ITS was not built to do and could not do with the measurement apparatus available to it. The "disappointment" in ITS's failure to match human-tutor effect sizes is partly the disappointment of a comparison that was structurally underdetermined from the start.

---

I want to name one further observation before closing this essai, because it has implications that reach into the current AI-tutor moment the next several essais will examine.

The contemporary wave of AI tutors — Khanmigo, the Kestin Harvard physics tutor, Eedi's Google-Research-collaboration system, Rori — has been presented, in much current discourse, as an advance on the ITS tradition. The framing is that generative AI, particularly large language models, provides the interactional flexibility that ITS lacked. These systems can engage in natural-language dialogue. They can handle student questions they were not specifically designed to handle. They can — in principle — perform some of the interactional moves Graesser documented as characteristic of expert human tutoring. The rhetoric suggests that the construct-mismatch this essai has described is, for these new systems, finally being addressed.

Read through the ITS apparatus, the claim is more complicated than the rhetoric suggests. The current AI-tutor evaluation studies — which the next essais will examine in detail — still measure what earlier ITS evaluations measured: item-level mastery, step-level performance, post-test scores on aligned assessments, immediate short-term outcomes rather than durable long-term learning. The measurement apparatus has been inherited. What has changed is the interaction layer, not the measurement layer. Whether the interaction-layer changes produce meaningfully different learning outcomes, or whether they produce the appearance of more-human interaction without producing the underlying effects, is an empirical question that the current literature has not cleanly answered.

What I want to highlight, as this essai closes, is that the construct-mismatch the ITS tradition reveals is not a solved problem. It is the structural feature of a field that has consistently measured what is easier to measure — procedural performance, step-level correctness, item-level mastery — and has consistently compared those measurements against benchmarks whose fuller character lives in dimensions the measurements do not index. Cognitive Tutor did this honestly, within a clear theoretical framework, and produced real effects of modest magnitude when evaluated appropriately. Contemporary AI tutors that invoke Cognitive Tutor's lineage as evidence of their own effectiveness are inheriting the measurement apparatus without having solved the construct-mismatch the apparatus rests on. The 0.20 sigma and the 0.76 sigma and the 0.73 sigma and every other sigma in this literature are reports on what specific measurement operations have produced. They are not reports on whether interactional richness approaching human tutoring has been achieved, because the measurements do not index interactional richness.

---

Here is what I would like you to carry out of this essai.

When you next encounter a claim that an educational technology has "approached the effectiveness of human tutoring," ask yourself three questions.

The first: what did the technology actually measure? If the evaluation used item-level or step-level assessments aligned with the technology's instructional content, the system has been measured against a construct aligned with what it was built to do. This is not a criticism of the evaluation; it is a description of what the evaluation supports.

The second: what does the human-tutoring construct actually involve? The research literature on expert human tutoring documents a specific set of interactional moves: expectation-and-misconception dialogue, comprehension checks, politeness management, student-question handling, recruitment, frustration control, demonstration. These moves are not peripheral features; they are the substance of what expert tutors do. A technology that does not perform these moves, or that performs a much thinner version of them, is not doing the thing the human-tutoring construct names.

The third: was the comparison between the technology and human tutoring conducted on an axis that indexes both? If the outcome measure is an aligned test that favors procedural scaffolding (which most ITS and AI-tutor evaluations use), the axis is not measuring what human tutoring does beyond procedural scaffolding. The comparison is structurally limited by the measurement choice. A finding that the technology "approaches" human tutoring on such a measure is a finding about procedural scaffolding, not about the interactional richness the construct *human tutoring* would require to be compared against.

These three questions give you a way to read any human-versus-machine comparison in the learning-systems field. They do not answer the question *can AI replace human tutors*; they answer the prior question *what are we measuring when we make the comparison*. The prior question is the one the field has skipped, and it is the one this essai has been trying to hold the reader's attention on.

The next essai examines a specific subset of the current AI-tutor moment — the wave of adaptive-learning and engagement-focused platforms that now invoke this same comparison apparatus to position themselves as solutions to the problems ITS did not solve. What has changed, what has persisted, and what the apparatus reveals about both is what comes next.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai has tried to respect the ITS research tradition while criticizing the comparison apparatus that has been used to frame its legacy. That balance is delicate, and I want to name specifically where I am unsure whether the balance has held.

I am not sure I have given Anderson, Koedinger, and the Cognitive Tutor researchers full credit for the care with which they built what they built. The 1995 *Cognitive Tutors: Lessons Learned* paper is a model of methodological transparency. Its authors were explicit about what their systems could and could not do. They acknowledged the structural limitations the essai re-examines. They built their systems in conscious conversation with what they knew about human tutoring, and they did not, in their primary research writing, claim that their systems were equivalents to human tutors. The disappointment narrative that developed around ITS came partly from outside the research community, from policy and philanthropic and journalistic framings that expected more than the researchers themselves had claimed. A reader who thinks my treatment of Anderson and colleagues is too compressed — that the full story of their methodological care deserves more pages than I have given — has a fair case to make.

I am not sure I have avoided the easy move of treating human tutoring as sentimentally richer than machine tutoring. The essai tries to ground the construct-mismatch argument in specific empirical research on what expert human tutors actually do. But the move from "here are documented tutoring behaviors" to "therefore ITS cannot be compared on a single axis with human tutoring" carries rhetorical weight that could slide into "humans are irreplaceable." This is not what the essai is arguing. The argument is that the *measurement apparatus* has compared the two things on a single sigma axis in a way that does not support the comparison. Whether a future technology could actually perform the interactional moves the apparatus does not currently index is an open empirical question. The essai does not answer it. A reader who finds the closing section drifting toward a more confident human-irreplaceability claim than the argument supports should tell me.

I am not sure the Graesser section gives AutoTutor its due. Graesser built AutoTutor specifically to do some of the things this essai argues ITS generally does not do. The dialogue-based AutoTutor architecture was an attempt to move beyond production-rule-based step tracking and into natural-language interaction with something like expectation-and-misconception dialogue. Whether AutoTutor succeeded at this — and my sense, from the effect-size literature, is that it succeeded partially, with effect sizes in the 0.3 to 0.8 range that modestly exceed step-based ITS — is a nuance the essai compresses. A reader interested specifically in the dialogue-based ITS tradition may find my treatment too brief.

Finally, I am not sure the transition to the contemporary-AI paragraph at the essai's close is the right move. The essai's primary argument is about the ITS tradition and the construct-mismatch comparison. The closing move extends that argument to current AI tutors, preparing for the essais that follow. But extending the argument requires claiming something about how current systems inherit the ITS measurement apparatus, and that claim is partly speculation until the next essais examine it with specificity. If the closing move overreaches — if it treats as settled what the rest of the book still needs to establish — that overreach would be worth correcting in a revision pass.

The next essai examines adaptive learning's particular flavor of the same construct-mismatch problem. I hope you will continue with me.
