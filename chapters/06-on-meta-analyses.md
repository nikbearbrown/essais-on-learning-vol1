# On Meta-analyses and What They Conceal

*Essai 6*

---

In 2011, Kurt VanLehn published a paper in *Educational Psychologist* titled *The Relative Effectiveness of Human Tutoring, Intelligent Tutoring Systems, and Other Tutoring Systems*. The paper ran twenty-four pages. It aggregated the findings of fifty-four specific comparisons drawn from twenty-eight evaluation studies, published between 1975 and 2010, of tutoring interventions of various kinds. VanLehn's inclusion criteria were strict: only experimental studies with individual-based assignment, requiring equivalent instructional duration across conditions, with sufficient statistical reporting to compute effect sizes, published in English in peer-reviewed venues. The analysis compared five categories of instruction: no tutoring, answer-based computer-aided instruction, step-based intelligent tutoring systems, substep-based tutoring systems, and human tutoring.

The paper's headline findings have become, in the fourteen years since its publication, among the most-cited numbers in the AI-tutoring advocacy literature. Human tutoring produced a median effect of 0.79 sigma across ten comparisons. Intelligent tutoring systems — the step-based variety that Cognitive Tutor and similar systems exemplify — produced a median effect of 0.76 sigma across twenty-eight comparisons. The two numbers are nearly indistinguishable. Together they have been cited as establishing that intelligent tutoring systems can approach the effectiveness of human one-to-one instruction.

I want to be clear about what this essai is and is not doing. It is not an attack on VanLehn. His 2011 paper is a methodologically careful piece of work. VanLehn himself was transparent about his inclusion criteria, about the limitations of his aggregation, and about the conditions under which his findings apply. He noted specifically, for example, that when one outlier study was removed from the human-tutoring sample, that median dropped from 0.79 to 0.68. He noted that the ITS effect size of 0.76 was produced within systems' "limited area of expertise" — well-defined problem-solving in STEM domains, primarily. He framed his own findings as a "progress report," not a final verdict, and explicitly recommended that ITS be used as "learning supplements" rather than as replacements for classroom instruction. His caveats are in the paper for readers who want them. What has happened to the two headline numbers in the fourteen years since is not VanLehn's responsibility.

The essai's project is to examine what meta-analysis as a genre does — what it aggregates, what it smooths over, what it produces as a summary number, and what relationship that summary number has to the underlying studies it was computed from. VanLehn 2011 is the specimen. The essai reads VanLehn carefully as an instance of a genre, and asks what kind of evidence a meta-analytic summary sigma actually is, and what kind of evidence it is often cited as being.

I want to ask you to hold two numbers in mind as the essai proceeds. VanLehn's human tutoring figure: 0.79. His step-based ITS figure: 0.76. They travel as though they were claims about the world. They are, more precisely, claims about a specific aggregation of fifty-four comparisons drawn from twenty-eight studies selected under specific inclusion criteria. The relationship between those two descriptions is the subject of this essai.

---

Begin by looking at what VanLehn actually aggregated. The twenty-eight studies he included spanned thirty-five years of research, multiple subject domains, a wide range of populations, and substantially different outcome measures. The heterogeneity is not a methodological error on VanLehn's part; it is what the literature he was summarizing actually looked like. But the implications of that heterogeneity for the meaning of the summary number are worth walking through carefully.

Consider the duration of the interventions. At one end of VanLehn's dataset are studies like Valerie Shute and Robert Glaser's 1990 evaluation of the Smithtown system, a discovery-based environment for teaching microeconomic inquiry. The intervention lasted approximately five hours. Students engaged with the system for less than a single school day. The outcome measure was a researcher-designed test of inquiry behaviors and economic concepts, administered shortly after the intervention concluded. At the other end of the dataset are studies like Joanne Anania's 1981 dissertation, conducted under Benjamin Bloom at the University of Chicago, which ran for an entire academic term and measured achievement on final achievement tests in probability and cartography.

Both studies contributed effect sizes to VanLehn's meta-analysis. Both are legitimate research. But they are measuring fundamentally different things. A five-hour intervention on inquiry behaviors cannot produce evidence about long-term retention of academic content. An academic-term intervention on probability cannot produce evidence about short-term skill acquisition during a laboratory session. The two findings are not interchangeable reports on "tutoring effectiveness" — they are reports on different interventions, over different timescales, measuring different constructs, in different populations.

When VanLehn's aggregation computes a median across all the studies in a category, the median treats these as if they were samples from a common distribution. In a narrow statistical sense, they are. In the sense that matters for what the summary number tells us about whether tutoring "works," they are not. The median smooths over the difference between a five-hour discovery learning intervention and a term-long mastery learning curriculum. Both contribute one effect size each to the calculation. The weighted average of heterogeneous effects is what produces the headline number, but the heterogeneity is what makes the headline number hard to interpret in any specific context.

Consider next the outcome measures. At one end of VanLehn's dataset are studies by John Anderson and colleagues on early Cognitive Tutor variants — the LISP Tutor and the Algebra Tutor. These studies used developer-designed assessments that were, in the authors' own methodological papers, tightly aligned with the specific problem-solving steps the tutors scaffolded. Students who worked through the tutor were tested on the kinds of problems the tutor had prepared them to solve. Effect sizes in these studies ran around 1.0 sigma. At the other end are studies like the Mathematica Policy Research evaluation conducted by Larri Campuzano and colleagues in 2009, responding to the No Child Left Behind mandate. This evaluation covered ten commercial software products across thousands of students in thirty-three school districts over one to two school years, with outcomes measured on standardized state tests. Effect sizes across this body of work were, on average, statistically indistinguishable from zero.

The 1.0 and the 0.0 are both real numbers. Both come from careful research. Both are evidence of something. What they are evidence of, however, is different. The Anderson-style effect is evidence of what a well-designed tutor can produce when assessed on items aligned with what it teaches. The Campuzano-style effect is evidence of what a variety of deployed commercial software produces when assessed on broad standardized measures across typical implementation conditions. Neither reading is wrong. They are readings of different things.

When VanLehn's aggregation computes a summary ITS effect of 0.76 sigma, that number averages across this range of outcome measurement. The number is neither the Anderson 1.0 nor the Campuzano 0.0 but something in between — a weighted average of findings produced under radically different assessment regimes. A reader who encounters "0.76 sigma for intelligent tutoring systems" and does not know that the underlying literature includes both 1.0s and 0.0s depending on the nature of the assessment has been given a number whose conditions are invisible. This is not a flaw in VanLehn's analysis; he was transparent about the range. It is a feature of the genre: summary numbers travel independently of the conditions that produced them.

Consider also the populations. VanLehn's inclusion criteria spanned K-12, higher education, and adult learners. Studies of college students solving physics problems in laboratory conditions contribute effect sizes alongside studies of fourth-graders learning probability in classrooms. The populations are different in every way that matters for how interventions produce their effects — cognitive maturity, prior knowledge, motivation, classroom context, the role the intervention plays in the student's broader educational life. Aggregating across these populations is defensible if you believe that "tutoring effectiveness" is a property of the intervention that generalizes across populations. If you do not believe that — if you think interventions produce different effects with different learners, which a substantial research literature on aptitude-treatment interaction has argued for half a century — then the aggregation is smoothing over differences that matter for whether the summary number applies to any given situation.

The point of walking through this is not to claim that VanLehn should have excluded some of these studies. Meta-analysts have to aggregate across the literature that exists. VanLehn's inclusion criteria were defensible. The point is to notice what the summary number is the summary of. The 0.76 for ITS is a weighted average of fifty-four comparisons drawn from research that varied in duration by three orders of magnitude, in outcome measurement from perfectly aligned to maximally distal, and in population from adult learners to elementary schoolchildren. The 0.76 is not a claim about what ITS will do in any specific setting. It is a claim about the central tendency of what ITS produced in the research literature as aggregated by VanLehn's inclusion criteria. Those are different claims, and the difference is the essai's first observation.

---

This brings us to a structural feature of meta-analysis as a genre that is easy to miss: summary numbers are artifacts of inclusion decisions, not neutral measurements of "what the evidence shows."

Consider what happens when other meta-analysts have addressed overlapping literatures using different inclusion criteria. In 2013, Saiying Steenbergen-Hu and Harris Cooper published a meta-analysis of intelligent tutoring systems in K-12 mathematics. Their summary effect size was 0.05 to 0.09 sigma — essentially zero. Their conclusion was that ITS had no meaningful effect on performance at the K-12 level. In 2014, the same authors published a follow-up analysis of ITS effectiveness with college students, finding effects of 0.32 to 0.37 sigma. In 2016, James Kulik and J.D. Fletcher conducted a meta-analysis of fifty controlled evaluations of ITS. They reported an overall summary of 0.66 sigma, with a notable moderator: 0.73 for studies using developer-designed assessments and only 0.13 for studies using standardized tests. In 2020, Andre Nickow, Philip Oreopoulos, and Vincent Quan published what remains the most systematic meta-analysis of randomized tutoring evaluations, reporting a pooled effect of 0.37 sigma — less than half of VanLehn's human tutoring figure.

All of these meta-analyses examined substantially overlapping literatures. All used defensible inclusion criteria. All reported different summary numbers. VanLehn's 0.79 for human tutoring, Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan's 0.37, Kulik-Fletcher's 0.66 for ITS, Steenbergen-Hu and Cooper's 0.05 to 0.09 for K-12 ITS, Kulik-Fletcher's 0.73 versus 0.13 split on outcome measure — these are all answers to roughly the same research question, and they are very different numbers.

The differences are not errors. They are consequences of different inclusion decisions. VanLehn required individual-level assignment, which excluded cluster-randomized trials. Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan included cluster-randomized RCTs and a broader range of tutoring types, including paraprofessional and volunteer tutors. Steenbergen-Hu and Cooper included answer-based CAI systems that Kulik and Fletcher treated as methodologically distinct from step-based ITS, and Kulik and Fletcher explicitly critiqued Steenbergen-Hu for precisely this decision. The intra-genre debate — *one meta-analyst's thoroughness is another's lack of rigor* — is ongoing and unresolvable. Each analyst is making principled choices about what should count. The choices produce different summary numbers.

This is the second observation the essai wants to surface. A meta-analytic summary sigma is not a measurement of the truth of its underlying literature; it is a measurement of what a specific aggregation of that literature produces under specific inclusion rules. Change the rules, change the number. The fact that careful meta-analysts disagree about the right rules is not a problem to be resolved before meta-analysis can be trusted — it is a structural feature of the genre. Summary numbers are interpretive artifacts, not neutral summaries. When a reader encounters "meta-analyses show tutoring produces effects of 0.79 sigma," they are encountering one meta-analyst's interpretive choices, not a fact about the world. A reader who encounters "meta-analyses show tutoring produces effects of 0.37 sigma" is encountering a different meta-analyst's interpretive choices, also not a fact about the world. Both are legitimate summaries. Neither is the summary.

---

Now consider what the underlying literature itself is made of.

A meta-analysis aggregates published studies. Studies with null or negative findings are systematically underrepresented in the published literature, a phenomenon that Robert Rosenthal named the *file drawer problem* in a 1979 paper that remains foundational in meta-analytic methodology. The logic is straightforward: journals preferentially publish studies with statistically significant findings; researchers preferentially submit studies with significant findings; the cumulative effect is that the published literature systematically overrepresents positive effects relative to what was actually conducted. Rosenthal did not stop at naming the problem. He proposed a specific statistic for interrogating it — the Fail-Safe N — which answers a concrete question: how many unpublished studies averaging a null result would have to exist, sitting in researchers' file drawers unseen, to neutralize the statistical significance of the published meta-analytic finding?

The formula is worth putting on the page, because it makes the critique concrete. For a meta-analysis of $k$ studies with a mean standardized score of $\bar{Z}$, the Fail-Safe N is given by:

$$k_0 = \frac{k(\bar{Z}^2 - 1)}{Z_\alpha^2 - \bar{Z}^2}$$

where $Z_\alpha$ is the critical value of the standard normal distribution at the chosen significance level. Rosenthal offered a "rough and ready" threshold for interpreting the result: a meta-analytic finding is reasonably resistant to the file-drawer threat if the Fail-Safe N exceeds $5k + 10$. If your meta-analysis aggregates twenty studies and the Fail-Safe N is three hundred, the published finding is robust — three hundred unseen null studies would have to be sitting in file drawers to overturn it, and this is implausibly many. If your meta-analysis aggregates twenty studies and the Fail-Safe N is fifty, the finding is fragile — fifty unseen null studies is entirely plausible, and the apparent significance of the finding evaporates if they exist.

This statistic is elegant, easy to compute, and rarely reported. I have not been able to locate a Fail-Safe N for VanLehn 2011, or for Kulik and Fletcher 2016, or for Steenbergen-Hu and Cooper, or for most of the other meta-analyses that have shaped contemporary AI-tutor advocacy. The statistic exists. Rosenthal has made it available since 1979. The education-research literature has, by and large, declined to use it. A reader who wants to know how robust a meta-analytic finding is to the file-drawer threat typically has to compute the statistic themselves from what the paper reports — which most readers will not do.

This would matter less if the underlying literature had correction mechanisms for publication bias. Some fields have those mechanisms: registered reports, pre-registration, null-result journals, replication initiatives. Education research has substantially weaker versions of these than, say, clinical medicine. A 2020 survey by Wolf and Frankenbach of recent meta-analyses in flagship education journals found that they "usually neglect publication bias adjustment." When such adjustments are applied, corrected effect sizes tend to be markedly smaller than the uncorrected summary numbers.

There is a second and more specific form of selection bias that applies to educational technology research particularly, and that Wolf and colleagues documented in a 2020 paper. Effect sizes in studies conducted by program developers — by the researchers and organizations who built the intervention being evaluated — are, on average, approximately eighty percent larger than effect sizes reported by independent evaluators of the same class of interventions. The number is worth sitting with. Interventions evaluated by their creators produce effects almost twice as large as the same interventions evaluated by researchers who did not create them. Wolf and colleagues estimated that approximately sixty-six percent of this gap is attributable to publication bias — that is, to selective reporting of positive outcomes by researchers with a vested interest in the intervention's apparent success. The remainder is attributable to other factors: researchers with more experience on a given intervention tend to implement it more faithfully; developer-evaluators may have privileged knowledge of what outcomes the intervention was built to move; and so on.

What this means for meta-analytic summary numbers is specific and important. Any meta-analysis that aggregates studies without distinguishing developer-conducted from independent evaluations is producing a summary that is partially an artifact of who conducted the included studies. The research literature on intelligent tutoring systems, throughout the period VanLehn aggregated, was heavily populated by developer-conducted evaluations. The LISP Tutor was evaluated primarily by Anderson and colleagues, who built it. The Cognitive Tutor line was evaluated primarily by Koedinger and colleagues, who built it. Early AutoTutor research was conducted primarily by Graesser and colleagues, who built it. This is not a scandal — it is how early-stage intervention research often works, because the developers are the people who understand the intervention well enough to evaluate it. But it means that summary effect sizes from this era disproportionately reflect the evaluations of researchers whose careers were invested in the interventions they were studying.

This is a structural feature of the literature meta-analyses aggregate from. Publication bias and evaluator-conflict bias are not errors in the meta-analysis; they are features of the underlying studies that meta-analysis, as a genre, tends to smooth over rather than correct. A summary number produced from a literature that systematically overrepresents positive findings will itself be biased upward. Subsequent independent evaluations — the kind that meta-analyses done a decade later can include — tend to find smaller effects than the original developer-conducted research. The pattern across the ITS literature fits this expectation: effect sizes tend to shrink as the research matures and independent replications accumulate.

---

There is a further problem with meta-analytic aggregation that is worth naming explicitly: the summary number can conceal heterogeneity that matters far more than the central tendency.

Methodologists have developed statistics for this. The I² statistic reports the percentage of variation across included studies that is attributable to heterogeneity rather than to sampling error. Larry Hedges and others have argued that meta-analyses should routinely report not just the summary effect but also a prediction interval — the range of true effects expected in a new study drawn from the same population as the included studies. The distinction matters enormously. A meta-analysis that reports a summary effect of 0.50 sigma with a prediction interval from 0.40 to 0.60 is reporting a consistent effect across the literature. A meta-analysis that reports the same summary effect of 0.50 sigma with a prediction interval from 0.10 to 0.90 is reporting a literature in which effects are extremely variable and the average is nearly uninformative about what any new study will find.

The vast majority of published meta-analyses in education research do not report prediction intervals. When such intervals have been computed post-hoc for prominent meta-analyses, they often turn out to be quite wide — suggesting that the apparent precision of summary effect sizes in this literature is partially illusory. A number like "0.76 for step-based ITS" is typically reported without the wide prediction interval that honest reporting would require. The number looks like a measurement. It functions more like an average across a distribution of measurements that themselves vary substantially.

John Ioannidis has made a related critique at the level of meta-analysis as a methodological practice generally. His argument is that the underlying studies in many research fields are themselves poorly designed, underpowered, and susceptible to researcher degrees of freedom in ways that make them individually unreliable. Aggregating unreliable studies does not produce reliable aggregates. In his 2005 paper *Why Most Published Research Findings Are False*, Ioannidis argued that across a wide range of research domains, the majority of findings are likely to be false positives. Subsequent work has documented that in psychology — a field closely related to learning-sciences research — a substantial fraction of published findings do not replicate when independent replication is attempted. A 2018 analysis by Nelson and colleagues found that in up to one-fifth of psychological meta-analyses, half of the included studies were not expected to replicate.

The implication for meta-analysis of education research is not comforting. If the individual studies being aggregated are unreliable, the meta-analysis produces a summary that is precisely estimated but not accurately estimated. The error bars around the summary look tight. The relationship between the summary and the truth is loose. This is what methodologists mean when they say that meta-analysis can produce precision without accuracy — the summary number has a narrow confidence interval, but the interval is narrow around the wrong value.

---

Now consider what happens when meta-analytic summary numbers leave their original technical contexts and enter contemporary discourse.

In his 2024 book *Brave New Words*, Sal Khan invokes VanLehn's findings to justify the trajectory from human-led to AI-driven education. The book's reading of VanLehn is that intelligent tutoring systems have been demonstrated to approach the effectiveness of human tutoring. The book cites the near-identity of the 0.76 and 0.79 figures as evidence for this claim. The specific invocations rarely mention VanLehn's caveats: that his ITS effect size was produced within systems' "limited area of expertise"; that his recommendation was that ITS be used as learning supplements rather than replacements; that removing a single outlier study dropped the human tutoring figure from 0.79 to 0.68; that subsequent meta-analyses on overlapping literatures have produced substantially different summary numbers.

Khan's invocation is not unusual. Across the contemporary AI-tutor advocacy literature, VanLehn's 0.76 has become a near-canonical reference point, cited as establishing that AI can match human tutoring. Gates Foundation program materials invoke meta-analytic effect sizes to orient "high-dosage tutoring" investment. Chan Zuckerberg Initiative materials invoke VanLehn-shaped findings to frame ITS features as "evidence-based." Academic papers in the AI-in-education tradition routinely cite VanLehn as shorthand for the claim that intelligent systems approach human effectiveness.

What is happening when these citations occur is not fraud or even careless scholarship. The citations are technically accurate in their attribution. VanLehn's paper does report the 0.76 figure. VanLehn's paper does claim that ITS approached human tutoring in his aggregation. What the citations strip is the context in which those numbers mean what they are cited as meaning. The heterogeneity VanLehn aggregated across disappears. The inclusion criteria disappear. The caveats disappear. The alternative meta-analyses disappear. The publication bias disappears. What remains is the number, detached from the analysis that produced it, circulating as though it were a stable fact about the world.

This is the rhetorical life of meta-analytic summary numbers. They are produced by researchers who understand their conditions and acknowledge their limitations. They are cited by advocates who understand the headline findings and not the conditions. The distance between the research context and the citation context is where meta-analytic numbers do most of their field-level work. They are invoked to license investment decisions, policy recommendations, and product claims that their original research contexts do not support. The invocations are not illegitimate — VanLehn's paper is real research that reports real findings — but the use of the findings in contexts where the conditions have been stripped is a specific rhetorical operation, not a neutral consequence of careful research.

---

The limit case of what this genre can produce is John Hattie's *Visible Learning*, first published in 2009. Hattie aggregated over 800 meta-analyses, comprising over 50,000 studies and data from millions of students, into a single ranked list of 138 influences on academic achievement. The list treats interventions ranging from "teacher-student relationships" to "direct instruction" to "class size" to "feedback" as commensurable effects on a single linear scale, each with a summary effect size, ranked from largest to smallest. Hattie proposed that 0.40 sigma should be treated as the average effect size of any educational innovation — the "hinge point" — and argued that educators should focus attention on interventions above this threshold.

*Visible Learning* has been enormously influential. It has been called "the Holy Grail of teaching" by educators who have structured professional development around its rankings. It has also been the subject of methodological critiques that, taken seriously, substantially undermine its central claims. Ivan Snook and colleagues have documented calculation errors in Hattie's work, including instances where Hattie's equations produced negative probabilities or probabilities exceeding one hundred percent. Steven Higgins has raised questions about the aggregation procedures. Pierre-Jérôme Bergeron has argued that the core methodology misapplies statistical procedures in ways that invalidate the headline rankings.

One example illustrates what Hattie's aggregation can produce. In his treatment of "feedback" — one of the highest-ranked interventions in *Visible Learning*, with a reported effect size of approximately 0.73 — Hattie drew on a diverse set of meta-analyses to compute the summary. One of the included studies evaluated the effect of background music on the agitation levels of elderly residents in nursing homes. Another evaluated feedback in classroom instruction. Another evaluated feedback in corporate training. Hattie's aggregation treated all of these as comparable evidence of what "feedback" produces as an educational intervention. A summary number computed across this range is not a claim about what feedback does in classrooms; it is a claim about the mathematical average of a set of heterogeneous studies labeled with the same word.

Hattie represents the limit case not because he is doing something fundamentally different from other meta-analysts, but because he has extended the logic of meta-analytic aggregation to its endpoint. If a summary number across fifty-four tutoring comparisons is a meaningful quantity, why not a summary across eight hundred meta-analyses of different interventions? If we can aggregate across different durations, populations, and outcome measures within a category, why not aggregate across categories? Hattie's work asks these questions structurally, and the answer his critics have given is that the aggregation produces numbers that do not correspond to anything in the world. Feedback in a classroom and feedback in a nursing home are not the same phenomenon. Their numerical average is not a fact about feedback; it is a fact about what happens when two unlike things are combined in a spreadsheet.

The critique that applies to Hattie applies, in more modest form, to careful meta-analyses like VanLehn's. Every meta-analysis involves aggregating studies that differ from one another in ways that matter. The question is always how much heterogeneity the summary number can honestly absorb before it becomes meaningless. VanLehn's aggregation is far more careful than Hattie's. It is still an aggregation. The summary numbers it produces smooth over heterogeneity, even when the heterogeneity is smaller and the aggregation is more principled. A reader who wants to know what the underlying literature actually shows has to look at the underlying studies. The summary number is an invitation, not a substitute.

---

Here is what I would like you to carry out of this essai.

When you next encounter a meta-analytic summary number cited as evidence for some claim about educational effectiveness — *meta-analyses show that tutoring produces gains of X sigma*, or *the research literature establishes that AI tutoring approaches human effectiveness*, or any variant of these — the questions to hold are not about whether the number is "correct." The number is probably computed correctly within its own framework. The questions are about what the number is the number of.

What was the inclusion criteria, and what would the number look like with different inclusion rules? What did the underlying studies measure, and over what durations, with what populations? Were the studies conducted by developers of the interventions they evaluated, or by independent researchers? How wide is the prediction interval around the summary, and does the interval include zero? What does a meta-analysis of the same literature with different inclusion rules produce? Have subsequent replications of the underlying studies succeeded?

You do not need answers to all of these questions to form a judgment. You need to notice that the questions exist. A meta-analytic summary sigma cited without its conditions is an invitation to examine the conditions. A citation that strips the conditions is doing rhetorical work rather than analytical work — inviting you to treat a specific aggregation as a general fact.

The apparatus from the second essai of this volume applies here with no modification. The five questions — outcome measure, baseline, population, timescale, cost — apply to meta-analyses just as they apply to individual studies. When applied to a meta-analysis, they become questions about the aggregation: what outcome measures does the aggregation include, what baselines, what populations, what timescales, under what cost conditions. A meta-analysis that aggregates across radically different answers to these questions is not producing a cleaner picture than the individual studies; it is producing a blurrier picture, with the blur concealed behind the apparent precision of a summary number.

This does not mean meta-analyses are worthless. A well-conducted meta-analysis that reports its heterogeneity openly, acknowledges its inclusion decisions as choices, and frames its summary as one interpretation rather than as a fact, can be genuinely useful. It synthesizes a literature. It reveals patterns. It surfaces the range of what studies have found. The work it does, however, is not to produce a single number that licenses claims about the world. It is to map the terrain of what has been studied, so that readers can navigate that terrain with specificity rather than trusting a summary to navigate it for them.

The next essai examines the intelligent-tutoring-systems literature that VanLehn aggregated — not through a summary number, but by reading what ITS actually measured and what ITS actually claimed. The essai argues that ITS measured exactly what it was built to measure, and that the "disappointment" narrative around ITS rests on comparing its measurements to a construct — *human tutoring* — that involves interactional moves the ITS apparatus was never designed to capture. Where VanLehn's meta-analysis placed ITS and human tutoring on a single sigma axis, the next essai asks whether the two are on the same axis at all.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai has tried to use VanLehn 2011 as a specimen of a genre without attacking VanLehn's work specifically. That balance is difficult, and I want to name where I am unsure I have held it.

I am not sure I have given VanLehn's methodological seriousness enough credit. His inclusion criteria were rigorous. His caveats were explicit. His framing of his own findings as a "progress report" was honest about what the analysis could and could not support. The critique this essai makes applies to the genre of meta-analysis rather than to VanLehn's specific execution, but the essai's structure — using his paper as the anchor for that critique — may read, in places, as though his work is the target. If it does, the drift is not what I intended. A reader who finds the treatment of VanLehn unfair is a reader I want to hear from.

I am not sure the alternative meta-analyses in Block B are represented with enough nuance. Steenbergen-Hu and Cooper, Kulik and Fletcher, Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan each did careful work with defensible inclusion criteria. The essai uses their different summary numbers to make the point that summaries depend on inclusion decisions, but this can read as though the analysts were disagreeing arbitrarily. They were not. Their choices were principled. The disagreement reflects genuine methodological differences about what should count as evidence for what. A more careful reading of any one of these meta-analyses would need more space than the essai gives them.

I am not sure the publication-bias section is proportionate. The Wolf et al. finding that developer-conducted evaluations produce effects approximately eighty percent larger than independent evaluations is striking, and the essai uses it to substantial analytical weight. Whether that single finding can bear the weight the essai places on it is uncertain. The pattern is real; the magnitude may be specific to particular research contexts and may not generalize as widely as the essai implies. A reader more expert in publication-bias methodology than I am may find my treatment simplified. I am counting on correction.

Finally, I am not sure the Hattie section lands where it should. I treat Hattie as the limit case of what meta-analytic aggregation can produce, and I use his critics' specific observations to argue that the logic of aggregation can be pushed past the point where the summary numbers correspond to anything in the world. But Hattie's influence on educational practice is substantial, and there are educators who have found *Visible Learning* useful in ways that my treatment does not engage. If the essai's reading of Hattie is too dismissive, or if it leaves important aspects of his work unaddressed, that is a failure of proportion rather than of argument. A Hattie-reader who wants to correct this reading should do so.

The next essai examines what ITS actually measured and what the construct of "human tutoring" involves that ITS never attempted to capture. I hope you will continue with me.
