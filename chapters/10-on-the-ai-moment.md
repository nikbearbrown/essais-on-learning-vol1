# On the AI Moment

*Essai 10*

---

In 2024, a team of physics education researchers at Harvard published a paper in *Scientific Reports* titled *AI Tutoring Outperforms Active Learning*. The lead authors — Gregory Kestin, Kelly Miller, Anna Klales, Timothy Milbourne, and Gregorio Ponti — reported what has circulated, in the year and a half since, as the largest single AI-tutor effect in the contemporary efficacy literature: an effect size of somewhere between 0.73 and 1.3 standard deviations, measured on a post-test covering the specific concepts of surface tension and fluid flow, across 194 undergraduate students enrolled in a large introductory physics course at Harvard called Physical Sciences 2.

The finding has taken on a specific rhetorical life. In the period between 2024 and 2026, in panels on AI and education, in investor presentations, in the Learning Engineering community discussions, and in journalism about where the AI-tutor moment stands, the Kestin study has been cited — often alongside studies from Eedi in the United Kingdom and Rori in Ghana — as evidence that something has finally changed. The long dry period of modest educational-technology effect sizes is over. The generative AI moment has produced findings that exceed human tutoring's benchmark, in a rigorous study, at an elite university. What intelligent tutoring systems could not do across four decades — what adaptive learning promised but did not deliver — AI tutors, apparently, can do.

I want to apply the apparatus this volume has built to this claim, honestly, without pre-judgment about where the apparatus will land.

The apparatus has nine elements at this point, developed across the prior nine essais. The five questions from the second essai: outcome measure, baseline, population, timescale, cost. The sixth observation from the fifth essai about how cross-study sigma comparisons smooth over structural incommensurabilities. The seventh from the sixth essai about what meta-analytic aggregation conceals. The eighth from the seventh essai about the construct-mismatch between what ITS measured and what human tutoring involves. The ninth from the ninth essai about engagement's substitution for learning evidence. These are the tools. This essai applies them to the four most prominent current-moment cases — Kestin, Eedi, Rori, Khanmigo — and reports what the apparatus reveals.

The essai's discipline, which I want to name before going further, is that the apparatus produces findings rather than conclusions. If the apparatus applied to Kestin reveals that the study supports a more specific claim than its public circulation implies, the essai reports that. If the apparatus applied to Rori reveals a finding that partially survives the critique — that a genuine cost-effective effect at real scale in a real context has been demonstrated — the essai reports that. The apparatus is not a debunking tool. It is a reading tool. What the reading produces is what the essai says.

---

Begin with Kestin, because the 1.3 sigma number is the one that has circulated most.

The first question is about the outcome measure. Kestin's post-test was researcher-designed. It was built by the same team that engineered the AI tutor and the active-learning comparison session. It covered the specific physics concepts — surface tension and fluid flow — that had been the content of the intervention session. The test measured, specifically, student performance on items drawn from the content taught in the two-hour window the study examined. The alignment between the intervention and the assessment was, by the researchers' own description, high. This is not a methodological failure; the researchers made a defensible choice. It is a specification of what the effect size is an effect size of. The 0.73 to 1.3 sigma gain is a gain on a test designed by the researchers to assess what the intervention taught. By the framing Cheung and Slavin and Kraft documented in the broader literature, effect sizes on aligned researcher-designed measures tend to cluster meaningfully higher than effect sizes on independent or standardized measures — sometimes by a factor of three or more. The Kestin effect, read through this lens, is what we would expect from a well-implemented intervention measured on its own test.

The second question is about the baseline. Kestin's comparison condition was described by the authors as "research-based active learning." This is a specific claim about the baseline's quality. The active-learning sessions involved peer instruction, small-group activities, and a flipped-classroom structure, conducted by instructors in Harvard physics classrooms. This is a strong baseline. It is arguably the state of the art in undergraduate physics pedagogy — the specific set of evidence-based practices that decades of physics education research, much of it by researchers at Harvard and elsewhere in the Physical Education Research community, has identified as superior to traditional lecture. An intervention that outperforms this baseline is outperforming good teaching, not bad teaching or no teaching.

This is a credit to Kestin's design. Most AI-tutor studies compare against weaker baselines — business-as-usual instruction, passive lecture, no intervention. Kestin's baseline was the higher standard. But the baseline being strong does not dissolve the effect-size question; it makes the effect size more interpretable. A 0.73 to 1.3 sigma gain over active-learning physics instruction means that the AI tutor, in this specific two-hour window, on aligned content, with this specific population, produced gains that active-learning instruction did not. The gain is real, in the sense that the measurement captured it. What it is a gain in — what construct it indexes beyond performance on the specific test — is the question the apparatus continues to ask.

The third question is about population. Kestin's 194 participants were Harvard undergraduates enrolled in Physical Sciences 2, an introductory physics course. These are students who cleared Harvard's admissions threshold, which selects for specific academic preparation, specific intellectual dispositions, and specific non-school resources (family support, academic coaching, summer programs, uninterrupted school attendance) that students from less-resourced contexts often lack. They chose to enroll in introductory physics, which self-selects further for quantitative and analytical inclination. They brought, as a population, high levels of self-regulated learning capacity — the ability to direct their own attention, to persist through difficult material, to manage their own engagement with a self-paced tool.

These characteristics matter for interpreting what the study shows. A self-paced AI tutor, deployed in an at-home-session format with a median of 49 minutes of engagement, asking students to work through carefully structured questions about surface tension, is a technology whose effectiveness depends on the student's capacity to engage productively with the interaction. Harvard physics undergraduates are close to an ideal population for this technology. Whether the same tool, with the same system prompt, deployed with the same 49-minute session structure, would produce the same effect with a different population — students with weaker self-regulation, students less comfortable with quantitative material, students with less reliable home infrastructure, students for whom the AI's particular conversational style feels more or less accessible — is a different question. The Kestin study does not answer it. It does not claim to.

The fourth question is about timescale. Kestin's post-test was administered immediately after the intervention session. The measurement window was short — the session, the test, and the reporting of outcomes occurred within a relatively compact time frame. There was no delayed post-test, no six-month follow-up, no measurement of whether the students retained the content, or could apply it in novel contexts, or could connect it to subsequent physics topics. The authors acknowledge this directly: the study measured immediate post-intervention performance and did not assess durability or transfer.

This is the point at which the apparatus produces its sharpest reading. Robert Bjork's research on storage strength versus retrieval strength, developed over decades, has established that immediate post-test performance and long-term learning are empirically dissociable. Conditions that maximize immediate performance — highly specific practice aligned with the test, short intervention-to-assessment intervals, testing contexts that share features with the learning context — often fail to produce durable storage that supports performance months or years later. Conditions that produce what Bjork calls "desirable difficulties" — spacing, interleaving, retrieval under difficulty — often depress immediate performance while building storage strength that shows up at delayed assessment. The Kestin study measured in the condition that maximally captures immediate performance. What it would show at three months, six months, a year out is not in the data. This is not a criticism of the study. It is a specification of what the effect size is a measurement of — performance, at the immediate timescale, on aligned material — which is different from *learning*, in the fuller sense Bjork's distinction names.

The fifth question is about cost. The Kestin paper does not report per-student implementation costs in the kind of detail Essai 12 of this volume will examine at length. Building and maintaining a carefully prompted AI tutor of the sort the study used — the system-prompt engineering, the iterative refinement, the constraint against direct answer-giving, the researcher oversight — required substantial research-team time and institutional infrastructure. The API costs for GPT-level inference at the scale of the study, while not prohibitive, are not trivial. Scaling this system from a 194-student research deployment to a population-scale rollout would require substantially more than what the research-deployment costs suggest. The study does not argue otherwise, but the rhetorical framing of the finding — that AI can produce human-tutor-exceeding effects at scale — is not supported by cost documentation within the paper itself. The cost denominator is absent from the comparison, and the comparison is weaker without it.

Put all five questions together. What the Kestin 2024 study supports, read through the apparatus, is this:

*A carefully designed AI tutor, deployed to Harvard introductory physics undergraduates for a self-paced session of approximately 49 minutes on the specific topic of surface tension and fluid flow, produced immediate post-test performance gains of approximately 0.73 to 1.3 standard deviations on a researcher-designed test aligned with the intervention content, compared to 60-minute active-learning classroom instruction on the same content, with costs for the research deployment not reported and costs for scaled deployment not documented, with durability and transfer unmeasured, in a population whose characteristics are close to ideal for this kind of intervention.*

This is a real finding. It is defensibly reported. It is not, however, the finding that has circulated as *AI tutoring outperforms human tutoring* or *AI has solved the 2-sigma problem*. The circulation strips the conditions. What remains — the sigma number bare — is what performs the rhetorical work. The finding that the apparatus produces is narrower, more specific, and supports less than the circulation implies.

I want to be careful about what this reading is and is not. It is not a claim that Kestin's researchers did anything wrong. They conducted a methodologically careful study, reported their results honestly, acknowledged their limitations. The gap the apparatus reveals is not their gap. It is the gap between what their finding specifically supports and what their finding is routinely invoked to support. The first is modest and interesting. The second, to the extent it differs from the first, is a rhetorical projection that the research does not underwrite.

Move to Eedi with Google DeepMind.

The Eedi-Google collaboration produced a 2025 randomized controlled trial examining LearnLM, a generative AI model that Google fine-tuned for pedagogical applications, deployed on Eedi's UK secondary-school platform. The study enrolled 165 students in Year 9 and Year 10 across five UK schools — roughly ages 13 to 15 — and compared three conditions: students receiving LearnLM-supported interactive tutoring; students receiving interactive tutoring from expert human tutors via online chat; and students receiving static pedagogical hints without interactive support. The reported finding was that LearnLM-supported students were 5.5 percentage points more likely to solve novel problems (66.2 percent success rate) than students receiving human-tutor support (60.7 percent), on measures of transfer to subsequent topics.

Run the apparatus on this finding.

The outcome measure, in this case, was notably different from Kestin's. The Eedi study measured performance on *novel problems on subsequent topics* — problems the student had not directly practiced during the intervention, covering topics downstream from the intervention content. This is a transfer measure, which is exactly what Bjork's distinction says honest learning claims should require. A 5.5 percentage point advantage on transfer to novel problems is not an artifact of test-alignment; it is evidence of something that generalizes past the specific content the intervention scaffolded. This is a methodological improvement over the typical EdTech efficacy study, and the Eedi researchers deserve credit for it.

The baseline matters here too, and it is where the study's structural distinctiveness becomes clear. The Eedi-Google trial compared the AI condition not just to passive controls but to *expert human tutoring via online chat*. This is an unusual comparison in the EdTech literature, which typically compares AI tutors to non-tutoring controls. Placing AI against a real human-tutor condition, with actual expert tutors chatting with actual students, is the test most directly relevant to the "AI approaches human tutoring" claim that the field has been making for fourteen years. The Eedi finding — that LearnLM performs at least as well as human tutors on the transfer measure — is a finding about this specific comparison.

But there is a feature of the Eedi study that the apparatus surfaces sharply, and that is easy to miss in the headline. The LearnLM condition in the study was not an unsupervised AI tutor. It was an AI tutor whose messages were monitored by expert human tutors, who were given the authority to revise each AI-drafted message before it was sent to the student. This is not the AI-tutor deployment most people imagine when they hear "AI can match human tutors." It is a hybrid system: an AI draft supervised and corrected by human experts before reaching the student. The comparison the study performed was not *AI versus human*. It was *AI-plus-human-oversight versus human-alone*. Both conditions had humans in the loop. Both required expert human labor.

This is not a criticism of the study. The researchers report the design clearly; they do not claim they tested unsupervised AI. But it changes the interpretation of the finding substantially. If supervised AI can match human tutors on transfer outcomes — that is, if AI-drafted messages with human expert review can do what human-drafted messages alone can do — the relevant question becomes cost-effectiveness. Does the supervised AI require less human expert time than pure human tutoring? Does the transfer gain come at reduced cost? The study does not comprehensively answer these questions, and the answer is likely "it depends on the specific deployment context." What the study clearly does not demonstrate is that AI alone, without human oversight, performs comparably to human tutors. That is a different and more difficult claim that would require a different study design.

Put the Eedi finding through the rest of the apparatus. The population was UK secondary-school students in a well-resourced context. The timescale was short — a single trial period, with the novel-problem assessment administered shortly after. The cost was not reported in a way that permits cost-effectiveness analysis. The study is an advance in methodology — transfer measurement is the right move, and the human-tutor comparison is stronger than most AI-tutor studies use — but the finding remains specific: *supervised AI-tutor interactions can produce transfer-to-novel-problems outcomes comparable to unsupervised human-tutor interactions, in a UK secondary-school context, over short timescales, with costs not fully documented.* This supports something, and the something it supports is meaningful. What it does not support is the circulated framing that "AI tutors have matched human tutoring."

Move to Rori.

Owen Henkel and colleagues at Rising Academies conducted a cluster randomized controlled trial of Rori, a WhatsApp-based AI math tutor, in Ghanaian secondary schools across 2024. The design enrolled an initial sample of 637 students in grades 3 through 8 across eleven schools — five treatment, six control — with the intervention consisting of two thirty-minute weekly sessions during study hall periods, over eight months. The outcome was a 35-question math assessment drawn from the Global Proficiency Framework, administered at baseline and endline. The reported finding was an effect size of approximately 0.36 standard deviations, with a marginal cost of approximately five dollars per student.

The apparatus reveals different things here.

The outcome measure was curriculum-aligned but not developer-designed. The 35-question assessment came from an external framework (the Global Proficiency Framework) rather than from the Rori team's own test construction. This is methodologically stronger than the typical aligned developer-designed assessment. The finding is not an artifact of researchers testing their own tool on their own test. It is a measurement of math outcomes using an external instrument, even though the instrument's items overlap with the mathematical skills the intervention taught.

The baseline was regular Ghanaian school math instruction. This is a substantially different baseline from Kestin's Harvard active learning or Eedi's UK secondary-school instruction. Ghanaian schools, on average, face resource constraints that UK and American schools do not: fewer trained teachers in some regions, limited mathematical curriculum materials, larger class sizes, constrained time per student. The baseline against which Rori produced 0.36 sigma is not the baseline Kestin's 0.73 measures against. This does not make the Rori effect less real. It means the comparison between Rori's 0.36 and Kestin's 0.73, which appears as a simple sigma comparison in many discussions of current AI tutoring, obscures the different baselines against which each was measured. The 0.36 against a constrained baseline and the 0.73 against a strong baseline are not findings on the same axis.

The population matters too. Ghanaian secondary-school students work with fewer non-school supports on mathematical learning than Harvard undergraduates do. They have less access to home internet, fewer mathematical tutors available, less cultural capital that maps directly to academic achievement. For an intervention to produce meaningful learning gains in this population, using basic mobile phones and limited data, is a different kind of accomplishment than producing effects with Harvard undergraduates in a research setting. The accomplishment is not smaller; it is different. A sigma comparison that treats them as comparable obscures the different work each is doing.

The timescale was longer than either Kestin or Eedi — an eight-month intervention period. This is meaningful. A delayed-assessment window of months rather than days captures retention and subsequent-month performance rather than immediate post-test gain. The apparatus reads this as a stronger timescale-specification than the Kestin study provides. Whether eight months is long enough to capture genuine durability, or whether effects decay over the longer subsequent years, is a further question the study cannot answer. But the eight-month window is meaningfully more robust than the same-day window.

The cost was reported, specifically: approximately five dollars per student per year in marginal cost, including LLM API fees and data costs. This is the anchor the entire current AI-tutor conversation most needs, and Rori provides it. The 0.36 sigma effect at five dollars per student is a qualitatively different finding from a 0.73 sigma effect at an unreported cost structure that is likely orders of magnitude higher. When the cost denominator is included, the Rori finding becomes one of the most interesting in the current literature — not because its sigma is largest, but because its sigma-per-dollar appears to be largest. The apparatus rewards this kind of reporting. The field should too.

The Rori study also had limitations that are worth naming. Attrition was substantial — 25 percent of students who took the baseline assessment did not take the endline, largely due to school attendance patterns. This affects how the effect size should be interpreted: the 0.36 is an effect among students who completed both assessments, not among all students assigned to the intervention. An intention-to-treat analysis across all originally-enrolled students would produce a smaller effect. This is common in educational research in low-resource contexts, and the researchers report the attrition honestly, but it is a specification that qualifies the finding.

Put the Rori study through the full apparatus. The outcome measure was curriculum-aligned but external. The baseline was weaker than Kestin's. The population faces substantially different constraints. The timescale was longer. The cost was five dollars per student. The attrition was significant. The effect size, at 0.36 sigma on an external measure with these qualifications, is a real effect on a specific population at a specific cost under specific conditions. It is also a qualitatively different finding from Kestin's, and placing the two on a single sigma axis — which both Jonah Bard's Learning Engineering list message and much subsequent discussion has done — is a category error the apparatus makes visible.

Finally, Khanmigo.

The Khanmigo arc is not a single study; it is a three-year public trajectory that the next essai of this volume will examine in detail. What I want to do here is apply the apparatus to what Khan Academy itself has publicly reported, which turns out to be a different kind of report than Kestin, Eedi, or Rori produced.

In April 2023, Sal Khan said from the TED stage that Khanmigo represented "probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen." The claim was forward-looking. The evidence base at that moment was pilots, early demonstrations, engagement data from a small population. There was no published efficacy study. In the years since, Khan Academy has continued to deploy Khanmigo to tens of thousands of teachers and students, with substantial research infrastructure and careful attention to product development.

What has Khan Academy's own research on Khanmigo found? As of April 2026, the honest answer is: not much at the level of rigorous efficacy claims. In the April 2026 *Chalkbeat* interview with Matt Barnum, Kristen DiCerbo — Khan Academy's Chief Learning Officer — said directly: *"So far I am not seeing the revolution in education."* Sal Khan acknowledged that for most students, Khanmigo has been a "non-event" — they have not used it enough to produce effects one way or another. DiCerbo observed specifically that students who did engage with the AI often responded to its prompts with "IDK IDK" — *I don't know, I don't know* — typing the phrase rather than engaging with the Socratic dialogue the tool was designed to support.

Apply the apparatus to this.

The outcome measure, in Khan Academy's own public reporting, is engagement. Students using the platform. Students not using the platform. Quality of interactions (which turn out to include the "IDK IDK" problem). There is no published rigorous learning-outcome measurement analogous to Kestin or Eedi or Rori. DiCerbo's statement that the revolution has not happened is not, technically, a finding from a study; it is an institutional acknowledgment from a senior researcher that what the tool was built to produce has not, at scale, been produced.

The baseline is therefore not a comparison condition in a study sense. It is the counterfactual implicit in the claim — what would have happened without Khanmigo deployment. DiCerbo's observation is that the difference between *with Khanmigo* and *without Khanmigo*, for most students, is small enough to call a non-event.

The population is students in schools that have adopted Khanmigo — primarily in the United States, primarily middle school and high school, primarily in Khan Academy's existing user base. This population has not, as DiCerbo and Khan have acknowledged, used Khanmigo in ways that produce the dialogue patterns the tool was designed to support.

The timescale is three years — from April 2023 to April 2026. This is a long enough window that durability and transfer questions could have been examined, if the outcomes had produced something worth durability-testing. The April 2026 acknowledgment is, in effect, a report that the fundamental engagement conditions for rigorous efficacy measurement have not been met at the scale required.

The cost for Khan Academy is not directly disclosed in public reporting. The organization is a non-profit; its development costs for Khanmigo have been substantial, drawing on partnership with OpenAI and philanthropic funding. The cost to schools adopting Khanmigo has reportedly been included in Khan Academy's broader pricing structure. The cost-per-student-actually-using-Khanmigo, given DiCerbo's "non-event" observation, is a different number from the cost-per-student-assigned-Khanmigo. If 80 percent of students do not meaningfully engage, the cost per meaningfully engaged student is five times the cost per assigned student.

What the apparatus reveals about Khanmigo, in April 2026, is specific and uncomfortable. The 2023 claim that Khanmigo would produce the biggest educational transformation ever seen has not been supported by evidence that Khan Academy itself finds compelling. This is Khan Academy's own honest reporting, not external critique. DiCerbo saying publicly that she does not see the revolution is the kind of institutional acknowledgment that most organizations do not make. Khan Academy gets credit, in this moment, for not continuing to invoke 2023-era claims when the evidence does not support them. The next essai — on what the field does when tools underperform — examines this kind of acknowledgment as a disciplinary move, contrasting it with the more typical rhetorical responses to disappointing evidence.

What the apparatus does not reveal is whether Khanmigo has produced any genuine learning effects in the sub-populations that have engaged with it meaningfully. DiCerbo acknowledged that *most* students produced non-events, which leaves room for some students having produced something. What Khan Academy could report — if it wanted to — is the learning outcomes of students in the top engagement tier, measured on independent assessments, with appropriate cost-per-actually-engaged-student. They have not reported this. Perhaps they will. Perhaps the answer would be small. The apparatus, in the absence of such reporting, cannot say more.

Now step back.

Four cases. Kestin, Eedi, Rori, Khanmigo. Each analyzed through the apparatus. What emerges?

Not a single verdict. Each study supports something, and what each supports is different. Kestin demonstrates that a carefully designed AI tutor deployed to elite undergraduates on researcher-designed aligned content over a short timescale can produce large immediate effects. Eedi demonstrates that supervised AI tutors can match human tutors on transfer measures under a specific human-oversight structure. Rori demonstrates that a low-cost AI tutor deployed over longer timescales in a constrained context can produce meaningful effects on external curriculum-aligned measures. Khanmigo demonstrates that large-scale deployment without intensive human-system support can produce the "non-event" pattern when students do not self-regulate their engagement.

These four findings are not the same finding. They are four different findings about four different deployments measured with four different methodologies against four different baselines. A reader who is asked to judge the contemporary AI-tutor moment as a single phenomenon — *is AI tutoring working* — is being asked a question that does not have a single answer because the phenomenon is not a single phenomenon. What the four studies together support is a much narrower claim: *certain AI-tutor deployments in certain contexts with certain populations measured in certain ways can produce certain kinds of effects*. This is a specification. It is not the transformative-claim framing that has circulated in advocacy.

The pattern the apparatus reveals is also the pattern the earlier essais identified in earlier technologies. Effect sizes inflate when outcome measures align with intervention content. Baselines matter; comparing against strong baselines produces different numbers than comparing against weak ones. Populations are not interchangeable; an intervention's effect on Harvard physics undergraduates is not the same as its effect on rural Ghanaian eighth-graders. Timescales matter; immediate post-tests capture performance, not durable learning. Cost is a constitutive variable; without it, sigma comparisons are not comparable. Engagement is not learning. Construct-mismatch persists.

These patterns, the apparatus shows, have not been solved by the current AI moment. They have been inherited. The generative AI layer has not changed the measurement apparatus underneath. The studies produce sigmas on aligned measures at short timescales at uncharacterized costs, and the sigmas circulate detached from the conditions. This is what the ITS era did. It is what the adaptive-learning era did. It is what the AI-tutor era is doing.

What the current moment *has* produced, and what is worth naming, are modest specific signals. The Eedi study's transfer measurement is an advance. The Rori study's cost reporting is an advance. Khanmigo's public acknowledgment in April 2026 that the evidence does not support the 2023 claim is an advance — not in evidence, but in the institutional willingness to say so. These are pieces of something. They are not, individually or collectively, the revolution.

I want to close this essai by articulating provisionally what the next generation of AI-tutor research would require to be worth believing.

Not because I know what the field should do. The closing essai of this volume will be explicit about my refusal to prescribe to the field. But because the apparatus has been applied to this case, and the application produces a specification — a set of methodological commitments that would address the gaps the apparatus revealed. That specification is where this essai lands.

A next-generation AI-tutor study, read through the full apparatus, would include the following specifications.

Independent outcome measures, not researcher-designed. Assessments constructed by evaluators separate from the intervention developers, using items that cover the relevant construct without being aligned to what the intervention specifically taught. This is the harder test that Bjork's distinction between performance and learning requires.

Delayed post-tests. Assessment windows at six months, twelve months, and ideally longer. The immediate post-test captures retrieval strength at maximum; durable learning is what the longer-timescale measurement reveals. An AI-tutor study that measures only at the immediate window is measuring performance, not learning.

Transfer testing. Problems the student did not explicitly practice, drawn from constructs the intervention targeted rather than from items aligned with what the intervention taught. The Eedi study's move in this direction is what transfer testing looks like; it should be the standard, not the exception.

Population diversity. Studies across multiple populations with honest reporting of which sub-populations the intervention helps, which it does not help, and which it might be harming. The Harvard physics finding is not the Ghanaian secondary-school finding. Both are valid. Neither generalizes.

Cost-inclusive reporting. Marginal cost per student in the specific deployment context. Infrastructure costs for scale. Human-oversight costs if the deployment requires oversight. The cost denominator is the dimension most missing from current reporting, and it is the dimension most essential for honest cross-study comparison.

Construct specification. What, specifically, does the intervention claim to produce? Is the measurement an honest proxy for that construct, or is it a narrower operationalization? The distinction between *what the measurement indexes* and *what the claim invokes* is what the thirteenth essai of this volume will examine centrally. For now, the specification is: researchers should state clearly what construct their claim concerns and how their measurement indexes that construct.

Pre-registration. The study's measurement choices and analytical decisions specified before the data is examined. This is standard practice in clinical research. Education research has not adopted it at scale. An AI-tutor literature that continues to make choices retrospectively, based on what the data looked like, cannot be distinguished from a literature that has tuned its choices to produce publishable effects.

Adversarial collaboration. Developers and independent evaluators working together, with methodological commitments negotiated in advance. This would address the developer-evaluator bias that the sixth essai of this volume documented.

These are not revolutionary specifications. They are standard practice in mature research fields. What is revealing about the contemporary AI-tutor literature is how few of these practices are routinely followed. The field's measurement apparatus, as currently practiced, produces studies that optimize for reportable effect sizes rather than for findings that would survive external scrutiny. This is not a conspiracy. It is an incentive structure. The path out is to specify the incentives differently — to reward evaluations that would hold up under the apparatus rather than evaluations that would survive only on their own terms.

I do not know if the field will do this. The book is not a prescription for the field. It is an apparatus for readers to apply to claims they encounter. If you encounter an AI-tutor efficacy claim, and the study reports immediate post-test effects on aligned measures without transfer testing or durability testing or cost reporting or independent evaluation, you know what the apparatus will reveal when you apply it. If the study reports something more — delayed assessment, transfer measurement, external measures, cost specification — the apparatus produces a different reading. The next generation of AI-tutor research that is worth believing will be the research that can survive the apparatus applied to it. Whether that research will be conducted, and whether it will be cited as carefully as the current wave of aligned-and-short-timescale studies has been, are questions the apparatus cannot answer.

The next essai examines what happens when the tools do not produce what was claimed. Khanmigo's 2023-2026 arc is one instance of this pattern. The essai asks what the field does when efficacy disappoints, and what the specific moves the field makes reveal about how claims are protected when evidence moves against them. I hope you will continue with me.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai is the volume's synthesis moment in Act III, and the discipline it requires — applying the apparatus honestly to the current moment without pre-judgment — is difficult to hold. I want to name where I am uncertain whether I have held it.

I am not sure I have been fair enough to the Kestin study. Kestin and colleagues conducted a methodologically careful piece of research, reported their findings honestly with their own limitations, and produced a result that is real within its conditions. The essai's treatment of the study applies the apparatus to reveal what the 0.73-to-1.3 sigma finding specifically supports, which is narrower than how the finding has circulated. But a reader could fairly ask whether my treatment gives the study its due as a contribution versus treating it as a specimen of a pattern. I have tried to name specific credits — the strong baseline, the honest limitations acknowledgment — but the essai's gravitational pull is toward the apparatus's reading, which is necessarily more constrained than the study's own framing. If the treatment reads as ungenerous, tell me where.

I am not sure about the treatment of Eedi. The study's methodological advance — transfer measurement, human-tutor comparison condition — deserves genuine credit, and I have tried to give it. But I have also surfaced the supervised-AI structure that changes how the finding should be interpreted. A reader sympathetic to the Eedi researchers might say the supervised-AI finding is itself the relevant finding for real-world deployment contexts, where some level of human oversight will remain a structural feature of AI deployment. The essai treats the supervised structure as a complication; a reader might reasonably treat it as a feature.

I am not sure I have done justice to Rori. The essai treats Rori as the most interesting current-moment case because its cost reporting is the most honest. But the essai spends less space on Rori than on Kestin, partly because Kestin is the larger public phenomenon and partly because the apparatus produces more specific findings applied to Kestin's conditions. A reader for whom low-resource-context interventions are the primary concern may find the Rori treatment too thin.

I am not sure about the specifications for next-generation research in the closing. The eight items I list — independent measures, delayed post-tests, transfer testing, and so on — are the methodological practices a careful reader would identify as currently underutilized. They are not controversial within methodology. They are also not guaranteed to produce the learning outcomes the field has hoped for; a next-generation study could meet all eight specifications and still produce findings that do not support the revolutionary claims of 2023. The essai does not promise that these specifications would produce good news. It promises they would produce better evidence. Whether a reader interprets "better evidence" as "evidence that would support more ambitious conclusions" or "evidence that would support more modest conclusions" is the reader's judgment, not the essai's.

Finally, I am not sure about the closing paragraphs that gesture toward what would be required for next-generation research to be worth believing. This is the closest the essai comes to prescription, which the book's register is trying to avoid. A reader might reasonably say that specifying methodological requirements is already a form of prescription, even if it is framed as an apparatus application. I have kept the specifications because they are the specific findings the apparatus produces when applied to the current moment; removing them would leave the essai without its closing. But the balance between application and prescription is delicate, and I am not sure I have held it where I want to hold it.

The next essai takes up what the field does when its tools disappoint — the specific rhetorical moves Khan Academy's honesty in April 2026 avoided, and that other organizations have deployed in comparable moments. I hope you will continue with me.
