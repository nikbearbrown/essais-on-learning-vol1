# On Engagement as Evidence

![09 On Engagement As Evidence](../images/09-on-engagement-as-evidence.jpg)


*Essai 9*

---

The essai opens on a pairing, and the pairing is doing most of the work before the argument begins.

On one side, Kristen DiCerbo, Khan Academy's Chief Learning Officer, speaking to *Chalkbeat* in April of 2026 about the three-year arc of Khanmigo. Students who engaged with the AI tutor, she said, often responded to its prompting questions by typing *IDK IDK* — *I don't know, I don't know* — and moving on. The platform recorded them as active users. The engagement metrics were, in their own terms, functioning. Whatever the metrics were supposed to be evidence of was not.

On the other side, Luis von Ahn, CEO of Duolingo, who has said for over a decade that his goal is to make language learning as addictive as a mobile game. He has said this from the TED stage, in press interviews, in podcast appearances, in the company's own blog posts. The company he built is one of the largest publicly traded education companies in the world, with tens of millions of daily active users, and its business model rests on the sustained engagement von Ahn has been transparent about optimizing for.

Both are being honest. Both are describing, accurately, what they see. One is saying the engagement metrics are not the thing the engagement metrics are supposed to be evidence of. The other is saying the engagement metrics are the thing the business is for.

The gap between these two accounts is where the essai does its work.

---

### What the Essai Refuses

This essai is disciplined in what it refuses. It does not say engagement metrics are meaningless. It says explicitly — in a section of genuine methodological care — that a product with zero engagement is doing nothing, and a product with high engagement is doing something, and these are real facts companies are right to measure. What engagement does not do, the essai says, is answer the question that educational technologies routinely position themselves as answering.

It does not say Duolingo is committing fraud. In a sentence I want to quote back because it does particular work, the writer names the essai itself: *This is why the essai is called On Engagement as Evidence rather than On Engagement as Deception.* The pattern the essai examines is not that companies lie about what their engagement metrics show. The pattern is that companies report engagement metrics in contexts where the audience expects learning-outcome evidence, and allow the audience to draw the inference that the two are closely connected.

It does not indict anyone. The Vesselinov-Grego 2012 study that produced Duolingo's canonical *34 hours equals one university semester* finding is read with methodological patience: WebCAPE is a placement test rather than a comprehensive proficiency measure, the 34 hours was a point estimate with a wide confidence interval, the sample was restricted in ways that limit generalization, the study was commissioned by Duolingo and conducted by researchers selected by Duolingo. None of this is scandal. Industry-commissioned research is standard. The finding is what it is. What the writer wants you to notice is not that the study is illegitimate but that its specific finding has traveled as something broader than what it actually found.

What the essai is doing, across all of this, is insisting on the distinction between a rhetorical critique and a moral accusation. Rhetorical critiques can be answered. Moral accusations cannot; they can only be denied or absorbed. The writer wants you to see the pattern and decide for yourself what it means. He does not want to tell you what it means.

Some readers will find this restraint too generous. They will want the accusation. They will want Duolingo called out, von Ahn's long-stated philosophy treated as evidence rather than as context, the company's evidence base named as inadequate rather than as thin. The essai gives them none of these. What it gives them, instead, is the apparatus for arriving at their own judgments without the writer's help.

---

### DiCerbo as Witness

The essai's deepest move — and I think this is the move that makes it work as a piece of writing rather than as a complaint — is what it does with DiCerbo.

DiCerbo could have described Khanmigo's engagement metrics and called the program a success. Khan Academy's platform had measured a certain number of sessions, processed a certain number of interactions, served a certain number of students. All of these numbers would have been true. Any of them could have been reported in the register the field expects — in a funder update, in a press release, in an investor communication if Khan Academy were the kind of organization that had investors. DiCerbo chose something else. She looked at what the students were doing inside those interactions. She saw them typing *IDK IDK* and moving on. She said, in public, that the tool was not producing what it was designed to produce, and that she and Khan had not figured out how to solve the problem.

The essai credits this. I want to credit how carefully it credits this.

The writer does not make DiCerbo a hero. He does not dwell. He gives her one full paragraph — careful, specific, analytically grounded — and moves on. What the paragraph names is the sentence I want to stay with: *in a field where the structural incentives run entirely the other direction — where the reputational cost of admitting that your product is not producing what you claimed is large, and the reputational cost of letting engagement metrics stand as effectiveness evidence is small — DiCerbo did the harder thing.* That is the sentence that does the work. It names what was structurally at stake in her speaking, without making her sainthood the essai's subject.

This is a Baldwin-ish move. Baldwin was careful, across his essays, to credit specific acts of honesty without sentimentalizing the people who performed them. The honesty was important because it was rare. The rarity was itself a comment on the structure that made it rare. The essai is doing this with DiCerbo — using her honesty as the specific example of what the discipline looks like when someone chooses to enact it, while refusing to turn her into a symbol that the essai would then have to defend.

What DiCerbo saw, the essai then tells you, is what the self-regulated-learning literature has named for a long time. Roger Azevedo and others have distinguished between *behavioral engagement* and *cognitive engagement*. Behavioral engagement is what platforms can measure from outside — clicks, sessions, time on task, lessons completed. Cognitive engagement is what happens inside the learner, which is not directly observable, which has to be inferred from indirect evidence, from the quality of responses rather than the presence of them, from later performance on transfer tasks. The distinction matters because the two can dissociate. A student can be behaviorally engaged without being cognitively engaged. DiCerbo witnessed this. The metrics could not.

This is where the essai turns from description to structural claim. Engagement metrics, by their nature, measure only what is observable from outside the learner. The thing educational technology is supposed to produce — the cognitive work that constitutes learning — is not observable from outside. The substitution of engagement for learning is partly the substitution of the measurable proxy for the unobservable thing the proxy is supposed to index. You could describe the substitution as a rhetorical trick. You could also describe it as the only option a platform has, given what platforms can see. The essai lets both readings stand.

---

### Three Substitutions, One Structure

The essai then does something I want to flag as structurally important. It names three layers of the substitution, and shows how each layer nests inside the next.

The first layer is rhetorical. Engagement metrics reported in contexts where learning-outcome evidence is what the audience expects. This is the Duolingo pattern. It is a reporting choice, not a factual claim, which is why it cannot be falsified in the ordinary sense but can be challenged.

The second layer is design. Products optimized for engagement are designed around mechanisms that reliably produce engagement — loss aversion through streak-loss penalties, social comparison through leaderboards, variable-ratio reinforcement through XP and badges. These mechanisms work. They are not the same mechanisms that produce learning, which tend to involve what Robert Bjork called *desirable difficulty* — effortful retrieval, spaced practice, occasional genuine failure. The research on desirable difficulty, running for decades now, has shown that short-term performance and long-term learning can dissociate in ways that make engagement-optimized products specifically ill-suited to learning production.

The third layer is economic. Rigorous learning-outcome evaluation is expensive. Independent researchers, external outcome measures, delayed follow-ups, sustained periods — this is what Saga Education and the high-dosage tutoring literature have produced, and it has produced real findings about real learning, and the interventions it has evaluated cost thousands of dollars per student per year. Engagement measurement, by contrast, is cheap: the platform's own analytics capture it by default, in real time, with no additional cost. The evidence structures match the cost structures. Cheap interventions get evaluated against cheap metrics. What cheap metrics can see is what gets reported. What cheap metrics cannot see is what the field's public discourse then has to go without.

These three layers are not three separate problems. They are the same problem at different scales. The rhetorical substitution is what you see. The design substitution is what produces it. The economic substitution is what sustains the structure that produces it. The essai names all three without turning any of them into the single villain, which is, I think, exactly the right call. A critique that targeted only the rhetoric would be answered by tighter marketing language. A critique that targeted only the design would be answered by research that never gets funded. A critique that targets the cost structure names a condition the field has not yet figured out how to change.

---

### What the Essai Leaves

What the reader leaves with are two questions.

*Is this engagement data, or is it learning-outcome data?* Engagement data describes what users do with the platform. Learning-outcome data describes what users can do afterward, outside the platform, measured independently. They are not the same category. If what is being claimed is that the product produces learning, and what is being offered as evidence is that users engage with the product, the evidence is in a different category from the claim.

*If this product were not producing learning, would anyone notice?* For products whose evidentiary apparatus rests on engagement metrics alone, the answer is usually no.

These are serviceable questions. They are not, I think, as sharp as the five questions Essai 2 of this volume installed. The first is diagnostic. The second is rhetorical — it does not tell you what to do, it tells you to be suspicious, which is a different operation. The writer flags this uncertainty in his own closing note. A reader applying the two questions to, say, the specific AI-tutor studies the next essai in the volume takes up will find them useful but slightly coarse compared to the five-question apparatus.

That is the essai's one real cost. Its argument is strong. Its specific observations about Duolingo's evidence base and DiCerbo's act of witness and the three-layered substitution hold up under pressure. Its closing diagnostic is slightly less load-bearing than the preceding work deserves.

But here is what the essai does that a generic critique of engagement-as-evidence would not. It credits the witness. It names the rare act. It refuses to let DiCerbo's *IDK IDK* observation function as an indictment of Khan Academy, and instead lets it function as the specific example of what a discipline looks like when someone enacts it. That is a difficult writerly choice, and the essai makes it without flinching.

When someone tells you, carefully and in public, that the metrics they are being paid to celebrate are not the thing the metrics are supposed to be evidence of, they are doing something Baldwin would have recognized. They are testifying. The essai's achievement is in noticing the testimony and refusing to turn it into anything else.
