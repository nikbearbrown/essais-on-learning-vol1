# On What We Measure and What We Care About

*Essai 13 — Draft*

---

Consider two findings, forty years apart.

In 1984, Benjamin Bloom published a short paper in *Educational Researcher* reporting that students tutored one-on-one under mastery-learning conditions performed, on average, two standard deviations above students taught in conventional classrooms. The paper ran seventeen pages. It was based on two doctoral dissertations Bloom had supervised at the University of Chicago. The finding has been cited tens of thousands of times and has become, over four decades, perhaps the single most-invoked benchmark in educational technology. Whenever a new intervention — a new tutor, a new system, a new platform — claims to approach the effectiveness of human one-on-one instruction, it is Bloom's 2-sigma finding it is claiming to approach.

In 2024, a research team at Harvard led by Gregory Kestin reported that an AI tutor, deployed in an introductory physics course, produced learning gains substantially larger than conventional active-learning classroom instruction. The effect size exceeded what prior literature had typically reported for any tutoring intervention, including Bloom's. The study was methodologically careful: a randomized design, a rigorous active-learning baseline, pre- and post-testing with aligned items, a specific physics domain with established assessment instruments. The finding circulated quickly. Within weeks it was being cited as evidence that current-generation AI tutors meaningfully exceed what good conventional instruction can deliver.

Forty years apart. Different technologies. Different research traditions. Different specific methodologies. And yet, read carefully, the two findings share a structure.

In each, a specific measurement — performance on items aligned to the intervention's content, assessed at short timescale, against a conventional-instruction baseline — is offered as evidence for a construct about which the measurement is not, strictly, a measurement. Bloom's 2-sigma is evidence about students' performance on aligned items under particular tutoring conditions. It is *cited* as evidence about the effectiveness of tutoring as an instructional mode. Kestin's physics finding is evidence about short-timescale aligned-item performance in a selective undergraduate population. It is *cited* as evidence that AI tutoring outperforms human instruction in some general sense the measurement does not index.

The measurements are not false. The findings are not inflated. In each case, the researchers reported carefully what they measured, and what they measured is what they found. The question this essai takes up is what happens between the measurement and its citation — the small, structural, and repeated gap between what the apparatus indexes and what the vocabulary surrounding the apparatus claims.

Across the previous twelve essais, the book has walked this gap in specific instances. In Bloom's case. In the intelligent-tutoring-systems literature. In adaptive learning's personalization claims. In the engagement metrics that substitute when harder outcomes refuse to cooperate. In the current generation of AI-tutor efficacy reporting. In the blame-the-implementation move the field performs when tools underperform. In the systematic omission of cost from efficacy claims.

Each of those essais read a particular case. What the cases share is the subject of this one.

## The structure

It will help to name the structure directly.

An efficacy claim in this field typically consists of three things: a measurement, a construct, and an asserted relationship between them. The measurement is the thing the researchers actually did — items administered, scores computed, conditions compared. The construct is what the measurement is meant to be evidence for — *learning*, *mastery*, *effectiveness*, *personalization*, *engagement*, some other category the field's vocabulary invokes. The asserted relationship is the claim that the measurement indexes the construct, at least well enough to license the uses the researchers and subsequent citers put the finding to.

This is the structure of any empirical claim in any field. Biology works this way, and so does physics, and so does nutrition research. The gap between measurement and construct is not a problem specific to educational technology. It is a feature of empirical inquiry. Measurements never exhaustively capture their constructs; operationalizations are always provisional; the question for any field is how seriously it takes the gap, how much work it does to establish the measurement-construct relationship, and how much of that relationship it assumes versus demonstrates.

The observation this book has been building toward, essai by essai, is that the learning-systems field has, across six decades, taken the gap less seriously than its claims require. The measurement-construct relationships it invokes are almost universally assumed. The field's vocabulary outruns what its evidence apparatus can actually support, and the gap persists not because it has not been noticed — it has been noticed, repeatedly, by careful researchers across multiple traditions — but because the apparatus that persists serves specific production conditions, and a more adequate apparatus would serve them less well.

The structure is not: *the field is wrong about what works.* The structure is: *the field makes claims about effectiveness that its measurements are not positioned to support, and does so systematically.* These are importantly different claims. The first is a claim about facts. The second is a claim about apparatus — about the specific set of measurement practices, citation habits, and research conventions that together produce what the field calls its evidence base.

The distinction matters because the remedy differs. If the field were making factual errors about particular interventions, the remedy would be better studies of those interventions. If the field's apparatus is producing a systematic gap between what it measures and what it claims, the remedy is different apparatus. The book has not argued for either remedy. It has argued, by the accumulated force of twelve close readings, that the second diagnosis is correct and the first is not.

What this essai does is make that argument visible as argument. Across the previous essais, the claim has been implicit in the pattern. It has never been stated at this level. Here it is stated, and the rest of this essai unpacks what follows from stating it.

## What the field claims to care about

Begin with the vocabulary.

Open a textbook in educational psychology. Open a learning-sciences journal. Open the marketing copy for any major adaptive-learning platform. Open the abstract of any recent AI-tutor efficacy study. The vocabulary is remarkably consistent. The field claims to be producing evidence about *learning*. About *understanding*. About *mastery*. About *effectiveness*. About, in more recent vintages, *personalization* and *engagement*. The platforms claim to improve *learning outcomes*. The studies measure *learning gains*. The reformers advocate for *learning at scale*.

Each of these words points toward a construct. Each of the constructs has, in serious research traditions, substantial theoretical and empirical articulation. None of them is trivial.

Consider *learning*. In Robert Bjork's decades of experimental work, learning is not a single construct but a distinction between two related-but-separable things: storage strength and retrieval strength. Storage strength refers to how well a representation is encoded; retrieval strength refers to how accessible it is at the moment of test. A student can have high retrieval strength at the end of a unit — they perform well on the post-test — without high storage strength. Weeks or months later, the retrieval strength decays rapidly, and the post-test performance turns out to have been measuring the wrong thing. This is the central insight of what Bjork calls desirable difficulties: conditions that depress retrieval strength in the short run often build storage strength in the long run, which is why practice conditions that feel harder (spaced practice, interleaved practice, testing rather than re-reading) produce better long-term outcomes than practice conditions that feel easier.

The distinction is not abstract. It is operationalized in the experimental literature. A learning claim grounded in Bjork's construct of learning requires evidence of storage strength, not just retrieval strength — which requires measuring performance after a delay, in new contexts, on items not identical to those used in training. The methodology exists. It has existed since the early 1990s. It is the basis of essentially every recommendation in *Make It Stick*, the popular synthesis by Brown, Roediger, and McDaniel that many readers of this book have encountered.

Now consider how *learning* is typically operationalized in EdTech efficacy research. The outcome measure is usually a post-test administered at the end of the instructional unit. The post-test uses items aligned with the instructional content. The interval between instruction and test is hours to days. The retrieval context is the same or similar to the learning context. What this operationalization measures is retrieval strength at short delay. What Bjork's construct of learning requires is storage strength at longer delay under different retrieval conditions. These are not the same thing. They are related. They are also empirically dissociable — which is what forty years of experimental work on desirable difficulties has demonstrated.

The gap between the two is not subtle. It is structural. And it is present in nearly every efficacy claim the book has read.

Consider *understanding*. Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger, and before them John Dewey, and alongside them James Greeno and the situated-cognition tradition, have spent decades articulating understanding as something different from performance on items. Understanding, in these traditions, involves the capacity to apply knowledge in contexts that differ from the contexts of acquisition. It involves participation in practices — knowing how to use what one knows in the world where it applies. It is not adequately tested by asking whether a learner can reproduce a trained response to a trained stimulus. Transfer testing — the capacity to apply learning to problems that differ meaningfully from the problems used in training — is the minimum methodological requirement for a claim about understanding.

Transfer testing has been advocated for in educational research since Thorndike's early twentieth-century work on the transfer of learning. Thorndike argued that transfer was much more limited than educators assumed. Subsequent work has largely confirmed this: transfer is real but specific, and most trained performance does not generalize automatically to untrained contexts. For a claim about understanding to be well-grounded, the evidence must include transfer data. Not necessarily a lot — but some.

How often does EdTech efficacy research include transfer testing? Rarely. Cognitive Tutor's evaluations, some of the more rigorous in the field, have occasionally included transfer measures, and the results have been instructive — effects on transfer items are typically smaller, sometimes considerably smaller, than effects on near-transfer or identical-to-training items. The pattern is not surprising to anyone working in the transfer literature. It is also almost never reported in the efficacy claims that circulate beyond the specialist literature.

Consider *mastery*. Bloom's own construct, as articulated in his mastery-learning work, involves not just performance on trained items but structural reorganization of knowledge — the kind of reorganization that allows a learner to solve problems the instruction did not specifically address. The mastery-learning tradition, which Bloom himself developed, included methodological requirements: criterion-referenced assessment against specific learning objectives, formative assessment with corrective feedback, demonstrated performance across multiple item types. Bloom's 2-sigma finding emerged from studies that implemented these methodological requirements. The 2-sigma number is cited routinely as a benchmark for tutoring effectiveness. Bloom's construct of mastery, including its methodological requirements, is cited far less often.

Consider *personalization* in the adaptive-learning tradition, as examined in Essai 8. The term invokes a construct — instruction responsive to the individual learner's state, needs, and trajectory — that has deep roots in differentiated-instruction research, in Vygotskian zone-of-proximal-development work, and in the aptitude-treatment interaction literature of the 1970s. What adaptive-learning systems typically operationalize is item sequencing and pacing based on item-level response patterns. These are not the same construct. An adaptive-learning system that delivers one item rather than another based on estimated item-level mastery is doing something technically specific; it is not, in the sense the construct implies, responding to the individual learner in a way that differs from what a competent human teacher with a rich model of the student does intuitively.

And consider *engagement*. The construct, as articulated in the psychological literature, involves attention, motivation, affect, persistence in the face of difficulty, and meaningful cognitive investment in the material. It is richly theorized. What adaptive-learning and AI-tutor efficacy research typically measures is time on task, session counts, and completion rates. These are not engagement in the construct sense. They are behavioral proxies that, under specific conditions, may correlate with engagement — and under other conditions, do not. A student can accumulate time on task while mentally elsewhere. A student can complete sessions without having engaged with the material in any meaningful cognitive sense. The apparatus does not distinguish.

This could go on. Mastery. Understanding. Learning. Personalization. Engagement. Each of these constructs has serious theoretical articulation in one or more research traditions. Each is routinely invoked by the learning-systems field's claim-making vocabulary. Each is routinely operationalized, in efficacy research, as aligned-item performance at short timescale. The gap between the construct and the operationalization is not the researchers' invention; it is what the apparatus produces. But it is also not small.

It is, taken across the field, the difference between the learning the vocabulary claims and the performance the measurements index.

## What the field has tried

It would be inaccurate to say the field has not tried to close this gap. It has tried, across multiple traditions, for decades. That these attempts have not produced a different default apparatus is itself instructive.

Consider transfer testing. Thorndike's early twentieth-century work established both that transfer occurs and that it is more limited than educators typically assume. The implication — that evidence for learning ought to include evidence for transfer — has been periodically advocated across the subsequent century. John Bransford, Ann Brown, and Rodney Cocking made it a central theme of *How People Learn*, the 1999 National Academies synthesis that remains the most widely cited overview of contemporary learning science. The book spent substantial space on transfer, including a chapter organized entirely around the question of what conditions produce transfer and what evidence we have that particular instructional methods do so. The methodological implication was straightforward: efficacy research should include transfer measures if it wants to make claims about learning rather than claims about trained performance.

Two and a half decades after *How People Learn*, transfer testing remains exceptional in EdTech efficacy research. The occasional study includes it. Most do not. The reasons have been examined in the methodological literature: transfer testing is harder to design, more expensive to administer, produces smaller and noisier effects than near-transfer testing, and offers fewer publishable positive findings per research dollar. Researchers who want to include transfer measures in their studies face the normal obstacles of any research practice that is more demanding than the prevailing default.

Consider durability testing. Bjork's work on storage versus retrieval strength, spanning the early 1990s to the present, has produced a methodology for distinguishing immediate performance from durable learning: test at immediate post, then test again at delay. If the effect persists, it is more likely to reflect storage strength; if it decays rapidly, it likely reflects retrieval strength at the moment of immediate test. The methodology is neither novel nor arcane. It is advocated in essentially every serious treatment of educational evaluation published in the last thirty years.

Three-month, six-month, and twelve-month follow-up testing in EdTech efficacy research remains exceptional. Occasional studies implement it. Most do not. The obstacles are structural: follow-up testing requires continued access to the study population, which typically becomes impossible after the course or academic year ends. It requires resources across a longer timeline than the typical grant cycle supports. It produces smaller effects, because learning decays, and smaller effects reduce publishability. The pattern is the same as with transfer testing: the methodology exists, its value is widely recognized, its implementation remains rare because the incentive structure rewards the alternatives.

Consider multi-paradigm convergence. The psychometric literature on construct validation, stretching back to Cronbach and Meehl's 1955 paper and developed substantially in Samuel Messick's 1989 chapter on validity, has articulated the principle that evidence for a construct is strengthened by convergence across multiple different methods of measurement. A claim about learning is more credible if it is supported by performance measures, behavioral observations, self-report, and transfer data, with the convergence itself constituting evidence that the construct is being indexed. The principle is the foundation of validity theory in educational and psychological measurement. Its methodological implications are straightforward: efficacy claims about rich constructs should be supported by multiple outcome measures that triangulate rather than by a single measure used in isolation.

Multi-paradigm convergence in EdTech efficacy research is rare. Single-outcome studies predominate. When a researcher-designed outcome measure shows a strong effect and a standardized measure shows a weak one on the same intervention, the strong effect tends to be cited and the weak effect tends to be absent from summary reporting. The divergence, which in a construct-validation framework would be informative about what the intervention actually does, tends to be treated as a discrepancy to be resolved in favor of the measure most favorable to the intervention. This is not fraud. It is a specific selective-reporting pattern that the apparatus produces.

Consider situated assessment. Jean Lave's *Cognition in Practice* (1988), Lave and Wenger's *Situated Learning* (1991), and the broader situated-cognition tradition articulated a different framework for assessment entirely: not what a learner can reproduce on an abstracted test, but how a learner participates in practices where what has been learned applies. Grocery shoppers who struggle with abstracted fraction problems perform fluently with fractions in the shopping context. The learning is not in the individual's head in an abstracted form; it is in the practice, enacted through the learner's participation. Assessment that captures this kind of learning requires observation of practice, not administration of tests.

The situated-cognition tradition has been influential in specific corners of learning sciences. It has had essentially no impact on deployed-product efficacy research. The methodological requirements — observational data, ethnographic care, participation in the practices where learning applies — are far more demanding than randomized-controlled-trial designs with aligned post-tests, and the RCT template is what the field has institutionalized as its gold standard.

Each of these traditions — transfer testing, durability testing, multi-paradigm convergence, situated assessment — has existed for decades. Each has produced methodology that could be adopted. Each would, if routinely adopted, substantially narrow the gap between measurement and construct in learning-systems efficacy research. Each remains exceptional rather than routine. The question is why.

## Why the apparatus persists

It is tempting to answer this question with some version of intellectual indictment. Researchers are lazy. The field is captured. Publication is broken. Peer review has failed. Each of these diagnoses has been offered, in various forms, by various critics, and each is partially correct and partially a distraction. The critical literature in this area is not small; readers who want the long form can consult any number of treatments. The diagnoses I want to propose here is different. It is also, I think, more accurate.

The apparatus persists because it serves the specific production conditions of the field in which it operates.

Consider what a researcher needs in order to do research in this field. They need funding, which comes on grant cycles of two to five years. They need publications, which come through peer-reviewed journals with specific conventions about what counts as a study. They need students or teams to conduct the work, which requires stable institutional support. They need access to populations — schools, classrooms, platforms — which requires institutional partnerships that have their own timelines and constraints. They need findings that other researchers can cite, which requires the findings to fit the conventions subsequent researchers are working within.

Now consider what a more adequate apparatus would require. Transfer testing adds design complexity and reduces effect sizes. Durability testing extends the study timeline past the typical grant cycle. Multi-paradigm convergence requires methodological range that most research programs, optimized around a single method, do not possess. Pre-registration of analytic plans constrains the exploratory-analysis moves that often produce publishable findings. Cost-inclusive reporting introduces variables the research design cannot control and that may produce unfavorable comparisons.

Each of these would, if adopted as a default, reduce the rate at which researchers produce citable positive findings. Not because the interventions do not work — some of them do — but because the findings that survive the more demanding methodology would be smaller, noisier, and less rhetorically useful. A researcher who adopts the more demanding methodology is competing with researchers who do not. The less-demanding researcher's findings will be larger, cleaner, and more citable. The grant agencies, tenure committees, and publication venues all reward the latter. This is not a secret. Everyone inside the apparatus knows it.

The same pressures operate on the institutions that surround the research. Product vendors who want to make efficacy claims have commercial reasons to prefer methodologies that produce larger numbers. Policy bodies that want to recommend interventions have political reasons to prefer evidence that looks clean. Philanthropists who fund efficacy research want defensible findings that can survive advocacy scrutiny, and clean findings are easier to defend than nuanced ones. The journal editors who publish in this space respond, reasonably, to what their referees will accept, and what referees will accept is shaped by the conventions the field has institutionalized.

No individual in this system is behaving cynically. Researchers are doing their best work under the constraints of their funding. Product vendors are reporting findings that pass peer review. Policy bodies are making recommendations based on available evidence. Philanthropists are funding work that looks defensible. The apparatus is not what anyone chose; it is what the incentives produce when rational actors operate within them.

This is why advocacy for better methodology has not produced better methodology. The problem is not that researchers do not know better methodology exists — they do. The problem is that operating under the existing apparatus produces careers; operating against it produces, for most researchers, shorter and more difficult careers. A young researcher who wants to implement the full Messick-validity framework for every efficacy claim is competing with researchers who do not. The young researcher will produce fewer papers, smaller effects, and less-citable findings. They will also, in most cases, not get tenure.

The apparatus persists, in other words, because it is an equilibrium. Equilibria are stable not because the actors inside them are irrational but because the actors inside them are responding rationally to incentives that no single actor created and no single actor can change. Changing an equilibrium of this kind requires changing the incentives — which requires coordination across grant agencies, tenure systems, journal conventions, institutional practices, and funder expectations. Such coordination is rare. It has happened, occasionally, in specific subfields when structural pressures aligned. It has not happened in learning-systems research, and none of the pressures currently aligned would produce it.

Naming the apparatus as an equilibrium is the observation this essai is offering. It is a structural observation, not a moral one. It is also, I want to suggest, the correct diagnosis. Researchers in this field are not broken. The evidence base in this field is not broken in the sense of being produced by broken people. The evidence base is what the apparatus produces when careful, rigorous, well-meaning researchers operate under the conventions the apparatus enforces. Improving any individual researcher's methods would not change what the field's evidence base looks like, because the evidence base is the aggregate output of many careful researchers responding to shared incentives.

What the book has been doing, across twelve essais and now this one, is making that apparatus visible. When the apparatus is visible, it can be examined. When it can be examined, readers of efficacy claims — the book's reader — can ask better questions of those claims than the apparatus itself asks. That shift, from reading claims produced by a taken-for-granted apparatus to reading claims with the apparatus visible, is the book's principal deliverable.

It does not resolve the apparatus. It does not provide the reader with a method for producing better evidence themselves. It does not propose a reform program for the field. It gives the reader the one thing that precedes all of those possibilities: the recognition that the apparatus is an apparatus, with specific features, producing specific outputs, subject to examination. What any reader does with that recognition, after closing the book, is their work and their choice.

## What more adequate evidence would require

I want to resist, in this section, the reformer's voice. This book's project, named in its opening, was not to tell the field what to do. It was to model an epistemic posture the field has largely lacked and to leave the reader with the habit of attention that posture requires. A book that ends by prescribing the field's path forward would have become, in its final movement, the very thing it began by declining to be.

But I also want to be specific, because vagueness here would be its own failure of discipline. The reader who has worked through twelve essais now holds the apparatus visibly enough to see what it does not produce. That view entails a reciprocal view: the shape of what better evidence would look like. I can describe that shape, provisionally, without prescribing that the field adopt it.

More adequate evidence about learning systems would begin with multiple outcome measures on the same intervention. Not as a choice of best measure, but as triangulation: developer-designed items, standardized instruments, performance-based tasks, and — where feasible — observational measures of practice, each reported openly regardless of whether they converge or diverge. Where they converge, the convergence is evidence for the construct the vocabulary invokes. Where they diverge, the divergence is itself informative about what the intervention does and does not do. The selective reporting that currently treats divergence as a discrepancy to be resolved would be replaced by routine reporting of the divergence as a finding.

It would include durability testing as baseline expectation rather than exceptional addition. A claim about *learning*, as the construct is articulated in the experimental-psychology tradition, requires evidence at delay. Three months is a common minimum; twelve months is better; decay patterns across intermediate intervals are informative. The methodology is not novel. It requires continued access to study populations and research timelines that extend past the typical academic year, which is harder than single-point-of-test studies, which is why it is rare. The rarity is not a statement about the methodology's value; it is a statement about the field's production conditions.

It would include transfer testing for any claim about understanding or mastery. Not identical-to-training items. Items that require the learner to apply what they have learned to problems that differ from the problems used in instruction, in ways the instruction did not specifically address. Transfer is difficult to achieve and therefore difficult to demonstrate. That is not an argument against measuring it. That is an argument for measuring it in order to see, specifically, how much of it the intervention produces.

It would include cost as a first-class variable, as Essai 12 examined. Cost-per-unit-outcome calculations, ingredients-method documentation, shadow pricing where necessary, comparison with alternative interventions at equivalent dollars per student. Not as an add-on business question but as a necessary condition for interpreting any comparative claim. A 0.3σ finding at $100 per student is not the same finding as a 0.3σ finding at $2,000 per student; they are different findings about different interventions, and treating them as comparable without cost information is the move the apparatus currently performs routinely.

It would include pre-registration of analytic plans. Not as a bureaucratic requirement but as a methodological commitment: the questions the study is asking, the outcome measures it will report, the analytic strategies it will pursue, all specified before the data are collected. Deviations from the pre-registered plan would be reported as deviations. The selective-reporting moves that currently produce a specific distribution of findings — strong effects on researcher-designed measures, weak or unreported effects on standardized measures — would become visible as selective rather than invisible.

It would include population diversity beyond the initial research setting. Findings from selective Harvard undergraduates are findings about selective Harvard undergraduates. They may generalize; they also may not. The generalization is an empirical question, and the empirical answer requires studies in different populations before the generalization is asserted rather than assumed.

Each of these requirements has been advocated for, across decades, in the specific research traditions where it originated. None of them is novel. The list above is not my list. It is the aggregate of what serious methodologists across transfer-testing, durability-testing, construct-validation, cost-effectiveness, and meta-science traditions have been advocating since well before most readers of this book were born. What I am doing here is gathering what the apparatus does not currently produce and describing its shape as a set, so that the reader can see the set as a set.

What would happen, as a practical matter, if the field adopted this set as routine? Efficacy findings would become smaller. Many findings that currently circulate would not survive the more demanding methodology. The rate at which the field produces citable positive findings would drop substantially. The rhetorical register of the field's claim-making would change, because the claims the current apparatus produces would no longer be supportable and the claims the more adequate apparatus produces would be more modest, more conditional, and less useful for advocacy, funding, and product marketing.

This is the answer, I think, to why the more adequate apparatus has not taken. The more adequate apparatus would produce different findings — not necessarily worse findings, but findings that serve the field's current production conditions less well. Whether the field should nonetheless adopt it is not my question to answer. It is the field's question. What I am doing, in naming the shape of what better evidence would require, is completing the view the book has been building toward. The reader now sees both what the current apparatus produces and what a more adequate apparatus would produce differently. What the reader does with that view is not the book's concern.

## The reader, integrated

What remains to say is brief.

The reader has worked through twelve close readings and is now at the end of the thirteenth. What they hold, if the essais have done their work, is not a method. It is not a checklist. It is not a framework they can apply mechanically. It is a habit of attention: the reflex to ask, when an efficacy claim arrives, what the claim's measurement is, what construct the claim asserts the measurement indexes, and whether the relationship between the measurement and the construct has been established or assumed. That reflex, developed through the cumulative work of the previous essais, is what the book has delivered.

The reflex generalizes beyond the specific cases the book examined. A learning-systems claim the book never read can be read, by the reader, using the same moves. A technology that does not yet exist, when its efficacy is claimed, can be read the same way. A research field other than educational technology, when it makes similar kinds of claims, can be read similarly. The moves are portable. That was the point of the spiral: not to produce a survey of specific claims but to develop, through varied application, a habit that transfers.

What the reader can also now see is the apparatus itself, as apparatus. Before the book, the field's evidence base appeared as a neutral background — the thing research produces, the thing claims are based on, the thing against which new claims are compared. After the book, that evidence base is visible as a specific equilibrium of measurement practices, production conditions, and institutional incentives. The same findings look different when seen in this light. Bloom's 2-sigma, read with the apparatus visible, is a specific finding under specific conditions serving specific citation functions in a specific field. Kestin's physics tutor, read with the apparatus visible, is another specific finding in the same tradition. Neither is false. Both are what they are, more precisely than the vocabulary around them typically allows.

The book's argument, such as it is, is that this is how efficacy claims in this field ought to be read. Not as either true or false, not as either endorsements or debunkings, but as specific findings with specific conditions producing specific relationships to the constructs the field claims to care about. The habit of reading them this way is not a skill the reader will apply effortfully. It is, I hope, a disposition the reader now has — available, operational, unavoidable.

What remains is the closing essai, which does not extend this examination. It releases the reader to it.
