# /c1 — Wave 4 (Essais 12 and 14)

Two essais here. Essai 13 — *On What We Measure and What We Care About*, the book's deepest and heaviest — gets its own standalone specification in the next artifact.

The structural work of Part IV is different from Parts II and III. Parts II and III applied the apparatus to specific kinds of material — inherited numbers, current technologies, rhetorical patterns. Part IV climbs up a level and applies the apparatus to structural features that cut across every essai that came before. *Cost in the Denominator* examines the variable that was quietly absent from almost every efficacy claim the book has read. *Closing* releases the reader, returning to *Que sais-je?*, after Essai 13 has delivered the book's deepest analytical move.

---

## Essai 12 — *On Cost in the Denominator*

### 1. Number, working title, one-line descriptor

**Essai 12. *On Cost in the Denominator*.** The reader comes to treat cost as an epistemic variable rather than a business concern — recognizing that efficacy claims compared without reference to cost are usually not comparable claims at all — and can re-read any claim in the book's earlier essais with cost as a first-class analytical question.

### 2. Keyword audit + title justification

Passes all three tests.

*Justification:* *In the Denominator* is the specific move the essai teaches. The phrase names cost's epistemic location — not as a separate question from efficacy but as the denominator of any efficacy comparison that purports to be generalizable. The title is slightly technical, which suits the essai: this is the essai where the book becomes structurally rigorous about a question the field has treated as optional.

### 3. The problem this essai solves for the reader

Across eleven essais, the reader has watched the apparatus applied to specific claims. Cost has appeared in nearly every one — Bloom's $2,000-per-pupil-year tutoring, adaptive-learning platforms at $10–50 per student, Cognitive Tutor's implementation costs, AI tutors at essentially zero marginal cost — but almost always as aside or footnote rather than as first-class analytical category. The reader may have noticed cost in passing. They probably have not held it as an epistemic variable.

The essai's job is to make the reader unable to read any efficacy claim again without asking about cost. Not *how much does this thing cost* as a budget question, but *what does this efficacy number mean when it is achieved at this cost, and is this number comparable to that number when the costs differ*. Once the reader has this move, they cannot read Bloom's 2-sigma the same way. They cannot read Kestin's physics-tutor finding the same way. They cannot read VanLehn's ITS meta the same way. The cost question, once installed, retroactively changes every essai that came before.

The essai is also the place where the book is most in dialog with a body of work — cost-effectiveness analysis in education, the Henry Levin tradition, more recent work by Alan Kraft and others — that the reader probably does not know and probably should. This essai can introduce that tradition briefly without turning into a cost-effectiveness primer.

### 4. Posture shifts (from `/l1`)

1. **Articulate:** Treats cost as part of the evidence claim, not as a separate business question — and can explain why this is an epistemic distinction, not a business one.
2. **Disassemble:** Can perform cost-in-the-denominator analysis on efficacy claims they encounter, and identifies when a claim is comparable to another only if cost is held constant.
3. **Revise:** Recognizes that the field's habit of separating cost from efficacy is itself an epistemic move — one that makes incomparable claims appear comparable — and can articulate provisionally what adequate cost-inclusive evidence reporting would require.
4. **Articulate:** Can articulate why Bloom's 2-sigma at roughly $2,000 per pupil-year is not directly comparable to a 0.2σ finding for a tool costing $20 per pupil-year as evidence for what to scale.

### 5. Opening strategy

Opens by **returning to a specific claim the reader has already read**, now asking a new question of it. The mitigation from `/l2` required Essai 12 to open on a live claim the reader remembers, so the deeper material of Part IV reconnects to the specific texture that drew them through Part III. Best candidate: **the Kestin physics-tutor finding from Essai 10**, re-examined under cost analysis.

*"The Harvard physics tutor we read in the previous part produced, under controlled conditions, learning gains substantially larger than active-learning classroom instruction. Taken as an efficacy claim, the finding was carefully examined — the outcome measure, the baseline, the population, the timescale. One question was barely raised. What did the intervention cost, and against what?"* Brief pause on this omission. Note that the omission is not specific to Kestin — the Kestin paper's cost reporting is actually better than most — but that cost has appeared as afterthought across every essai in the book. Name the essai's question: *what would it mean to take cost seriously as part of the evidence, from the beginning?*

This opening also satisfies the Revise-move setup: the reader is being asked to re-read something they already read, with a new question they did not have the first time. The spiral is doing its work.

### 6. Core movement blocks

**Block A — Cost as afterthought (3–4 pages).** Opens on Kestin. Walks through a brief audit: across the essais, where did cost appear and where did it not? Bloom's $2,000-per-pupil-year mentioned in Essai 4 but not foregrounded. ITS implementation costs in Essai 7 largely absent. Adaptive-learning platforms at $10–50 per student in Essai 8 mentioned without comparison. AI-tutor near-zero marginal costs in Essai 10 mentioned without being tied to the efficacy comparison. The essai names what the absence of cost does — it allows every efficacy claim to be read as if the number represented an intervention independent of its resource footprint, which is exactly what it does not represent. Takes the absence seriously: the field's researchers are generally not avoiding cost maliciously; they are working within disciplinary conventions that treat cost as outside the scope of efficacy research. The essai's target is the convention.

**Block B — Cost-effectiveness as an existing tradition (4–5 pages).** Brief introduction to the cost-effectiveness-analysis tradition in education research. Henry Levin's decades of work on cost-effectiveness methodology for educational interventions — published in the 1970s, updated across the 80s and 90s, now standard in a specific subfield of policy research. What Levin's approach requires: ingredients method (enumerate all resources), shadow pricing where necessary, per-unit-outcome calculation, cost-inclusive comparisons. What it makes visible: the intervention that produces 0.3σ at $100 per pupil may be more scalable than the intervention that produces 0.5σ at $2,000 per pupil, depending on the policy question. The essai is respectful and brief here — it is not a primer on cost-effectiveness methodology. It is establishing that the methodology exists, that it has existed for decades, and that its general absence from EdTech efficacy research is therefore a choice, not a limitation of what is possible.

**Block C — What Bloom costs, and what that means (4–5 pages).** Returns to Bloom's 2-sigma under cost analysis. The original Bloom tutoring condition required roughly one adult tutor per one-to-three children for substantial instructional time. In 2026 dollars, this works out to something like $2,000–$3,000 per pupil per year for the tutoring component alone. The field cites Bloom's 2-sigma as a benchmark without reference to this cost — *"we want to close the two-sigma gap"* — implying that the number is the thing to scale. But the number is inseparable from its cost condition. At $2,000 per pupil, you can produce 2-sigma gains with human tutors. At $20 per pupil, you cannot — not because $20-per-pupil interventions are inadequate, but because $20-per-pupil interventions are a different intervention, measured under different conditions, producing different numbers. The essai sits with this. It is not a claim that $20-per-pupil interventions are worthless; it is a claim that comparing them to Bloom as if they were alternatives on the same axis is a category error. The Revise move: cost-in-the-denominator, applied carefully, reveals that many of the field's most-cited benchmark comparisons are not comparisons at all.

**Block D — Cost reveals what else (3–4 pages).** Applying cost-in-the-denominator analysis to the essais already read produces a further observation: the claims that travel most widely in the field are usually the ones with no cost denominator. Bloom's 2-sigma travels without cost. Kestin's finding travels without cost. Khanmigo's claims travel without cost. The pattern is not an accident — *a claim without a denominator is easier to move*. A claim with a denominator has to be carried with its denominator, which makes it harder to cite, harder to compare, harder to deploy as a benchmark. The apparatus rewards denominator-free numbers. The essai names this as part of the apparatus of claim-making the book has been examining — not as a separate problem but as the same problem, seen from a new angle. Cost is not a topic the book ignored. Cost was the variable the apparatus systematically suppressed.

**Block E — What the reader can now ask (2–3 pages).** Given any efficacy claim, the portable question: *what does this number mean, and can I compare it to that number, without holding cost constant?* Given any scaling claim — *we should scale what works* — the portable question: *what does "works" mean at what cost, and at scaling cost?* Given any benchmark invocation — *this tool approaches human tutoring* — the portable question: *at what cost ratio?* The reader does not need to become a cost-effectiveness analyst. They need to treat cost as a first-class analytical variable in the disassembly move. Bridge to Essai 13: if cost was the suppressed variable the apparatus systematically omitted, measure-vs-construct is the deeper suppression underneath it. Cost is one proxy relationship. Measure-vs-construct is the general case.

### 7. Anchor material

- **Primary anchor (as reopened case):** The Kestin physics-tutor finding from Essai 10, re-examined under cost.
- **Primary anchor (as benchmark case):** Bloom 1984, re-examined under cost with specific reference to the tutoring-cost implications acknowledged in the original paper.
- **Primary anchor (methodology):** Henry M. Levin, *Cost-Effectiveness Analysis: Methods and Applications* (Sage, multiple editions 1983–2017). Author to select the most accessible edition. Brief treatment only; the essai is not a methodology text.
- **Secondary anchors:** brief re-examination of Khanmigo cost/efficacy relationship (using the Chalkbeat 2026 material and the Microsoft-subsidy framing from Meyer's April 2026 piece); brief re-examination of the adaptive-learning era's cost range.
- **Tertiary anchors:** the Alan Kraft education-research literature on effect-size interpretation that touches on cost; the What Works Clearinghouse's methodology for cost reporting (which is typically absent or minimal).
- **References to hold:** broader cost-benefit analysis literature in economics; Tessa Bold and related recent work on cost-effective education interventions in development contexts. One-sentence mentions.

**Anchor discipline:** The essai does not indict researchers for failing to include cost. It names cost omission as a disciplinary convention, names Levin's tradition as the alternative that has existed for decades, and uses the reopened Kestin and Bloom cases to show what becomes visible when cost is included. The move is methodological, not moral.

### 8. Essai closing / bridge to Essai 13

Closes on the deeper connection. *"Cost turned out not to be a topic the book ignored. Cost was the variable the apparatus systematically suppressed — and suppressing it is part of how the apparatus works. That observation applies more broadly. Cost is one kind of thing the apparatus does not index. The thing it most fundamentally does not index is the distance between what is measured and what the field claims learning is about. The next essai is about that distance."* Direct bridge to Essai 13.

### 9. Contributor notes

- **Do not become a cost-effectiveness primer.** Block B is essential but bounded — three or four pages establishing that the tradition exists and what it requires. A draft that turns into a Levin explainer has missed the essai's purpose. The essai is about the epistemics of cost omission, not about cost-effectiveness methodology per se.
- **The Bloom re-examination is the essai's hinge.** Block C's move — that Bloom at $2,000 and AI tutors at $20 are not claims on the same axis — is the single most important payoff in the essai. Get it clean. The reader must finish Block C unable to cite Bloom as a benchmark the same way they did at the start of the book.
- **Cost is not the subject; the apparatus is.** The essai is easy to read as *the book about how EdTech is overpriced or undersold.* It is not that book. The essai is about cost as an epistemic variable and about what the field's habit of suppressing it does. Watch for drift into budget commentary.
- **Do not indict Kestin, Bloom, or anyone else.** The essai reopens these cases to show something about the apparatus, not to pile on. Kestin's paper reports cost better than most; Bloom acknowledged cost implications in the original 1984 paper. The apparatus suppresses these acknowledgments in circulation, not always in publication.
- **The Revise move in Block E is short and portable.** *Ask about cost* is the take-away, operationalized. No more than three pages; the reader has done enough work, and this is about giving them the one additional analytical category to carry.
- **Connect to the pattern, not to the numbers.** By the end of Essai 12, the book is revealing the apparatus as a system of systematic suppressions — of durability, of transfer, of cost, and in Essai 13, of construct. The essai's cumulative force comes from connecting these suppressions, not from dwelling on cost as if it were the one missing piece.

**Primary sources to have at hand:** Kestin 2024 paper; Bloom 1984 (specifically the cost acknowledgment sections, which are usually overlooked); Levin's *Cost-Effectiveness Analysis* (most accessible edition); the Chalkbeat 2026 Barnum interview for Khanmigo subsidy material; Meyer's April 2026 piece; a sampling of recent EdTech efficacy studies to confirm the cost-reporting-absent pattern.

### 10. Projected length

**22–25 pages.** The reopening moves (Kestin, Bloom) plus the Levin tradition plus the apparatus connection require space, but the essai's discipline is to stay focused on cost's epistemic role rather than on cost as a topic. 23 pages target.

## Essai 14 — *On What Examination Licenses*

### 1. Number, working title, one-line descriptor

**Essai 14. Closing — *On What Examination Licenses*.** The reader, at the end of the book's examination, is released with the habit of attention they have developed, without a summary, a verdict, or a prescription for the field.

### 2. Keyword audit + title justification

Passes all three tests.

*Justification:* *Licenses* is the key word. The essai names what the examination produces for the reader — not a conclusion, not a verdict, but a license: permission and capacity to continue the examination on their own. The word carries both senses: it licenses the reader (authorizes them) and names what examination as an activity produces (continued examination, not termination). The title connects back to Essai 1's *Que sais-je?* without restating it.

### 3. The problem this essai solves for the reader

The closing essai is the most form-specific in the book. A monograph closes with a synthesized argument; a textbook closes with a summary; a polemic closes with a call to action. The essai form, honestly executed, closes with a release. The reader has watched thirteen essais perform close reading on the field's evidentiary apparatus. They have developed — through observation, through the accretion of cases, through the spiral's cumulative work — a habit of attention. The closing essai's job is to name that habit without prescribing its use, return to the book's form (*Que sais-je?*) without lapsing into philosophy, and release the reader with the apparatus as theirs.

The temptations to resist are specific: the essai must not summarize what the book argued (the book did not argue in the monograph sense; summarizing would betray the form). It must not prescribe what the field should do (the book explicitly declined reform as a posture at `/i4`; prescribing would betray the thesis). It must not offer the reader a verdict on any specific product or researcher (the book's discipline across thirteen essais has been provisional commitment on findings, not verdicts on actors). It must not be long.

### 4. Posture shifts (from `/l1`)

1. **Integrate:** The reader understands that the examination the book performed does not conclude — it licenses further examination, by the reader, of claims the book did not cover.
2. **Articulate:** The reader can articulate what habits of attention they have now that they did not have at the start — not as a list of techniques, but as a shifted stance.
3. **Integrate:** The reader recognizes that the epistemic posture the book modeled is itself the principal deliverable — the form was the content, as Montaigne intended — and that what the reader does with it next is not the book's concern.

### 5. Opening strategy

Opens by **returning to the sentence the book began with**. The Khan TED statement from Essai 1 — *"probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen."* Quote it again, cleanly. Pause. Note, briefly, how differently the reader can now hold that sentence than they could when they first encountered it. They are not being asked to have a verdict on Khan Academy; they are being asked to notice what has changed in their own attention. The sentence has not changed. The reader has.

This return is the book's structural closure. The sentence that opened the examination is the sentence that closes it. The reader sees the full arc. What Montaigne did at the end of many essais — returning to the opening proposition after the examination had done its work — is what this essai does for the book. The return is not rhetorical. It is formal. The sentence is the same; the reading is different; the book is over.

### 6. Core movement blocks

**Block A — The sentence, returned (2–3 pages).** Opens on the Khan quote. Asks the reader to notice how they now hold it. What has changed — not about the sentence, but about the reader's attention. A reader who has worked through the book can now read that sentence and ask: *what measurement would have to be in place for this to be a claim rather than an invocation? what did the field's apparatus need to produce this sentence, and what did the apparatus omit that would have let the reader judge it?* These are not new questions. They are the questions the book has practiced. The reader's having them automatically, on a sentence they once read uncritically, is what the book did.

**Block B — The habit, named (3–4 pages).** The essai names what has been developed. Not a checklist, not a procedure, but a habit. The reader now has — available, not always active, but available — the reflex to ask about outcome measures, baselines, populations, timescales, costs, and the gap between what a measure indexes and what the claim asserts. The reflex emerges not because the book installed a framework but because the reader watched the reflex modeled thirteen times across varied material. The spiral produced the habit by repetition under variation. The essai names this carefully: the habit is not a method the reader has learned. It is a way of attending the reader has developed. Those are different things, and the difference matters.

**Block C — What the book did not do (2–3 pages).** Honest account. The book did not tell the reader whether specific products work. It did not produce a verdict on Khan Academy, on Kestin's physics tutor, on Cognitive Tutor, on Duolingo. It did not propose a replacement apparatus for the field. It did not prescribe what the field should do next. It did not synthesize the essais into a unified argument. These refusals are deliberate, and the essai names them as deliberate. Each refusal is an instance of the form's discipline: essais commit provisionally, specifically, but do not adjudicate on behalf of the reader. The reader does the adjudicating. The book provided the close readings; the reader does the rest.

**Block D — Que sais-je?, revisited (3–4 pages).** Returns to Montaigne's motto, now with the book's work behind it. *Que sais-je?* — what do I know? — is not skepticism. It is not relativism. It is discipline. The discipline that takes positions, holds them provisionally, and shows the reasoning, including where the reasoning revises itself. The essai connects this discipline to what the reader now holds. The reader does not know less than they did at the start of the book. They know more — about the apparatus, about specific claims, about the field's habits. But they know it in the form Montaigne called for: as provisional commitments, held carefully, specific about what they depend on. *Que sais-je?* is the motto of someone who has taken the examination seriously and refused to pretend certainty they have not earned. The essai does not belabor this. A few paragraphs connecting the discipline to what the reader now carries.

**Block E — The release (1–2 pages).** The closing move. The reader is released. The book ends. What the reader does with the examination now — whether they read the next efficacy claim differently, whether they push back in the next funding meeting, whether they recommend the book to a colleague — is not the book's concern. The apparatus the book modeled is now the reader's apparatus. The book returns to *Que sais-je?* one last time. One or two paragraphs, closing.

No bridge. No preview. The book ends here.

### 7. Anchor material

- **Primary anchor:** The Khan 2023 TED statement, returned from Essai 1. Quoted cleanly, without commentary, at the opening of the essai.
- **Primary anchor (formal):** Montaigne's *Que sais-je?* (Essais, Book II, *Apologie de Raymond Sebond*), briefly, in Block D. The motto is the book's structural frame, and Block D's treatment returns to it.
- **No other anchors.** The essai is short. It does not introduce new material. It returns to material the reader has already encountered, in a changed register.

**Anchor discipline:** The essai does not cite new studies, does not introduce new cases, does not engage new researchers. Its material is the book the reader has just read, re-encountered through the habit of attention the book developed. Adding new anchors here would betray the form.

### 8. Essai closing

The book ends. No bridge.

The final lines are the essai's most deliberate. They should be quiet, earned, specific. Something to the effect that the examination the reader has worked through is now theirs to continue on whatever claims they encounter next, under whatever form the apparatus next takes, without the book in their hand — which is what the book always intended. The motto holds. *Que sais-je?* remains the question.

The specific final sentence is craft work the author should hold until the rest of the book is drafted. A closing sentence written before the book exists will read as programmatic; a closing sentence written after the book is finished will read as earned.

### 9. Contributor notes

- **Length discipline.** This is the shortest essai in the book. Target 10–12 pages, ceiling 14. The reader has done enough work. A long closing is a betrayal. Every paragraph in this essai should be earning its place; any paragraph that restates, summarizes, or recapitulates previous essais is a paragraph to cut.
- **Do not summarize the book.** The most tempting failure mode. A draft that walks back through what each essai did is a draft that has not understood what the closing essai is for. The reader already read the book. The closing is not for the reader who has not read; it is for the reader who has.
- **Do not prescribe.** Closely related failure mode. The essai must not slip into *"what the field should do"* or *"how we could fix this."* The book's thesis at `/i4` committed to the Montaignian register: the book examines, it does not reform. A draft that gives the field a plan has drifted from form and thesis at the closing moment.
- **Do not issue a verdict.** The reader has worked thirteen essais under the discipline of provisional specific commitment. The closing essai must honor that discipline. No *"in the end, the field has failed"* or *"the AI moment will pass like every other moment."* Neither is what the book showed. The book showed the apparatus. The closing names what the reader now carries. That is all.
- **The Montaigne move must land without becoming a Montaigne primer.** Block D connects *Que sais-je?* to the reader's new habit in 3–4 pages, maximum. A reader who finishes Block D feeling they learned about Montaigne has read a draft that has drifted. The reader should finish Block D feeling that the discipline Montaigne named is what they themselves now practice.
- **The final sentence holds until last.** Write the rest of the essai first. Write the rest of the book first. Return to the final sentence at the end. It is the only sentence in the book whose craft-responsibility is entirely about closure, and it should be earned by everything that came before it.
- **Faux humility risk, one more time.** *Que sais-je?* can be read as skepticism or as relativism if the essai is not careful. Montaigne was neither. The essai must land the discipline point clearly: *Que sais-je?* means *I take positions; I hold them provisionally; I show my reasoning; I accept that I may revise.* It does not mean *I decline to commit.* The distinction is the difference between the form working and the form failing.

**Primary sources to have at hand:** Khan's 2023 TED statement (return use); Montaigne's *Apologie de Raymond Sebond* for *Que sais-je?* context; the author's own draft of Essai 1, for continuity of register and voice.

### 10. Projected length

**10–14 pages.** Shortest essai in the book. 12 pages target. A longer closing essai would betray the form; a shorter one would feel truncated. Plan for 12, accept 10, resist 15.

## Pattern adherence summary (for Essais 12 and 14)

Both essais follow the template with the expected variations:

- **Block counts.** Essai 12: 5. Essai 14: 5. Consistent with Parts II and III.
- **Anchor strategy.** Essai 12 uses the reopening move (returning to claims from earlier essais) which is appropriate for Part IV's structural role. Essai 14 uses no new anchors — returning to Essai 1's Khan quote and Montaigne's motto — which is appropriate for a closing.
- **Projected lengths.** Essai 12: 23 pages. Essai 14: 12 pages. The short closing is a formal choice.
- **Voice discipline.** No indictments; specific findings; inquiring frame; provisional commitment. Essai 14 specifically extends the discipline to refusing verdict at the closing.
- **Bridging.** Essai 12 bridges to Essai 13 (the deeper suppression). Essai 14 ends the book without bridge.
