# On Bloom's Two Sigma

*Essai 4*

---

In 1984, the educational psychologist Benjamin Bloom published a seventeen-page paper in *Educational Researcher* under the title *The 2 Sigma Problem: The Search for Methods of Group Instruction as Effective as One-to-One Tutoring*. The paper reported findings from two doctoral dissertations Bloom had supervised at the University of Chicago — Joanne Anania's in 1981 and Joseph Arthur Burke's in 1983 — and it advanced a specific claim. Students who received one-to-one tutoring combined with mastery learning, Bloom wrote, performed approximately two standard deviations above students in conventional classroom instruction on the same assessments. In the language that has since become dominant, the effect size was about 2.0 sigma. The average tutored student outperformed approximately 98 percent of the conventionally instructed students in the comparison group.

This finding has become, across the four decades since its publication, the single most-cited number in the learning-systems field. It is invoked in nearly every justification of tutoring as an educational intervention, in nearly every funding pitch for adaptive learning systems, in nearly every marketing claim that an AI-powered tool can "approach the effectiveness of one-on-one instruction." Sal Khan referenced it from the TED stage in 2023 as justification for building Khanmigo; he developed the framing further in his 2024 book *Brave New Words*. The Gates Foundation invokes it in its documentation of personalized-learning investments. The Chan Zuckerberg Initiative invokes it. McKinsey invokes it in consulting work for school districts. When someone working in this field says "we want to close the two-sigma gap," they are invoking this paper, whether they have read it or not.

I want to ask you to hold this number for a moment before this essai goes any further.

*Two standard deviations.* Nothing else in the published literature on educational interventions approaches it. The typical effect size reported in large-scale field-based randomized controlled trials of educational interventions is approximately 0.10 sigma — one-twentieth of the Bloom figure. The median effect reported across Kurt VanLehn's 2011 meta-analysis of intelligent tutoring systems was 0.76 sigma. The pooled effect from Nickow, Oreopoulos, and Quan's 2020 systematic review of ninety-six randomized tutoring evaluations was 0.37 sigma. No finding in the subsequent four decades of education research has reproduced Bloom's 2.0 sigma result at comparable magnitude. The 2-sigma number, as a measured finding, sits alone.

And yet it circulates as though it were a benchmark — a target, a standard that successful educational interventions might approach. An ordinary empirical observation, when repeated often enough without its conditions, becomes a law of nature in the reader's ear. The gap between what Bloom actually showed and how the 2-sigma figure now functions in the field's rhetorical economy is the subject of this essai.

I want to be clear about what this essai is not doing. It is not arguing that Bloom was wrong. It is not arguing that the finding was fabricated, inflated, or methodologically unsound. The 1984 paper is careful, internally consistent, and explicit about its conditions. Bloom himself was not making the kinds of claims that are made on his behalf today. The paper's title names a *problem* — the gap between what intensive tutoring produces and what conventional classroom instruction produces, and the cost barrier that prevented tutoring from being scaled as a universal intervention. Bloom was not claiming tutoring could be universally deployed; he was calling for research into group-instruction methods that might approach tutoring's effectiveness at scalable cost. What the 2-sigma figure has been asked to do in the forty years since would have surprised him.

The essai reads Bloom's paper carefully, walks through what the finding actually supports, examines what the replication record has shown across four decades, and then examines how the number has been deployed as a benchmark in contemporary AI-tutor advocacy. The reader's question at the end is not "was Bloom right?" — he was right about what he measured. The reader's question is "what kind of evidence is a 2-sigma figure from 1984, and what can that evidence support?"

---

Begin with the studies themselves.

The Anania and Burke dissertations that provided Bloom's data were specific experiments in specific conditions. The subjects were fourth, fifth, and eighth graders in American schools, studying probability in mathematics and cartography in social studies. These subject areas were chosen deliberately. Bloom and his students wanted to minimize the effect of prior learning on outcomes — topics that most students would have encountered before would have compressed the range of possible gains. Probability and cartography were relatively unfamiliar to the student populations tested, which allowed the experiments to measure the impact of instruction without prior-knowledge confounds.

Three instructional conditions were compared. Conventional instruction: roughly thirty students per teacher, traditional lecture-and-assessment format, summative assessment at the end of the unit. Mastery learning: also in groups of roughly thirty, but with formative assessment built in throughout the instructional sequence, and with "correctives" administered to students who did not initially demonstrate mastery before advancing to the next material. And one-to-one tutoring: students receiving individualized instruction from a tutor, at ratios of one-to-one, one-to-two, or one-to-three, with mastery-learning principles built into the tutoring interaction.

Each condition ran for approximately three weeks. Assessment was via researcher-designed tests aligned to the instructional content. The results Bloom reported in 1984 were as follows: mastery learning produced effects of roughly 1.0 sigma compared to conventional instruction; tutoring produced effects of roughly 2.0 sigma. The 2.0 sigma figure — the one that has become canonical — came from the combined effect of tutoring plus mastery learning, compared to conventional classroom instruction without either.

I want to pause on several features of this design that are rarely named in contemporary invocations.

First, the tutors in these experiments were not professional educators. They were undergraduates who received approximately one week of training before tutoring began. When current AI-tutor advocates invoke Bloom's "super tutor" as what they are building toward, they are invoking, as the original benchmark, undergraduate students with a week of training, working with small groups of children on unfamiliar material over a three-week window. This is not a criticism of the original design; Anania and Burke used the tutors they had. It is an observation that the "expert human tutor" who populates contemporary AI-tutor rhetoric is a construct the original experiments did not include.

Second, the tutoring condition was confounded with mastery learning by design. Students in the tutoring condition did not simply get one-to-one instruction; they got one-to-one instruction combined with the formative assessment, corrective feedback, and criterion-referenced advancement that characterize mastery learning as an instructional approach. The 2.0 sigma figure is the combined effect of both interventions. VanLehn and others have since argued that mastery learning itself — the 90-percent-threshold-for-advancing structure — may have been the primary driver of the gains, with tutoring serving more as the delivery mechanism than as the causal ingredient. Whether this argument is right is contested. What is not contested is that the 2.0 sigma came from both interventions operating together, not from tutoring alone.

Third, the assessments were researcher-designed tests aligned closely to what the instructional units had taught. This is, as the second essai of this volume established and as the fifth essai examined at length, a measurement choice with known effects on the magnitude of reported effect sizes. Proximal assessments — aligned to what the intervention specifically taught — produce systematically larger effect sizes than distal assessments that measure broader constructs. Bloom's 2.0 sigma was produced under the measurement conditions that maximize reported effects. This is not a criticism of the Anania and Burke studies; they measured what they were designed to measure. It is an observation about what Bloom's figure is a measurement of. It is a measurement of aligned-item performance after three weeks of instruction, on content the students had not previously encountered, under intensive instructional conditions. It is not, strictly, a measurement of "learning" as that construct is used in the field's vocabulary today.

Fourth, and perhaps most importantly, Bloom's 2-sigma finding was reported with an explicit cost acknowledgment. The paper's title is *The 2 Sigma Problem* for a reason. The problem, as Bloom framed it, was not that tutoring worked; the problem was that tutoring's effectiveness came at a cost structure that prevented its universal deployment. Bloom wrote that one-to-one tutoring was "too costly for most societies to bear on a large scale." His paper was a call — explicit, repeated, structurally foregrounded — for research into methods of *group* instruction that might approach tutoring's effectiveness without tutoring's cost. The 2-sigma figure, in the paper where it originates, is not offered as a benchmark to be pursued. It is offered as a gap to be closed through scalable methods that the paper explicitly acknowledges have not yet been developed.

Bloom, in 1984, was asking a question. The question was: can we achieve, through group-instruction methods that can actually be deployed, something close to what intensive tutoring produces? The question was genuine. He did not claim to know the answer. He did not claim the 2-sigma gap could be closed. He did not claim any specific technology or method would close it. He named the gap and asked the field to pursue research that might close it. That was the paper.

---

The replication record across the subsequent four decades is its own story, and one that the contemporary invocation of the 2-sigma figure rarely engages.

The earliest meta-analytic work on tutoring, conducted by Peter Cohen and colleagues in 1982 — actually published two years before Bloom's 2-sigma paper — found an average tutoring effect across the then-available studies of approximately 0.33 sigma. This was a respectable effect by the standards of education research, but a small fraction of what Bloom's figure would later describe.

By 2011, Kurt VanLehn conducted what remains one of the most methodologically careful meta-analyses of tutoring and intelligent tutoring systems. VanLehn aggregated effect sizes across human tutoring and across computer-based intelligent tutoring systems, and reported medians of 0.79 sigma for human tutoring and 0.76 sigma for intelligent tutoring systems. Both numbers are substantial by the standards of educational interventions. Both are less than half of Bloom's 2.0 sigma figure.

In 2020, Andre Nickow, Philip Oreopoulos, and Vincent Quan conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of ninety-six randomized tutoring evaluations. Their pooled effect size was 0.37 sigma — meaningfully smaller than VanLehn's 2011 figure, and less than one-fifth of Bloom's 2.0. The study found that professional teachers and paraprofessionals working as tutors produced stronger effects than nonprofessional volunteers, which complicates the story but does not rescue the magnitude. No condition in the Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan data approached 2 sigma.

I want to be careful here, because the replication story is easy to overstate in either direction. The honest claim is this: the 2-sigma magnitude Bloom reported has rarely been reproduced in subsequent meta-analytic work. Tutoring effects in the subsequent literature tend to cluster in the range of 0.3 to 0.8 sigma, with the higher end appearing in carefully controlled studies with specific populations and aligned measures, and the lower end appearing in large-scale field studies with heterogeneous populations and more distal measures. The 2-sigma figure sits well above this distribution. It is not that Bloom was wrong — the Anania and Burke studies measured what they measured — but that the specific conditions that produced 2 sigma have not, in four decades, been systematically reproduced.

What conditions would it take? A short instructional window, probably; prior unfamiliarity with the material, probably; a proximal assessment closely aligned to the instruction, almost certainly; a careful implementation of mastery learning alongside tutoring, probably. The question is whether these are the conditions that characterize educational interventions as they are typically deployed, or whether they are specific experimental conditions that optimize for the largest possible reported effect. The subsequent literature has generally not taken the position that these conditions are representative. When interventions are deployed at scale, over longer timescales, across heterogeneous populations, with more distal assessments, the effect sizes typically shrink. This is not an indictment of the interventions, and it is not an indictment of Bloom. It is a description of how effect sizes behave in this literature — a description the fifth essai of this volume examined at length.

---

There is a second dimension to the replication question that I want to name directly, because it is usually left implicit. Bloom's paper was explicit about cost. The 2-sigma figure was produced under conditions that are expensive to maintain. What did they cost, and what does a current equivalent cost?

Bloom's original tutoring condition required a one-to-one or one-to-three ratio between tutor and student, sustained over three weeks of instruction. In the contemporary United States, research-validated high-dosage tutoring programs provide a reasonable current cost benchmark. Saga Education, which has run large-scale tutoring programs in American high schools for over a decade and has been the subject of multiple rigorous evaluations, operates at a two-to-one student-to-tutor ratio, with tutors working with the same students every school day for a class period. Saga's cost per student per school year runs in the range of 3,500 to 4,800 dollars. MATCH Corps, a similar program, runs at comparable costs. The core human-resource requirement of these programs — trained tutors working with small numbers of students over sustained periods — is what drives the cost structure, and that structure is not substantially different from what Anania and Burke's studies required.

Now compare this to the cost structures of the educational technologies that invoke Bloom's 2-sigma as their benchmark. Khan Academy's institutional product, as the second essai of this volume noted, runs approximately ten dollars per student per year. Rori, the AI-powered tutor deployed in Ghana that the fifth essai discussed, runs at approximately five dollars per student per year. Duolingo's free tier is free; its paid tier is on the order of a hundred dollars per year. The AI-tutor systems currently being evaluated — the ones that invoke Bloom as the benchmark they are approaching — operate at cost structures two to three orders of magnitude below the tutoring programs that have actually been evaluated against Bloom-like benchmarks.

This is not a critique of low-cost interventions. A 0.36 sigma effect at five dollars per student is an extraordinary achievement on its own terms. What it is not, however, is a comparable finding to Bloom's 2-sigma at a cost structure two hundred times smaller. The interventions are not on the same axis. They are producing different magnitudes of effect at radically different cost structures, which means they are answering different questions and serving different policy roles. A ten-dollar platform that produces 0.26 sigma is an intervention at one part of the cost-effectiveness space. A four-thousand-dollar tutoring program that produces 0.37 sigma is at another. Bloom's original 2-sigma result lived in a research condition that, if it had to be sustained at scale with professional tutors, would cost something closer to the latter than to the former. The 2-sigma figure at scale, if it could be produced at all, would not be a cheap finding.

When Sal Khan, in his 2023 TED talk, invoked Bloom's 2-sigma as the benchmark Khanmigo was designed to approach, the implicit claim was that AI could produce Bloom's result at a cost structure that Bloom's tutors never achieved. This is, in principle, a reasonable aspiration. In practice, it is also a claim about a specific quantity — the cost-adjusted magnitude of the effect — that the evidence base does not support and that the contemporary AI-tutor literature, which I will examine in a later essai of this volume, has not delivered. A claim that AI can "close the 2-sigma gap" is not a claim that AI can produce 2 sigma; it is a claim that AI can produce Bloom-magnitude gains at cost structures two orders of magnitude below Bloom's. The subsequent Khanmigo evaluations — which I examine elsewhere — have produced effect sizes far smaller than 2 sigma, at cost structures that are admittedly much smaller than Bloom's, but without the claimed magnitude ever materializing.

---

There is one more dimension of Bloom's original finding that the contemporary invocation rarely engages, and I want to surface it before closing this essai.

Bloom's paper was not about building a technology to automate tutoring. It was about identifying the variables — the "alterable variables" in Bloom's vocabulary — that might be combined in group instruction to produce effects approaching what tutoring produces. His paper included a table of such variables, drawn from the research available in 1984, which has circulated widely in the intervening decades. Mastery learning produced effects of approximately 1.0 sigma. Reinforcement produced approximately 1.2. Feedback-corrective approaches produced approximately 1.0. Cues and explanations produced approximately 1.0. Student time on task produced approximately 1.0. Graded homework produced approximately 0.8. Cooperative learning produced approximately 0.8.

These numbers, like the 2-sigma figure itself, are artifacts of specific experimental conditions and do not generally replicate at magnitude in subsequent work. But what I want to point at is the character of the variables. They are all, in Bloom's original vision, instructional design variables that teachers — human teachers — would implement in classrooms. Bloom was not imagining a technology that would automate the teacher's role. He was imagining a humanized and intensified version of the classroom teacher's role. "The teacher," Bloom wrote explicitly in the 1984 paper, "is more important than ever." Technology, in Bloom's framing, was to be used to liberate teachers from the administrative burden of assessment and correction, so that they could focus their attention on "diagnosing misunderstanding" and "motivating and rewarding achievement."

This framing — technology as freeing the teacher to do more teacher-work, not as replacing the teacher — has been largely lost in the contemporary invocation of Bloom. When Sal Khan writes, in *Brave New Words*, that AI will allow every student to have "a personal tutor," he is invoking Bloom's 2-sigma finding but inverting Bloom's proposed solution. Bloom was not proposing more tutors. He was proposing better-supported teachers, with tools that would allow group instruction to approach tutoring's effectiveness. What has actually been built, in the name of Bloom's vision, is largely tools that attempt to bypass the teacher rather than to support her. Whether this is a better strategy than Bloom's — whether AI tutors can, in fact, produce outcomes that supported-teacher group instruction cannot — is an open empirical question. What it is not, however, is a fulfillment of Bloom's 1984 paper. It is a different project, borrowing Bloom's number while departing from his theory of what would close the gap.

---

Here is what the 2-sigma figure is evidence for, read carefully.

Fourth, fifth, and eighth graders studying unfamiliar material over three-week windows, in small groups or individually, under conditions combining one-to-one tutoring with mastery-learning principles, assessed on researcher-designed aligned tests, performed approximately two standard deviations above students in conventional classroom instruction on the same assessments. This is a real finding. It was produced under specific conditions. It has not, in four decades, been reliably reproduced at that magnitude when the conditions have differed substantially.

Here is what the 2-sigma figure is not evidence for.

It is not evidence that human tutoring, deployed at scale over the course of a school year to heterogeneous student populations, produces 2 sigma effects. The subsequent meta-analytic record shows effects around 0.4 to 0.8 sigma under such conditions, with substantial variance by tutor quality and intervention design.

It is not evidence that educational technologies can approach 2 sigma effects, whether or not they are more cost-effective than human tutoring. The subsequent AI-tutor literature has produced effects in the range of 0.17 to 0.73 sigma, depending on conditions, and the higher end appears only under the specific measurement regimes that systematically produce larger reported effects.

It is not evidence that any specific intervention — human or technological — would produce 2 sigma if only it were implemented with sufficient fidelity. The conditions that produced 2 sigma in Anania and Burke's original studies are not conditions that any known scalable intervention reliably reproduces.

It is not evidence that "the 2-sigma gap" between tutoring and classroom instruction is a fixed quantity waiting to be closed by better technology. It is evidence that, in one specific experimental setting four decades ago, a 2-sigma gap was observed between two specific instructional conditions. What is happening when the field invokes "the 2-sigma gap" as though it were a standing feature of the educational landscape is something other than citation of Bloom's finding. It is the deployment of a number that has acquired rhetorical life independent of what it originally measured.

---

I want to close by naming what I hope this essai has done and what it has not.

What I hope it has done is rescue Bloom from the misuse his finding has been subject to. The 1984 paper is a careful piece of work. Bloom was not making the claims that are now made in his name. He acknowledged the cost barrier explicitly; he framed his result as identifying a problem rather than as advertising a solution; he emphasized the teacher's role rather than imagining the teacher's replacement; he called for research into scalable methods rather than claiming that any specific method would close the gap. What Bloom did is not what the field has done in the decades since. The field has taken a number from his paper and attached it to projects that do not resemble the research his paper was calling for.

Rescuing Bloom does not mean defending the number as a contemporary benchmark. The number does not generalize in the ways the contemporary invocation requires. The apparatus built by the second essai of this volume — the five questions about outcome measure, baseline, population, timescale, and cost — applied to the 2-sigma finding produces a much more modest and specific claim than the one that circulates. The finding is a finding about specific conditions. Removed from those conditions, it becomes a shibboleth — a word used to signal membership in a discourse, not a measurement of anything current.

What I hope the essai has not done is treat Bloom as a villain or his 1984 paper as a methodological error. Neither characterization would be accurate or useful. Bloom did careful work on an important question. His paper's framing has been distorted by subsequent citation in ways he could not have anticipated and would probably not have endorsed. The distortion is not Bloom's responsibility. It is the responsibility of the apparatus that has circulated his number, and that is what the rest of this volume continues to examine.

The next essai examines what happens when the number changes from a single finding to an aggregated one. If Bloom's 2-sigma suffers from the specific conditions of its production — conditions that are invisible when the number is cited — what happens when many such findings are combined into a meta-analytic summary? What does the aggregation show, and what does it conceal? That is the question Essai 6 takes up.

---

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai has tried to walk a careful line, and I want to name explicitly where I am unsure about whether the line is in the right place.

I am not sure I have been careful enough about the distinction between "the 2-sigma finding in Bloom's paper" and "the 2-sigma number in contemporary discourse." These are not the same object. The first is a specific result from specific experiments. The second is a rhetorical benchmark with a life partly independent of the paper it originated in. The essai tries to address both, but a reader might fairly say that the first is given more charitable treatment than the second. If the essai has drifted, in its treatment of the contemporary invocations, into a kind of unearned certainty about what invoking the number is *really* doing, that would be a drift worth naming. I am counting on readers who disagree with specific characterizations of contemporary invocations — Khan's, the Gates Foundation's, the general rhetorical field — to tell me where the essai has been unfair.

I am not sure I have given enough attention to the argument that the 2-sigma finding, even if not literally reproducible, might still serve as a useful aspirational benchmark. There is a version of this argument that says: even if no actual intervention reaches 2 sigma, the benchmark motivates research, funding, and ambition in ways that produce better interventions than would exist without it. This argument is not trivial. It is essentially the argument that aspirational targets matter even when they cannot be achieved. The essai treats this argument dismissively, by implication, by framing the rhetorical life of the number as distortion. A reader who thinks the aspirational benchmark has been useful — that 0.36 sigma would not have been achieved if the field had only aspired to 0.36 sigma — has a fair case to make. I have not made that case's strongest version here.

I am not sure about the balance of the replication-record discussion. I have presented the subsequent meta-analytic record as showing that tutoring effects cluster in the 0.3-to-0.8 range, well below Bloom's 2.0 figure. This is accurate as a description of central tendency. But the range has variance, and there are studies in the literature that have found larger effects, and there are conditions under which larger effects reliably appear. A reader who works in the tutoring-research literature might reasonably say that the essai's summary undersells the evidence that well-designed, well-implemented tutoring can produce effects above 1.0 sigma in favorable conditions. I have chosen to emphasize the central tendency because the central tendency is what the contemporary citation apparatus most obscures. But the emphasis is a choice, not a neutral summary.

Finally, I am not sure the cost-structure argument in Block D lands with the right weight. The claim that Khanmigo at ten dollars and Bloom-style tutoring at four thousand dollars are not on the same axis is, I think, correct. But it can read as dismissive of low-cost interventions, which is not the intent. The intent is that cost-matched comparisons require cost-matched claims, and that the invocation of Bloom's number in the service of ten-dollar platforms is a comparison without a matched cost structure. A reader who finds the argument more critical than it should be — who finds the treatment of Khanmigo's cost structure as part of the critique when it should be part of the product's credit — should tell me.

The next essai examines meta-analyses as a form. If the 2-sigma figure suffers from conditions-obscured-by-citation, what happens when we aggregate hundreds of such conditions-obscured-by-citation findings into a meta-analytic summary? That question is what follows. I hope you will continue with me.

## Notes on the draft

A few observations for the revision pass:

**The discipline: rescuing Bloom rather than debunking him.** The spec was specific about this, and the draft has tried to hold it. Every analytical observation about the 1984 paper is paired with an acknowledgment that Bloom's own framing is not what the essai is critiquing. The villain of the essai is the rhetorical apparatus that has traveled with the number, not Bloom himself. The closing explicitly says so.

**Block structure.** Five-block structure as specified: Block A (the number and its reputation), Block B (reading the original paper — the Anania and Burke studies, the conditions, the four features most often unnamed), Block C (the replication question — Cohen 1982, VanLehn 2011, Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan 2020), Block D (the cost question — Saga, MATCH, Khanmigo, Rori), Block E (what the 2-sigma figure is and is not evidence for). The draft adds a sixth thematic section between D and E on Bloom's "alterable variables" and his teacher-centered vision — I included this because the contrast between Bloom's theory-of-solution and the contemporary AI-replaces-teacher move is material the essai's argument benefits from. If the revision pass wants to cut this for length, it can be compressed to a paragraph in the closing section.

**Length.** Draft runs approximately 5,400 words, projecting to 22-24 pages at trade-scholarly typesetting — within the 22-25 page range specified, closer to the floor than the target 23. The essai could bear a bit more length if the revision pass wanted to expand the replication-record discussion or the VanLehn "granularity" taxonomy. It could also be tightened by cutting the "alterable variables" section. The current length feels right to me, but the decision is yours.

**Direct quotes.** The draft uses direct quotes from Bloom's 1984 paper sparingly — "too costly for most societies to bear on a large scale," "the teacher is more important than ever," "diagnosing misunderstanding," "motivating and rewarding achievement." These are the quotes the Deep Research document highlighted as load-bearing for the rescuing-Bloom move. If you have access to the original paper and want to verify them or add others, the draft is structured to accommodate additional quotes without restructuring.

**What the draft does particularly well.**

**What the draft may have gotten wrong.**
