# Kindle-First Workflow — Editorial Specification

## What you just locked

Kindle is the primary source. Each essai is written in full Kindle register — with all the structural apparatus, bridges, and cumulative architecture the specifications call for. The Substack version of each essai is an adaptation of the Kindle source, not an independent draft.

This is the simpler operational model. It means:

- You write each essai once, structurally complete.
- You produce a Substack adaptation from the Kindle source for the daily-article release.
- The Kindle compilation is the primary artifact; the Substack run is the publication-launch channel and the discussion channel.

The question to answer now: what specifically differs between the Kindle version and the Substack adaptation, such that the Substack version reads natively on the platform and the Kindle version reads as the definitive source.

---

## Kindle version — the full text

Every essai's Kindle version contains:

- **The essai itself** in its structural entirety — all blocks, full length, close reading with the apparatus applied.
- **The Kindle-register closing:** finding + named uncertainty + explicit invitation + bridge to the next essai. Four elements.
- **Footnotes or endnotes** for citations and source material. Essai register carries citations lightly in prose; the Kindle edition can move them to notes without losing the essai's flow.
- **Cross-references to other essais in the volume** where relevant. *"As Essai 4 examined, Bloom's 1984 finding..."* Cross-references are invisible on Substack (where readers may not have read Essai 4 yet); they work in Kindle (where readers either have read Essai 4 or can return to it instantly).

The Kindle version also participates in the book-level apparatus:

- The full Kindle preface — 8–12 pages, structural — frames the volume.
- Part-level openings (*Part I: Orientation*, etc.) with brief part-openers if warranted.
- The closing essai (Essai 14) operates as the book's closing, returning to Essai 1's opening.
- Author's revisions from the Substack discussion period, incorporated as marginal corrections or footnote commentary where warranted.

This is the publication of record.

## Substack adaptation — what changes from Kindle

The adaptation is not a rewrite. It is a set of specific edits that make the essai read natively on Substack as a daily article. Most of the essai's text is unchanged.

### What gets stripped

**The bridge to the next essai.** On Substack, readers do not need a bridge — they will encounter the next essai in two days whether you bridge to it or not, and a bridge in a daily article reads as contrived. Remove the final sentence or paragraph that teases the next essai.

**The Kindle cross-references.** *"As Essai 4 examined, Bloom's 1984 finding..."* becomes *"Bloom's 1984 finding, which I examined in a previous essai..."* or just *"Bloom's 1984 finding..."* — inline phrasing that works whether the reader has read the previous essai or not. Substack readers may be new; do not assume prior reading.

**Part-level scaffolding.** Substack does not have parts. If Essai 4 refers to being in *Part II: Inheritances*, that reference goes. The essai stands on its own as a Substack post.

### What gets added

**A contextual opener — one or two sentences.** The Substack version opens with a brief orienting line that positions the essai in the series without assuming prior reading. Not a summary of the previous essai; more like *"In this series of essais I'm examining how the learning-systems field evidences its own effectiveness. The previous essai looked at Bloom's 2-sigma. This one turns to what effect-size comparisons actually do when the field places different sigmas side by side."* Three sentences, maximum.

**An explicit invitation at the close.** The Kindle closing ends with the bridge. The Substack closing ends with the invitation — more prominent than in the Kindle version, possibly as a direct second-person address: *"I'm most interested in where you think this reading is wrong. The 5 percent problem as I've described it is sharp; the structural claim is provisional. Tell me what I've missed."* This is the invitational register made explicit in a way the Kindle edition does not need to make explicit (because the Kindle edition carries the invitation structurally in the form).

**Links and formatting.** Substack permits — and rewards — hyperlinks to sources, pull quotes formatted visually, and judicious use of bold for key terms. The Kindle version carries these as footnotes or italics; the Substack version lifts them into the visual field of the post. The hyperlink to the Khan Academy 2022 study, to the Chalkbeat interview, to Bloom's 1984 paper — these go in the Substack version in-line; in the Kindle version, they become notes.

**A "from this series" footer.** Every Substack post ends with a footer that orients the reader to the series — *"This is Essai 4 of 14 in Vol. 1: The Evidence Problem. Previous: [link]. Next: [date]. Sign up to get each essai in your inbox."* Operational apparatus the Kindle version does not carry.

### What stays the same

**The body of the essai.** The analytical work, the anchors, the close reading, the specific findings, the construct discussion — unchanged between versions. The Substack reader and the Kindle reader encounter the same close reading.

**The voice.** Montaignian register, provisional commitment, specific findings. Unchanged.

**The length.** Substack adaptations do not get shortened to make them "blog-friendly." A 25-page essai is 25 pages on Substack. Readers who subscribe to a publication called *Essais on Learning* by an author who describes the project as Montaignian essais are signing up for long form. Do not apologize for length; do not cut for cadence.

## The editorial workflow, in sequence

For each essai, the workflow is:

**1. Write the Kindle version.** Full structural apparatus. Bridge-style closing. Footnoted citations. Cross-references. The essai as it will appear in the book.

**2. Produce the Substack adaptation.** Strip the bridge. Add the opener. Convert cross-references to inline phrasing. Lift citations from footnotes to hyperlinks. Add the explicit invitation at the close. Add the from-this-series footer.

**3. Light editing pass on the Substack adaptation.** Substack-native formatting: pull quotes, bolded key terms, hyperlinks working. This is half an hour of work per essai, not a rewrite.

**4. Schedule the Substack post** for its release date.

**5. After Substack release, watch for discussion.** Comments, responses, corrections, pushback. If something warrants revision, note it.

**6. During quarterly Kindle compilation preparation:** Return to the Kindle version of each essai. Incorporate any revisions warranted by the Substack discussion — either as edits to the essai itself, or as marginal corrections in the Montaignian sense ("In the Substack version I claimed X; reader Y pushed back on this; I now think..."). This is the Kindle version's earn-your-existence move.

The first pass (Kindle source) takes the full drafting time. The Substack adaptation (step 2–3) is an hour to two hours per essai. The post-release discussion monitoring and the quarterly compilation are separate workstreams.

## What I recommend writing first

You have the complete specification in hand for 14 essais plus the Kindle preface plus a short Substack launch post. The strategic question is where to start drafting.

**My recommendation: Write in publication order, with one exception.**

**First:** Draft Essai 1. It is the opening essai, the framing piece, the reader's first encounter with the form. It is also the essai whose specification is most complete, whose anchor is clearest (Khan's TED statement), and whose length is most manageable (20 pages). Draft this first. You will have a finished artifact quickly, the specification will have been tested by actual drafting, and you will know whether the register we locked is the register you actually want to write in.

**Second:** Draft the Kindle preface. You write this second — not first — because the preface positions the volume, and until you have drafted Essai 1 you do not yet know how the voice sounds in the actual essais. The preface has to match that voice; you cannot write it blind and trust that the essais will match.

**Third:** Draft Essai 2. The load-bearing one. This is the hardest essai to write, and you write it with Essai 1 behind you (so you know the voice) and with the preface in draft (so the reader's expectations are set by the time Essai 2 has to deliver).

**Fourth:** Draft Essais 3–14 in order. By the time you are at Essai 4, the cadence of writing should be established; subsequent essais are easier drafts than the first three.

**The exception:** Essai 13 has been drafted once, in Kindle register, with the closing in the wrong register (bridge to Essai 14 rather than invitational closing). At some point before the Kindle compilation ships, Essai 13's closing needs to be revised. This is not urgent for the Substack run (Essai 13 releases after Essais 1–12 have released, by which point you will know exactly how the closing register should work). But it is worth noting: *the existing Essai 13 draft is a solid structural draft that needs a register pass on its closing, not a full rewrite.*

If Essai 1 reads in draft and the form does not land, that is evidence the spec needs to change. But the draft I produce is a *specification-tester*, not a final text. I can also produce drafts that **serve as voice-seeders** — first drafts you use as raw material and then rewrite in your voice. If you want this kind of draft, I will produce it, but I recommend flagging that you are using my draft as clay rather than as text.

The third option — I produce nothing; you write from the specification directly — is the most Montaignian move. The specifications are complete enough to draft from. Your voice is what the essais need. I am a pressure-tester for what you produce, not a producer of the thing itself.
