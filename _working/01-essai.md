# On the Book's Project, or: *Que sais-je?*

*Essai 1*

---

In April of 2023, Sal Khan stood on a TED stage in Vancouver and described what he had built. He had been working with OpenAI on an AI tutor, integrated into the Khan Academy platform. He had given it a name: Khanmigo. He had been testing it with students. He thought it represented, and here I want to quote him cleanly, *probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen.*

The talk was well-received. Millions of people watched it on the internet in the days that followed. The clip has been cited, in one form or another, in dozens of subsequent articles about AI and education. Sal Khan is a generous public figure, a careful thinker, and by every account I can find a person of genuine conviction about the work he has spent twenty years doing. The sentence he uttered that afternoon is not a strange sentence for him to have uttered. It is not, I think, a dishonest sentence. It is the kind of sentence Sal Khan says when he is trying to tell people what he believes is happening.

I want to ask you to sit with it for a moment before this essai goes any further.

*Probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen.*

Not *a significant advance*. Not *a technology with real promise*. Not even *the biggest transformation of the last decade*. The biggest transformation education has ever seen. Since the invention of the printing press. Since the establishment of public schooling. Since the Americanization of the research university. Since the GI Bill. Since the internet. The biggest.

Three years later, in April of 2026, Sal Khan and Khan Academy's Chief Academic Officer, Kristen DiCerbo, sat for an interview with Matt Barnum at *Chalkbeat*. The subject was, in some sense, the same: Khanmigo, its rollout, what had happened. The language had changed. *"For a lot of students,"* Khan said, *"it was a non-event. They just didn't use it much."* DiCerbo, speaking alongside him: *"So far I am not seeing the revolution in education."*

The sentence from 2023 is in the record. So is the sentence from 2026. Both were uttered, in public, by the same person. The technology between them is the same technology, more or less, with three years of development work layered on. Some students have used Khanmigo across those three years and some have not. Some teachers have deployed it in their classrooms and some have not. The biggest positive transformation education has ever seen has, in its own founder's account, been for most students a non-event.

I am not going to tell you, in this essai, what to think about that. You have the two sentences in front of you. You can hear the distance between them, and you can develop your own account of what the distance means. What I want, right now, is something simpler: I want you to notice the shape of the first sentence.

*Probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen.*

This is a sentence of a specific type. It is the type of sentence the learning-systems field has been producing for a very long time. Not only about AI tutors. Not only about Khan Academy. About teaching machines, in 1958. About computer-assisted instruction, in 1966. About MOOCs, in 2013. About adaptive learning, in 2015. Each time, the shape is the same. A new technology arrives. A claim is made about its transformative potential. The claim is staked in superlatives — biggest, largest, most revolutionary. The claim circulates. Funding follows. Deployment follows. Three years later, or five, or ten, someone speaking about the same technology uses the word *non-event*.

This essai is not about Sal Khan. It is not about Khan Academy. It is not, ultimately, about AI tutors at all. It is about the shape of the sentence he uttered in 2023, which is a shape the field has been producing for six decades, and which I think is worth looking at carefully — not for what it claims, but for what it is.

---

You are reading this, I think, because you have heard sentences of that shape before. You may have uttered sentences close to them yourself, though probably not in quite that register. You may have nodded along when others uttered them, in meetings or at conferences or in the pages of articles you read for work. You may have felt, at some of those moments, something like the quiet unease of not quite having the words to push back. You knew the claim was too big. You could not quite say why.

If that describes you, I know something else about you. You are in or adjacent to a field — education, educational technology, learning science, instructional design, the funding and policy and journalism that surround these — where efficacy claims circulate as currency. You have cited an effect size in a proposal. You have read a study and wondered whether its findings held. You have watched a tool get funded or adopted or celebrated, and have wondered whether the evidence behind it was quite what the celebration implied. You have seen the Bloom 2-sigma number invoked, probably more than once, and you may or may not have read the original paper, and if you have read it you may or may not have noticed certain things about what Bloom actually claimed.

You are a careful person working in a field that does not always reward careful attention to its own evidence. The tools of careful attention are not secret. Much of the vocabulary you need has been available, in one research tradition or another, for decades. But the vocabulary is not distributed evenly, and the press of daily work does not always leave time for the careful reading that would let you trust or distrust a claim for your own specific reasons rather than by deferring to the consensus, or to the sigma, or to the confident sentence uttered from a stage.

These essais are written to the reader I have just described. I have been that reader. I have sat in the meeting where a sigma number was invoked at me and have not had the words. I have read the study and wondered what the study was actually about. I have nodded along, and I have bristled, and I have left the room still not quite sure whether the claim was one I should have accepted or resisted.

What follows is not a solution to that condition. It is an attempt at the tools. Not the tools for winning the argument. The tools for reading the claim.

---

Perhaps I should say what this book is not, because the list is almost as important as what it is.

It is not a takedown of Khan Academy, or of Khanmigo, or of Sal Khan. I have nothing against any of them. I am going to return to Sal Khan at the end of this volume, in the closing essai, and ask the reader to notice how differently they can hold the 2023 TED sentence than they could hold it fifteen essais ago. That is a very different project from a takedown.

It is not a critique of artificial intelligence in education, or of any particular generation of learning technology. AI tutors are one case in this volume. Intelligent tutoring systems are another. Adaptive learning is a third. Teaching machines in the 1950s are a fourth. What these cases have in common is not the technology. It is something about the apparatus by which the field evaluates whether any of these technologies work. The subject is the apparatus.

It is not a polemic against the technology industry, against educational philanthropy, against the companies that sell products to schools, or against any specific researcher or advocate. I am in no position to write that book, and writing it would require a register I do not trust. This volume does not make claims about who is to blame, or who is captured by whom, or what the field's participants' motivations are. Where I am tempted toward those claims, I have tried to put the temptation down.

It is not a prescription for what the field should do. If these essais are successful, you will finish them with a sharper sense of what the evidence apparatus is and a sharper sense of what it does not tell us. You will not finish them with a five-point plan. I have thoughts about what a better evidence apparatus would require, and I will sketch them provisionally in the thirteenth essai, but I will not tell you what the field should do with those thoughts. That is the field's conversation to have. These essais are an attempt to contribute to it. They are not an attempt to settle it.

What this book is, instead, is a series of close readings. Fourteen of them. Each one takes up a specific instance — a foundational claim, a family of studies, a rhetorical pattern, a structural feature of how the field produces and defends its evidence — and reads it carefully. With the apparatus you will acquire in the second essai, you will watch me apply the same kinds of questions to each instance. You will, I hope, eventually begin to apply those questions yourself. The repetition is the point. The habit of attention develops through watching the attention enacted, many times, across varied material.

If the book has a thesis, it is this: the learning-systems field evaluates its own effectiveness using a measurement apparatus optimized for publication, funding, and rhetorical use — not for the questions careful readers would actually want answered about whether and how people learn. Nearly every efficacy claim the field produces, from Bloom's 2-sigma forward, rests on evidentiary foundations that do not support the weight the field places on them. And this matters because the next generation of learning systems is being designed, funded, and adopted on the basis of these inherited claims, without the apparatus to ask whether they were ever true in the way the field has been repeating them.

I am committed to that thesis in the way Montaigne was committed to his positions. Which is to say: specifically, provisionally, and with visible willingness to revise when readers push back. The essais will test the thesis across fourteen cases. If the cases do not hold up, the thesis does not hold up, and the book has told you something different from what I thought it would tell you when I started writing it. I will be glad to find that out. I will be gladder to find it out from you than from a reviewer.

---

A word about the form.

These are essais, not essays. The word is Montaigne's, from the French *essayer*, to try. He invented the form in the 1570s, working alone in a tower on his family's estate in what is now southwestern France, writing short prose pieces he did not know what else to call. The word he chose, and the form he invented with it, have been diluted across four and a half centuries into something almost unrecognizable. An essay, today, is expected to make a case. An essai, properly, does not.

What an essai does is commit — specifically, carefully, sharply — and then hold the commitment as provisional. Montaigne would take a position. He would defend it. He would follow its implications, sometimes to places that surprised him. He would, occasionally, revise it within the same essai, writing himself out of a position by thinking harder about what it required. Across the three editions of the *Essais* published during and after his lifetime, he wrote corrections into the margins of his own earlier essais. A claim made in 1580 might appear, in 1588, with a handwritten caveat in the margin: *I see now that this was wrong, or incomplete, or required more care than I gave it.* The finished essais are layered documents. Thinking that has been visibly revised.

I am not the first contemporary writer to work in this tradition. Adam Phillips does it, in his way. Rebecca Solnit does it, differently. Phil Agre did it for artificial intelligence in the 1990s in a book called *Computation and Human Experience*, which is out of print and which remains the closest intellectual ancestor this book has. John Berger did it. Maggie Nelson has done it. The form has a lineage in English that is older than most readers realize.

The form is not a concession to uncertainty. It is a discipline. An essai commits to things it could be wrong about. What distinguishes it from a monograph is not that it is less confident, but that it holds its confidence differently. A monograph builds an argument and defends it. An essai offers a reading, specifies what the reading depends on, and invites the reader to check the reading against their own attention. Both are valid ways of writing. Both produce knowledge, of different kinds. For the subject matter of this book, I think the essai form is more honest to what I can actually claim. A confident monograph about the inadequacy of the field's evidence apparatus would use the very rhetorical moves it was critiquing.

---

There are fourteen essais in this volume, organized into four parts. The part you are in now, *Orientation*, is the first.

The essai you are reading is the first of three in this part. It is doing what prefaces cannot do: positioning you as the reader this book is written to, naming the reader's situation in terms specific enough to be recognizable, establishing the book's project in a voice that extends across fourteen essais rather than in a preface's more condensed register. By the end of this essai, you should have, I hope, a clear sense of whether this book is for you. If it is not, this is the right moment to put it down. I would rather you do so than read thirteen more essais waiting for something the book is not going to deliver.

The second essai — and I want to say this clearly, so that you have it before you encounter the essai itself — is the most important essai in this volume. If you read only one essai in this issue, or only one essai in this book, read the second. It installs the apparatus the rest of the volume uses. The five questions to ask of any efficacy claim. The distinction between what a measurement indexes and what the measurement is cited as evidence for. The analytical move the book will return to, essai by essai, across every subsequent case. I am telling you this in the first essai because essai collections do not usually install tools that later essais depend on, and I want you to know before you read the second essai that this one does. You can skip almost any other essai in this volume. You can read them out of order. You can dip in and out. The second essai is the exception. Read it, and read it carefully.

The third essai takes the apparatus the second essai installs and locates the measurement habits it exposes in their six-decade history. Back to Skinner's teaching machines in 1958, forward through PLATO and the early CAI systems, through the intelligent-tutoring-systems literature of the 1980s and 90s, through adaptive learning in the 2010s, into the present moment. The claim the third essai makes is that the measurement problem I am asking you to take seriously is not new, and that the current generation of AI-tutor claims inherit measurement habits older than any of their authors. This claim is load-bearing for the rest of the volume. If the problem were new, you might reasonably wait for better studies from better researchers using better methods. The third essai argues that the problem is not new, and that waiting for better studies from within the same apparatus will produce the same findings in a different costume.

The second part of this volume, *Inheritances*, applies the apparatus to the field's foundational numbers. Bloom's 1984 2-sigma finding, which is cited in nearly every contemporary efficacy claim as a benchmark of what good tutoring can achieve. The general practice of comparing effect sizes across studies. The institution of the meta-analysis, which purports to aggregate evidence but which, read carefully, can aggregate methodological heterogeneity into apparent convergence. And the intelligent-tutoring-systems literature, which is the single most-cited body of efficacy research in the learning-systems field and which has never been read in the register I am going to read it in. These four essais, taken together, should leave you holding the field's foundational evidence differently than you held it when you began.

The third part, *The Current Moment*, applies the same apparatus to claims that are currently alive. What adaptive-learning platforms actually operate on versus what they are sold as doing. Engagement metrics and the rhetorical work they perform when harder outcomes refuse to cooperate. The wave of AI-tutor efficacy studies — Khanmigo, Kestin, Eedi, Rori — that have arrived since 2022 and are currently being cited as evidence that the landscape has changed. The field's rhetorical moves when tools publicly underperform, which I think are part of the apparatus and not a separate phenomenon. These are the essais most in the moment; they are also the essais most likely to date, because the moment will pass. I accept that cost. The alternative would be a book that does not engage the present, and a book that does not engage the present is not the book I want to write.

The fourth part, *The Deeper Concealments*, does what I think is the book's most structurally important work. It steps back from specific approaches and specific technologies and examines features of the apparatus that have been present under every case the earlier essais examined. Cost, which the field treats as a business question separate from efficacy but which is, read carefully, an epistemic variable that changes what any efficacy claim can mean. The distinction between what the field measures and what the field claims it is producing evidence about. And then a closing essai that releases you, with whatever habit of attention the preceding thirteen have produced, back into the field to do what you do with it.

That is the shape. I have told you more about it than a preface usually tells, because I want you to know what you are signing up for before we continue. If the shape does not speak to you, this is the moment to close the book.

---

One thing remains before this essai ends.

I said a page ago that this book is not a prescription for what the field should do. I want to extend that. It is also not a book that will give you a verdict on any specific product, researcher, company, or claim you currently hold opinions about. If you have strong feelings about Khanmigo, this book will not confirm or refute them. If you have strong feelings about adaptive learning, or about intelligent tutoring systems, or about the AI moment in education, this book will not tell you that you have been right or wrong all along. It will, I hope, make your feelings more specific. It will give you language for what you suspect and what you cannot yet say. What you conclude from that language is your conclusion, not mine.

The reason for this is not humility. It is form. These essais are trying to model a way of reading, and a way of reading does not terminate in verdicts. It terminates in being able to read the next thing. The habit I am trying to offer is portable — portable to claims these essais do not cover, to technologies that do not yet exist, to your own next Tuesday morning when someone emails you a new efficacy study and asks what you think. If the essais do their work, the question *what do I think?* becomes answerable without my help. That is the condition of the essai form working. It is also the condition of these essais being worth writing.

I am going to return, in the closing essai, to the Khan sentence we began with. *Probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen.* When we get there, you will have had a chance to read twelve more essais. Some of them will have revised your sense of what it means to claim a transformation. Others will have shown you what the apparatus of such a claim looks like, historically, across six decades of different technologies making similar claims with similar outcomes. By the time we return to the sentence, I think you will be able to hold it differently than you can hold it now, with the preface barely behind you and this first essai closing in front of you.

That holding — that different way of attending to a sentence that used to sound like confidence and now sounds like something else — is the principal deliverable of this book. What you do with it afterward is your work, not mine.

*Que sais-je?* — what do I know? — is Montaigne's motto. It has been adopted, across four centuries, as a motto for skepticism, and that is a misreading. Montaigne was not skeptical in the contemporary sense. He took positions. He committed to them. He changed his mind, visibly, when he had reason to. What *Que sais-je?* names is not the absence of knowledge but the discipline of holding knowledge provisionally, specifically, with honesty about what it depends on. That discipline is what these essais are trying to enact and what they are trying to invite from you.

I am committed to the thesis of this volume. I am not sure I am right. I think I have read more of the primary literature than most of the people who write about these questions, and I think the readings I have produced are defensible. I also think I am almost certainly wrong about something in these essais — some case I have misread, some finding I have overstated, some connection I have made that does not hold up. I do not yet know which somethings. I am counting on readers — including you — to tell me, specifically enough that I can revise.

The next essai begins that work. It opens on the Khan Academy 2022 efficacy study, a specific finding about a specific tool under specific conditions, and it walks through what it would mean to read that finding carefully. By the end of the next essai, you will have the apparatus. By the end of the third, you will know the apparatus is not new. The rest of the volume is what happens when that apparatus is pointed at fourteen things, across 300 pages, over the course of a year.

If you are still here, thank you for coming. The book begins now.

---

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

I want to close this essai in the register the series has committed to.

I am not sure, in the way I have framed this essai, that I have been fair enough to Sal Khan. It is possible that my reading of the 2023 TED statement is harder on it than the statement warrants. The sentence was delivered in the register of a TED talk, which is not the register of a peer-reviewed claim, and I have asked you to treat it as a specimen of the field's rhetorical habits partly because I think it is one. A reader might think I have loaded the dice by beginning with a sentence no serious researcher would defend under peer review. The response to that concern is that the sentence has been cited, by Khan and by others, as though it were a defensible claim — and the reading I am proposing is a reading of what the sentence has been asked to do, not of what Khan meant when he said it. But the concern is legitimate. A reader who thinks I have treated Khan unfairly in this opening should say so.

I am not sure the reader I have positioned in this essai is actually the reader who will find this book. The description of *someone who has felt unease about the field's claims without having the framework to articulate what* matches many readers I know. It also may miss readers who would benefit from the book. Education researchers who are quite comfortable with the field's existing framework may read this essai and feel condescended to. Early-career practitioners may read it and feel they are being asked to hold something they have not yet earned the standing to hold. I cannot position every reader. The position I have taken is, I think, the one most useful to the book's project. A reader who does not recognize themselves in this opening may still find the subsequent essais useful.

I am most interested, as you read forward, in whether the thesis holds up under the specific readings. If the readings are sound, the thesis follows. If the readings are not sound, the thesis does not follow, and the book should be revised — or, in the margins of subsequent Kindle editions, visibly corrected. The apparatus I am asking you to hold is specific enough that sound readings and unsound readings can be distinguished. I will be interested, essai by essai, in your sense of which are which.

The comments on this essai's Substack page are the simplest place to tell me where this essai falls short. In the quarterly Kindle compilation, where I incorporate the series' discussion into the record, any pushback from this essai will be visible — either in the form of marginal corrections, or in a "from the discussion" section at the close of the issue, or in whatever form I find the response apparatus calling for as the series develops.

The second essai follows. I hope you will continue with me.

## Notes on the draft

A few structural observations for the author's revision pass:

**Block structure adherence.** The draft follows the four-block structure specified at /c1 closely:
- Block A (*The sentence and what it sounds like*, roughly pages 1–5): opens on the Khan quote, pauses on it, contrasts with the 2026 retreat, names the sentence-shape as the essai's subject. Works.
- Block B (*The reader*, roughly pages 5–9): turns to the reader, names them specifically, gives them permission to have felt the unease, names common forms of it. The essai's most emotionally direct block, as the spec required.
- Block C (*The book's project*, roughly pages 9–16): names what the book will and will not do, introduces the essai form, walks through the four-part structure, names Essai 2 as load-bearing. The longest block, as the spec predicted.
- Block D (*The invitation*, roughly pages 16–20): closes with how to read the book, the Montaigne motto, and bridges to Essai 2.

**Length.** The draft runs about 4,700 words, projecting to approximately 21 pages at trade-scholarly typesetting — within the 20–22 page range specified, closer to the ceiling. If the revision pass wants to tighten, Block C's four-part walkthrough could be compressed; I left it at current length because the Kindle-register preface gives it shape, and the Substack version of this essai (which will be adapted later) can cut it more sharply.

**Invitational closing.** This is the first essai written under the invitational register we locked. The closing block includes the three elements the new register calls for: the finding (implicit — the book's project and the essai form as adequate to the subject), the named uncertainty (three specific things I am not sure about: the fairness to Khan, the reader positioning, whether the thesis holds under the specific readings), and the explicit invitation (pushback on the Khan reading, pushback on the reader positioning, case-by-case response from readers going forward). This is the template that all subsequent essais will follow.

**What the draft does well.**
- Sits with the Khan quote for the opening without immediately attacking it. The spec flagged that this was the essai's hardest craft move, and I think the draft holds it.
- Does not indict Khan — describes him carefully, names what he was doing, refuses to characterize his motives.
- Names the book's project clearly without preempting the essais that follow.
- The Montaigne introduction is brief (two paragraphs), which the spec asked for. Does not become a Montaigne primer.
- Names the reader in terms specific enough to be recognizable, general enough to encompass the book's target community.

**What the draft may have gotten wrong.**
- The "what this book is not" block (Block C middle) is longer than the spec suggested. I kept it because the adjacent-community reader needs to know what the book is NOT doing, given the invitational reframe. A revision pass might tighten this.
- The sentence "If the book has a thesis, it is this..." introduces the thesis without softening it to working-hypothesis register as the prior artifact suggested. I used "committed... specifically, provisionally" which retains the invitational frame, but a reader comparing this to the working-hypothesis framing may notice that the thesis here reads more like a thesis than a hypothesis. The revision pass might prefer to soften slightly to match the preface.
- The listserv reference that appeared in the preface does not appear here. I left it in the preface only, which is probably right — the essai does not need to name the community twice.
- The essai uses "I" frequently. This is a choice, and the Montaignian register permits it, but in places the "I" could be softened to keep the reader as the subject. Specifically in Block C, where "I am committed to the thesis" could be "these essais are committed."

**Tone check.**
- The faux-humility risk flagged in the contributor notes. The "I am not sure I am right" passage in the closing is the highest-risk moment. I think it holds because it is followed by specific claims about what I DO think (the readings are defensible; I have read the primary literature; I am counting on specific revision). It reads as discipline rather than hedge. But this is the passage a hostile reader should push on.
- The "biggest positive transformation" reading. I have worked to make this a reading rather than an indictment. The first two paragraphs treat Khan charitably — generous, careful, genuine — before pausing on what the sentence does. I think this is right. A reader who thinks I have not been fair to Khan should tell me, and the closing block of the essai invites exactly that pushback.

Ready for your revision pass.
