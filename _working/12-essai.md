# On Cost in the Denominator

*Essai 12*

---

I want to begin this essai with a retrospective.

Nine essais ago, the fourth essai of this volume examined Benjamin Bloom's 1984 paper and the 2-sigma finding that has structured educational-technology claims for four decades. The examination focused on what Bloom's two dissertations (Anania 1981 and Burke 1983) actually measured, under what conditions, with what duration, against what baseline. The examination did not center cost. I want to center it now.

Bloom's 1984 paper is usually remembered for the two-sigma claim — the finding that students receiving one-to-one tutoring combined with mastery learning scored approximately two standard deviations higher than students in conventional classroom instruction. The paper is sometimes remembered for Bloom's framing of this as "the 2-sigma problem" — the problem being that tutoring was effective but not scalable at reasonable cost. What is less often remembered is the specific phrase Bloom used to describe the cost barrier. He wrote that one-to-one tutoring was *"too costly for most societies to bear on a large scale"* — and he wrote this as the central motivation for the paper. The 2-sigma problem was not "how do we get tutoring's effect with different instructional design." The 2-sigma problem was "how do we get tutoring's effect at a cost that a society can bear."

The field has spent forty years citing Bloom's effect size while stripping the cost constraint out of the citation.

This essai is about what that stripping conceals, and about what happens when we put the cost denominator back into the comparison.

The specific argument is this. Cost is not a business concern about whether schools can afford an intervention. It is an *epistemic variable* — a constitutive feature of what any effect size is a finding about. An effect size produced at $5 per student per year and an effect size produced at $4,000 per student per year are not findings on the same axis. Comparing them without the cost denominator is a rhetorical move rather than an analytical one. What looks, in the bare sigma comparison, like one intervention being more effective than another may actually be two interventions deploying different quantities of resource, and the "effectiveness" comparison may be measuring resource intensity rather than instructional power.

I am going to walk through what this means for the anchor cases the book has examined in prior essais. Bloom at the 1984 tutoring conditions, priced in contemporary dollars. Kestin at the Harvard AI-tutor study, priced with the caveats the data permits. The Khan Academy arc at its marginal-cost pricing. Rori at the five-dollar deployment in Ghana. Cognitive Tutor at the RAND-documented $97 per student. i-Ready and DreamBox and ALEKS at their list prices. Saga Education tutoring at three-and-a-half-to-four-and-three-quarters thousand dollars per student per year. Each of these cases has been invoked in contemporary EdTech discourse; each has a cost structure; and the cross-study sigma comparisons that have structured the field's claims have typically stripped the cost structures out.

Before I walk through them, I want to be specific about what this essai is and is not arguing.

It is not arguing that expensive interventions are better. Saga tutoring's 0.25 sigma at $4,000 per student is not automatically superior to Khan Academy's 0.04 sigma at $10 per student just because the former is more expensive. The argument is the opposite: once you look at the cost denominator, the Khan Academy effect per dollar is substantially larger, even though its raw sigma is smaller.

It is also not arguing that cheap interventions are better. Rori's 0.36 sigma at $5 per student is not automatically superior to Saga's 0.25 sigma at $4,000 just because it is cheaper. The two interventions are serving different populations in different contexts with different baselines, and the cost denominator does not resolve those differences into a single ranking.

What the essai is arguing is that the interventions are not commensurable without the cost denominator. An effect size without cost is a number that does not support the comparative claims the field has been making with it. The comparison *is not happening on a single axis* until cost is in the frame. Adding cost does not produce a single ranking either; it produces a productivity frontier on which different interventions occupy different positions, each appropriate to different contexts and resource environments. The frontier is an analytical space. The single axis is a rhetorical one.

---

Let me start with Bloom, because the anchor findings of the book mostly invoke his paper.

Bloom's 2-sigma finding came from two doctoral dissertations — Anania's 1981 and Burke's 1983 — that the volume's fourth essai examined at length. The specific conditions were three-week instructional windows with undergraduate tutors trained for one week, using a 1-to-1 or 1-to-3 tutor-to-student ratio, with a mastery learning feedback-corrective infrastructure that allowed students to spend 20 to 33 percent more instructional time than the control group. These conditions produced the two-sigma effect.

What did these conditions cost?

In the specific dissertations, the cost was absorbed by the universities and the study infrastructure. The undergraduate tutors were graduate-program resources. The mastery learning materials were built by the research team. The three-week windows were short enough that the per-student cost of the experimental setup itself was modest. Bloom's paper does not attempt a formal cost accounting of the dissertation interventions, because the research was conducted at research-grade resource intensity without the translation-to-scale question in view.

What Bloom did attempt, and what makes his 1984 paper more honest than much of what followed, was to estimate what it would cost to deploy this intervention at scale. One-to-one tutoring with trained tutors, using mastery learning feedback across a full school year, at the intensity the dissertations documented, would require a substantial labor investment. In 1984 dollars, Bloom's implicit estimate suggested that this was prohibitively expensive for public education systems. Translating to contemporary dollars — using standard CPI adjustments that put 1984's dollar at approximately 2.7 to 3 times current value — places the cost at somewhere between $2,000 and $4,000 per student per year for the high-dosage tutoring conditions Bloom was pointing at.

We have a contemporary benchmark for this estimate. High-dosage tutoring programs that attempt to replicate Bloom's conditions in current public education settings — Saga Education, MATCH Corps, Blueprint Education, and comparable programs documented in the Nickow-Oreopoulos-Quan 2020 meta-analysis of tutoring RCTs — report per-student-per-year costs in the range of $3,500 to $4,800. This is the price, in 2026 dollars, of producing the conditions Bloom's research was a finding about. Saga's reported effects, when evaluated in the contemporary randomized controlled trial literature, are in the range of 0.25 to 0.4 sigma — not the 2.0 sigma Bloom found, because the operational conditions in contemporary public schools differ from Bloom's three-week high-fidelity laboratory conditions. But the cost comparison is the comparison the field keeps stripping out. Bloom's 2-sigma effect, in its operational form, runs at roughly $4,000 per student per year.

This places Bloom's 2-sigma finding in a specific location on the cost-effectiveness plane. An effect size of 2.0 sigma at $4,000 per student produces a sigma-per-dollar of 0.0005. This is the fraction of a standard deviation you get for each dollar spent on the intervention. Keep this number in your mind as the rest of the essai walks through the other cases.

---

Take Kestin's 2024 Harvard physics AI-tutor study next.

The Kestin study's reported effect size, as the tenth essai examined, was in the 0.73 to 1.3 sigma range. The study does not report per-student deployment costs in primary form. What it does report is the composition of the intervention: a customized ChatGPT instance built on GPT-4, with system-prompt engineering performed by physics education researchers over some period of time, deployed to 194 Harvard undergraduates enrolled in Physical Sciences 2 for a research-session approximately 49 minutes in duration.

Without primary-source cost reporting, the essai can only reason about order-of-magnitude estimates. But the components of the cost are identifiable, and each can be approximated.

The compute cost — GPT-4 API calls at Socratic-dialogue token volumes — is not negligible but is also not enormous. For a 49-minute session of active dialogue, per-student compute costs were likely in the range of a few dollars to perhaps ten dollars, depending on message density. Scale this across 194 students and the research-phase compute bill is in the low thousands.

The labor cost — physics education researchers engineering system prompts, iterating on the tutor's design, monitoring its behavior, analyzing outputs — is substantially higher. Academic researcher time is expensive when properly accounted for, and the system-prompt engineering Kestin and colleagues describe was not a weekend's work. A conservative estimate places this cost in the tens of thousands of dollars for the research phase of the study.

The institutional baseline cost — the Harvard physics course infrastructure the AI tutor was deployed alongside, including lecturers, teaching assistants, course materials, and facilities — is already expensive, and the AI tutor was an addition to rather than a replacement for this baseline. Whatever the tutor's effect size, it was produced inside a course that Harvard was already running at research-university cost structures.

Projecting the Kestin deployment to scale is where the cost question becomes sharp. If the intervention required Harvard-grade system-prompt engineering, researcher oversight, and institutional baseline to produce the 0.73-to-1.3 sigma effect, deploying it at population scale would require either replicating these conditions (expensive) or accepting that the effect will not hold outside them (which changes what the effect size is a finding about). Khanmigo's experience — Khan Academy's own AI-tutor deployment at scale, with the marginal cost set at roughly $35 per student per year for district licenses — suggests that scaled AI-tutor deployment at affordable pricing produces substantially smaller effects than Kestin's research-grade deployment did. The DiCerbo "non-event" from the eleventh essai is, among other things, a finding about what scaled deployment at sustainable cost produces: something considerably smaller than 0.73 sigma.

We can place Kestin's research-grade deployment on the cost-effectiveness plane roughly. If we estimate the per-student all-in research cost at $100 to $200 (amortizing the research-team time across the 194 participants), the sigma-per-dollar is in the range of 0.004 to 0.013. This is substantially more efficient than Bloom's 0.0005, which is the specific honest thing the Kestin study suggests — at research-grade intensity in a high-resource context, AI-mediated instruction produces sigma per dollar that is meaningfully higher than high-dosage tutoring. What the Kestin study does not support is the claim that this efficiency persists when the research-grade conditions are removed. Khanmigo's operational deployment produces a sigma per dollar considerably smaller than Kestin's research-grade deployment, and this gap is the gap the apparatus needs to name rather than smooth over.

---

The Khan Academy arc, which the book has examined at several points, operates at a specific price tier that makes it the single most important cost anchor in current educational technology.

Khan Academy's district pricing for its core platform runs at approximately $10 per student per year — a marginal-cost figure that the organization has documented publicly in its pricing materials and in philanthropic-funding disclosures. This $10 does not include the amortized cost of Khan Academy's development work, which has been covered by over a hundred million dollars in philanthropic gifts across the organization's history. A for-profit organization trying to match Khan Academy's pricing would have to recover those development costs in its license fees, which would push the true cost-per-student considerably higher. The $10 is the marginal cost of delivering the platform to an additional district, not the fully-loaded cost of having built the platform in the first place.

With Khanmigo, Khan Academy's AI tutor, the marginal cost runs higher — roughly $35 per student per year for district pricing, or $44 annually for individual subscriptions. The 3.5-times increase over the base platform reflects the compute cost of generative AI dialogue and the incremental development and monitoring labor involved in maintaining an AI tutor at scale. Khan Academy's public framing has consistently emphasized that Khanmigo was priced to approximate its marginal cost rather than to generate profit, which is credible for a non-profit organization but leaves open the question of what a for-profit AI tutor at comparable quality would have to charge.

What effect sizes does the Khan Academy deployment produce? The second essai of this volume examined the 2022 Khan Academy evaluation by Thomas Grimaldi and colleagues, which reported effect sizes in the range of 0.04 to 0.09 sigma for the broad Khan Academy user population. The five-percent-problem framing from Laurence Holt, which the same essai invoked, surfaced that the aggregate effect was largely driven by the subset of students who used the platform at high dosage. For students who engaged with Khan Academy for the recommended 18 or more hours per year, effects were substantially larger — approaching the ranges reported for more resource-intensive interventions. For students who engaged below that dosage threshold, effects were much smaller. The average effect the evaluation reported was an average across this heterogeneous engagement distribution.

Placed on the cost-effectiveness plane, the Khan Academy deployment at roughly 0.06 sigma for the average user at $10 per student per year produces a sigma-per-dollar of 0.006 — meaningfully higher than Bloom's 0.0005. For high-dosage users, the sigma per dollar is higher still. This is not a claim that Khan Academy is "better" than high-dosage tutoring. Saga's 0.25 sigma is a real learning effect in a population that Khan Academy's intervention could not reach with the same magnitude; Saga and Khan Academy serve different kinds of work. What the cost-normalized comparison reveals is that the two are not on the same axis of "effectiveness." They occupy different positions on a productivity frontier, each appropriate to different contexts and resource environments. Treating them as competitors on a single sigma ranking — which the field's typical discussion does — is an analytical error that the cost denominator makes visible.

---

Rori, the WhatsApp-based AI math tutor deployed to Ghanaian secondary-school students, which the tenth essai examined, is the single most important anchor in the current AI-tutor literature precisely because its cost is rigorously reported.

Henkel and colleagues' 2024 study of Rori documented a marginal cost of approximately $5 per student per year. This figure includes the LLM API costs for the specialized pedagogical model Rori uses, the cost of data bandwidth for the 3G networks the tool is deployed over, and the operational costs of maintaining the WhatsApp-based delivery infrastructure. What it does not include is the amortized cost of Rori's development, which was funded by Rising Academies (Rori's developer) and philanthropic sources in ways analogous to Khan Academy's development history.

The effect size Rori produced was 0.36 sigma on curriculum-aligned math assessment, measured over an eight-month intervention, with substantial attrition that the study reports honestly. This is the highest effect-per-dollar finding in the contemporary AI-tutor literature by a large margin. At 0.36 sigma for $5 per student per year, the sigma-per-dollar is 0.072 — approximately 144 times more efficient than Bloom's two-sigma benchmark, approximately 12 times more efficient than the Khan Academy deployment at average-user engagement, approximately 5 to 18 times more efficient than Kestin's research-grade Harvard deployment depending on how the Kestin costs are estimated.

I want to be careful about what this comparison supports and what it does not.

The comparison does not support the claim that Rori is uniformly superior to other interventions. The Ghanaian baseline against which Rori's effect was measured is substantially weaker than the baselines against which Kestin or Saga or Khan Academy were evaluated — a fact the tenth essai examined at length. A $5 intervention that produces 0.36 sigma in a context where the counterfactual is under-resourced mathematical instruction is not the same finding as a $5 intervention producing 0.36 sigma in a well-resourced context. The denominator matters, but the baseline context matters too.

The comparison also does not support the claim that the Rori deployment can be replicated at $5 per student per year in other contexts. Different infrastructure, different labor costs, different data-bandwidth pricing, different content-development needs would all affect the marginal cost of a Rori-style deployment in, say, the United States. The $5 is a finding about Ghana specifically, not a portable price.

What the comparison *does* support is the observation that the current AI-tutor literature has been comparing findings across a range of sigma-per-dollar that spans roughly three orders of magnitude — from Bloom's 0.0005 at the low efficiency end to Rori's 0.072 at the high end — while treating all of them as findings on a single "effectiveness" axis. Rori is not the most effective intervention in the raw sigma sense; Bloom's 2-sigma tutoring is. But Rori is dramatically more effective per dollar. Once the denominator is visible, the comparison across these studies is a comparison between different points on a productivity frontier, not between points on a single ranking. And the productivity frontier is the analytical space the field needs to be working in, if the field wants to have honest conversations about where interventions should be deployed and under what conditions.

I want to walk more briefly through the other cases the book has examined, because the cost-denominator pattern is visible across all of them.

Cognitive Tutor, as documented in the Pane RAND 2014 report that the seventh essai examined, ran at $97 per student per year in implementation cost. The RAND report is unusually transparent about the cost composition: approximately $35 for materials and licensing, substantial additional costs for teacher professional development (three days of initial training plus ongoing coaching), technology infrastructure costs, and technical support. The comparison baseline in the study was traditional algebra textbook curriculum at $28 per student per year. Cognitive Tutor, therefore, was a 3.4-times resource-intensity increase over the baseline. The effect size Pane's team reported, the statistically significant second-year effect in high schools, was approximately 0.20 sigma. The sigma-per-dollar figure is 0.002 — more efficient than Bloom but substantially less efficient than Khan Academy's marginal-cost operation or Rori's low-cost deployment.

i-Ready and DreamBox, the adaptive-learning systems the eighth essai examined, run at approximately $22 to $35 per student per year per subject at list pricing, though districts typically negotiate lower prices under non-disclosure clauses. A single day of i-Ready professional learning support can cost $2,200. Large district deployments often spend over $100,000 on the "partner services" that make the software operate at claimed fidelity. The Harvard CEPR evaluations of DreamBox found that variation in effectiveness was driven substantially by teacher and school-level implementation practices — which means the true cost of a "working" adaptive-learning deployment includes the labor cost of the school-level management that makes it work.

ALEKS, which the seventh essai mentioned as a representative of the intelligent-tutoring-system tradition, runs at $179.95 per year for individual subscriptions, with institutional pricing that varies substantially across districts.

Duolingo, the ninth essai's anchor, operates at near-zero marginal cost per user because its revenue model is advertising and premium-tier subscription. The high-end Duolingo Max tier with GPT-4-powered features like video call tutoring runs around $30 per month to the user. The company's internal cost structure for delivering these features is proprietary, but the recent announcement that Video Call was being moved from Max to the broader Super Duolingo tier, sacrificing approximately $50 million in foregone bookings to drive user growth, reveals that the tradeoff between revenue and scale is actively managed in ways that standard cost-effectiveness accounting does not capture.

Saga Education high-dosage tutoring, the contemporary Bloom-lineage intervention I invoked earlier, runs at $3,500 to $4,800 per student per year. The effect size in Saga's randomized controlled trial literature is approximately 0.25 sigma. The sigma-per-dollar is around 0.00006 — the lowest of any intervention the book has examined, which makes Saga the least efficient intervention in the productivity-frontier sense, while also being the intervention that produces the most durable effects in the hardest-to-reach student populations. Both of these facts are simultaneously true, and the field needs to be able to hold them simultaneously rather than picking one to be "the finding."

---

I want to draw a pattern from what this walk-through reveals.

The current state of the AI-tutor literature discusses its findings as if they were points on a single axis of "effectiveness" that can be ranked against each other and against historical benchmarks. What the cost denominator reveals is that they are points on a productivity frontier — a two-dimensional space where the horizontal axis is cost-per-student and the vertical axis is sigma-produced, and where different interventions occupy different regions of the space.

Bloom's 2-sigma tutoring, at $4,000 per student, sits at high cost and high sigma. Saga's contemporary Bloom-lineage tutoring, at $3,500-$4,800 per student, sits at high cost and moderate sigma. Cognitive Tutor, at $97 per student, sits at medium-low cost and low-moderate sigma. i-Ready and DreamBox, at $22-$35 per student, sit at low cost and very low sigma, at least at the scales the evaluation literature has examined. Khan Academy, at $10 per student, sits at very low cost and very low sigma on average, with a high-dosage user subset at higher sigma. Rori, at $5 per student, sits at lowest cost and a sigma figure that is genuinely remarkable given the denominator. Kestin's Harvard research deployment sits at high per-student-cost (amortized) and high sigma, with an open question about whether the effect persists at scale.

These positions on the productivity frontier are different findings about different interventions in different contexts. The comparison across them is not a comparison of effectiveness in the way a bare sigma ranking implies. It is a comparison of where each intervention fits in a resource-effect space. A policymaker asking "what should we deploy in our district?" is asking a productivity-frontier question, because they have a budget and they have a student population and they need to know what produces the most sigma per dollar for their specific context. A sigma ranking without cost cannot answer the question.

This is where the existing methodological tradition becomes relevant, because economists of education have been building cost-effectiveness analysis tools for exactly this purpose for decades.

Henry Levin's ingredients method for cost-effectiveness analysis, developed across the 1970s and 1980s, specifies that a program's true cost is the value of all the resources required to implement it, regardless of who pays for them. A volunteer tutoring program is not actually free; its cost includes the opportunity cost of the volunteers' time and the administrative overhead to manage them. Clive Belfield and Levin's more recent work has elaborated this into a systematic methodology for ranking educational interventions by effect-per-dollar, using the ingredients method to establish true costs and then computing cost-effectiveness ratios.

Matthew Kraft's work on interpreting effect sizes, published in the mid-2020s, has argued that the field's reliance on Cohen's benchmarks for effect size interpretation — where 0.2 is "small," 0.5 is "medium," 0.8 is "large" — has led to the dismissal of interventions that are small-in-magnitude but large-in-policy-relevance. Kraft argues that effect size should be interpreted in light of three factors: the outcome measured, the study features, and the program cost. Under Kraft's schema, Khan Academy's 0.06 sigma at $10 per student may be more policy-important than a 0.5 sigma at $5,000 per student, because the former is deployable at scale across entire school systems while the latter can only reach a small subset of students. This is not a claim that the smaller effect is "better" in the psychological sense. It is a claim that policy decisions require cost-normalized comparison, and that raw sigma rankings cannot provide it.

The Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab — J-PAL — founded by Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo, whose work on cost-effective educational interventions in developing countries earned them the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2019, has been conducting cost-inclusive randomized controlled trials of educational interventions for nearly two decades. Their reporting convention uses "additional years of schooling gained per $100 spent" or "standard deviations gained per $100" as the reportable outcome. This is cost-inclusive efficacy reporting as standard practice. It has transformed the study of educational intervention in developing-country contexts. It has not transformed the study of educational technology in high-resource contexts, which is the observation the essai's closing will return to.

---

I want to name why cost has been systematically excluded from EdTech efficacy reporting, because the pattern is not an accident.

The exclusion has three specific features that the apparatus makes visible.

First, cost is typically reported in methods appendices rather than in findings sections, which means citations of the research typically strip the cost out. A paper that reports an effect size of 0.3 sigma and, in its methods appendix, notes that the intervention cost $50 per student per year, will be cited as reporting a 0.3 sigma effect. The $50 will not appear in the citation. The finding that circulates is the numerator, not the fraction.

Second, EdTech industry communications — investor presentations, philanthropic program documents, district-facing marketing — almost never report effect size normalized by cost. They report effect size and they report cost, but they do not compute or present sigma-per-dollar. The omission is rhetorical. An intervention at $50 per student producing 0.3 sigma looks more impressive when sigma is presented alone than when sigma-per-dollar is presented alongside. The communication formats optimize for the presentation of sigma, which means the cost information, when it appears, appears in ways that do not support direct comparison.

Third, the academic research conventions in educational psychology — the discipline that has dominated EdTech efficacy research — have historically not required cost reporting as a condition of publication. The Every Student Succeeds Act's evidence tiers include some consideration of implementation context, but cost-inclusive effect-size reporting is not a condition of meeting the tier standards. The What Works Clearinghouse has faced sustained criticism for its cost-blindness and has begun to explore cost-inclusive reporting, but the data remains sparse and relies heavily on publisher-reported pricing rather than independent audit.

These three features — appendix burial, rhetorical framing, discipline-level convention — together produce the pattern the book has been examining in various forms across the prior eleven essais. The cost-blind comparison is not a mistake by individual researchers. It is a structural feature of the measurement apparatus that the field has inherited and continues to operate under. Changing the pattern requires changing the reporting conventions, the publication standards, the citation conventions, and the discourse norms of the field simultaneously. This is a large ask, and it is the ask the essai's closing will articulate specifically.

---

Here is what I would like you to carry out of this essai.

When you next encounter an educational-technology efficacy claim — in a paper, a press release, an investor pitch, a philanthropic report — ask a specific sequence of questions.

The first: what is the effect size, and what conditions produced it?

The second: what did the intervention cost to produce that effect, measured honestly — not as list price, not as marginal cost to the school, but as the all-in cost of the resources the intervention deployed? Include the development costs amortized across the user base. Include the teacher training and support costs. Include the infrastructure the intervention assumes is already in place. Include the opportunity cost of the time the intervention takes from alternatives. The ingredients method, as Levin specified it, is the discipline for this accounting.

The third: what is the sigma-per-dollar? Compute the fraction explicitly. Do not accept the bare sigma as the reportable finding.

The fourth: where does this intervention sit on the productivity frontier relative to alternatives at matched cost? Would the same dollars spent on a different intervention produce more, less, or comparable sigma? This is the comparison that matters for policy and deployment decisions.

The fifth: what does the cost-normalized comparison reveal that the raw sigma comparison concealed? In my experience with the cases in this essai, the cost-normalized comparison almost always surfaces something the raw sigma did not: that an expensive intervention has purchased its effect rather than discovered it, or that a cheap intervention has achieved something that a fair comparison would credit more than the field typically does, or that two interventions the field treats as competitors are actually doing fundamentally different kinds of work at different points in the resource-effect space.

These questions will not resolve every comparison cleanly. The productivity frontier is a two-dimensional space, not a single ranking, and two interventions at different positions may both be defensible choices in different contexts. But the questions move the comparison from the sigma-ranking mode — where the field has been making claims it cannot defend — to the productivity-frontier mode, where the claims the field can actually defend are the ones worth making.

The thirteenth essai of this volume, which is the essai that follows this one, makes the deepest analytical claim the book has been building toward: that the measure-construct distinction, applied throughout the book's prior cases, is the single most important analytical tool for reading EdTech claims honestly. Cost, which I have treated in this essai as an epistemic variable, is one specific application of that deeper distinction. The sigma a measurement produces is one specific operationalization; the learning it purports to index is the construct. The cost a deployment consumes is the specific resource the measurement's operational conditions required; the educational productivity the deployment purports to embody is the construct. Reading carefully, at all the relevant layers, is what the thirteenth essai asks for explicitly. I hope you will continue with me.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai is the first of Part IV's "Deeper Concealments," and its relationship to the prior eleven essais is retrospective rather than novel. The essai re-examines cases the book has already examined, adding a dimension (cost) the earlier essais mentioned but did not center. I want to name where I am uncertain whether the retrospective move has worked.

I am not sure I have given each case its full complexity. Bloom's cost structure, Kestin's cost structure, Khan Academy's cost structure — each could be examined at greater length than the essai has allowed, and each contains uncertainties that the essai has compressed for the sake of the larger argument. The estimates I have offered for Kestin's all-in per-student cost are order-of-magnitude estimates drawn from what the study permits, not primary-source cost accounting. The Saga figure and the Rori figure are primary-source but the comparability between them is not fully specified. A reader who works specifically on one of these cases may find the treatment too compressed. The alternative is to extend the essai substantially, which would shift it away from the synthesis move it is trying to make.

I am not sure the sigma-per-dollar calculations are presented in a way that guards against misuse. The calculations are arithmetically straightforward, but their interpretation depends on the baselines and populations and timescales the underlying studies operate under — all of which the essai's prior chapters documented but this essai compresses. A reader who uses the sigma-per-dollar figures as rankings without the prior chapters' context for each case would misunderstand what the comparison supports. The essai is trying to model the right use of these figures, not the wrong one, but the distinction is fragile.

I am not sure the essai is fair to the field. The characterization of "cost-blindness as rhetorical move" places the field's conventions in a specific interpretive light — that the omission of cost is structurally produced by incentives that reward the omission. This framing is defensible but also critical, and a reader who believes the field's conventions are the natural result of different analytical priorities rather than structural bias may find the critical framing overstated. I have tried to note that the exclusion is structural rather than intentional, but the line between structural critique and moral critique can be delicate.

Finally, I am not sure the essai holds the Montaignian register well. The density of specific figures — dollar amounts, sigma values, sigma-per-dollar fractions — is higher than in most prior essais, and the essai's tone is more quantitative than the volume's characteristic voice. I have tried to keep the voice provisional throughout, acknowledging uncertainties, framing the analysis as what the apparatus reveals rather than as what must be concluded. But the essai approaches the form of a cost-effectiveness analysis more closely than any prior essai, and a reader for whom the Montaignian register is the reason to read the book may find this essai more technical than the register accommodates. If the balance between the analytical apparatus and the essai form slips in places, I would like to know where.

The next essai is the volume's synthesis moment — the deepest claim the book has been building toward about the measure-construct distinction, and the closest the volume comes to stating its central analytical tool plainly. I hope you will continue with me.
