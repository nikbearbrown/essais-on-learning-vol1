# On When the Tool Underperforms

*Essai 11*

---

In April of 2023, Sal Khan stood on a TED stage and described the moment we had arrived at: "We are at the cusp of using AI for probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen." The specific framing of what this would look like followed: "an amazing personal tutor for every student on the planet." Three years later, in April of 2026, he sat for an interview with Matt Barnum of *Chalkbeat* titled *Why Sal Khan's AI revolution hasn't happened yet* and described Khanmigo's impact on most students differently. Khanmigo, he said, had been "a non-event." Kristen DiCerbo, Khan Academy's Chief Learning Officer, said in the same interview: "So far I am not seeing the revolution in education." She offered the specific observation that gave the statement its evidentiary weight: the analysis of student-bot conversations was returning more "IDK IDK" interactions — students typing *I don't know, I don't know* in response to the Socratic prompts — than substantive engagement.

Between those two public moments, three years of institutional behavior unfolded: pilot programs at Hobart High School and elsewhere, the 2023-2024 annual report documenting 221,200 users, Sal Khan's May 2024 publication of *Brave New Words: How AI Will Revolutionize Education (and Why That's a Good Thing)*, the 2024-2025 annual report documenting "explosive growth" to 2.0 million global users, the Yearly Proficient Learner metric defining success as students mastering 60 or more skills per year, the 18-hours-of-Khanmigo-use-per-school-year dosage recommendation, Michigan Virtual pilot findings that teacher tools were "surprisingly helpful" while student engagement remained "curious but complicated," and the gradual accumulation of evidence that the 2023 framing had not held. What Khan Academy said across those three years, and how what they said changed as the evidence arrived, is the subject of this essai.

The essai's specific question is this: what does the learning-systems field do when its tools publicly underperform the claims that launched them? The question matters because underperformance is ordinary in research. Interventions rarely work exactly as their designers hope. Effects are smaller than expected, populations engage differently than predicted, contexts resist deployment. This is unremarkable. What is remarkable is the pattern of rhetorical moves the field has developed to manage the gap between claim and evidence — a pattern that recurs with specific linguistic signatures across every generation of educational technology and that, with specific attention, becomes legible as a genre.

The Khan Academy arc is the essai's anchor because it spans three years publicly, because both its opening and its closing are documented with specific quotations, and because Khan Academy made an unusual choice at the end of the arc that the essai treats as instructive. Most organizations do not announce, at the end of a three-year underperformance arc, that their revolutionary product has been a non-event for most students. They deploy one of the six rhetorical moves the essai is about to examine. Khan Academy, in April 2026, did not fully deploy them. DiCerbo's and Khan's willingness to describe what they had observed, in the language they used to describe it, is what makes the arc instructive rather than just another example of the pattern. The essai credits this specifically, and uses the contrast between what Khan Academy said and what the field typically says to clarify what the typical moves are.

I want to be explicit about what this essai is and is not doing. It is not arguing that Khan Academy failed, or that Khanmigo was a fraud, or that AI tutoring is a scam. It is not arguing that claims-that-don't-survive-evidence are evidence of bad faith. Many people who make large claims about what their work will accomplish are sincere. The claims often don't survive because research is hard and the world is complicated, not because the claimants were dishonest. What the essai examines is the set of rhetorical responses available when the evidence comes in below the claim — the specific moves the field has made, across many technologies and many decades, to protect the original claim when evidence moves against it.

---

Let me name the six moves. I will characterize each in its specific linguistic form, document Khanmigo-specific instances where they have appeared, and draw on comparable historical cases to establish each as a field-wide pattern rather than a Khan Academy idiosyncrasy.

The first move is **implementation blame**. Its linguistic signature is the shift of attribution from the tool to the conditions of deployment. *The schools that implemented well saw strong effects; the average effect is smaller because some schools implemented poorly. Inconsistent fidelity across districts is what produced mixed results. Classroom culture varies; teachers need more professional development to get the full benefit.* The move reframes underperformance as an environmental problem rather than a product problem. The tool works; the schools don't.

This move has real empirical grounding, which is part of what makes it rhetorically durable. The Pane RAND 2014 evaluation of Cognitive Tutor found exactly the pattern the move invokes — no effect in year one, a statistically significant effect in year two attributable to school-level learning about how to deploy the system. Implementation does matter. Schools do get better at using tools with time. The move's legitimacy in some cases is what makes it deployable in others. When Sal Khan in April 2026 compared Khanmigo to a tutor sitting in the back of the room waiting to be asked for help — "Some will; most won't," in his phrasing — he was performing a version of implementation blame. The AI was ready; the classroom conditions did not bring students to it. This is not necessarily wrong. It may also not be the full explanation. The move's fingerprint is that it leaves the tool's claim intact while attributing any gap in evidence to what happened after the tool arrived.

The second move is **user blame**. The attribution shifts further, from the deployment conditions to the people using the tool. *Students aren't ready for the Socratic method. Teachers lack the digital fluency the tool requires. Users don't know how to prompt well. Self-regulated learners benefit; those who lack self-regulation don't.* The linguistic signature is the diagnostic framing of users — the description of what users can or cannot do — positioned as explanation for why the tool did not produce what was claimed.

DiCerbo's statement that "students aren't great at asking questions well" is a specific example. The observation is empirically accurate — students, in Khan Academy's dialogue data, often did not engage substantively with Khanmigo's prompts, and the "IDK IDK" pattern is the specific residue of that non-engagement. The question is what the observation is doing rhetorically. Used as diagnosis — *this is what we observe, this is what the apparatus needs to address* — the observation is analytically productive. Used as deflection — *the revolution didn't happen because students aren't prepared for it* — the observation protects the original claim by relocating its failure in the users rather than in the tool or the design. The difference between diagnosis and deflection is subtle in linguistic form. It is often detectable in what follows the observation: does the speaker then take responsibility for designing for the observed user behavior, or does the speaker attribute the tool's shortfall to the users and let the original claim stand?

The MOOC era provides a clean historical example of user blame at scale. Early MOOC claims — Sebastian Thrun predicting in 2012 that only ten universities would remain in fifty years — did not survive first-year completion data showing retention rates in the single digits. The rhetorical response across the MOOC research literature between 2013 and 2015 was that MOOCs worked for students with appropriate *self-regulation* and that low completion rates reflected insufficient self-regulatory capacity rather than the unsuitability of the massive one-to-many format. The move did real rhetorical work; it preserved the MOOC value proposition for a time by narrowing who the proposition was about. It also eventually failed to hold, as the self-regulation framing itself became a signal that the original universal-access-to-elite-instruction promise had been quietly abandoned.

The third move is **UX friction attribution**. The tool's underperformance is attributed to interface problems, onboarding experience, or product-design issues that the next iteration will address. *The next version has a better conversation flow. The onboarding has been redesigned based on user feedback. Help-seeking prompts are being refined. New engagement features are in development.* The linguistic signature is the invocation of a future product version whose improvements will close the gap between the original claim and current evidence.

Khan Academy's public communications across 2024 and 2025 contain specific examples. Product updates referenced "Help Me Focus" tools, "Lesson Hooks," refined conversational flows, and improved teacher-facing dashboards as mechanisms that would address observed engagement shortfalls. These product updates were not fraudulent; the organization was genuinely iterating on the tool. What makes the move rhetorical is that the *original claim* — the revolutionary transformation of education — was positioned as achievable once the UX friction was resolved. If the framing holds, no amount of evidence can falsify the original claim: any shortfall can always be attributed to friction the next version will address. The claim is insulated from evidence by an indefinite series of forthcoming improvements.

This move was particularly visible in the adaptive-learning era. Knewton's public communications across 2014 through 2018 routinely invoked the next version of its adaptive engine — the one that would finally deliver the psychometric-profile-of-every-student promise that earlier versions had not delivered. Jose Ferreira, Knewton's founder, had described the system as a "robot tutor in the sky" that could "semi-read your mind" to produce a custom learning experience for each student. Each iteration was framed as approaching this vision; none actually produced it. When the forthcoming version finally arrived without producing the promised effects, the move simply moved to the next forthcoming version, until Knewton's 2019 sale to Wiley for substantially less than its total venture funding ended the arc without ever arriving at the final version. The sale itself was rhetorically framed as a "strategic acquisition" rather than as a recognition that the original claim had not held.

The fourth move is **extending timelines**. The claim is not retracted; its time horizon is extended. *Year one was about adoption; year two will show impact. We are playing the long game. The full effects will emerge over multiple years of institutional learning. Students who start now will see compound benefits through middle school.* The linguistic signature is the reframing of the original claim from "what we're doing now" to "what we're working toward."

This move has legitimate versions and rhetorical versions, and the distinction matters. The Pane RAND 2014 Cognitive Tutor study, as already mentioned, documented a real second-year effect that was not visible in year one. This is an empirically supported version of timeline extension — the claim "effects may emerge in year two" can be true, based on specific evidence about how schools and teachers adapt to new instructional tools. In this legitimate form, the timeline extension is accompanied by a specific mechanism: what is the tool doing between year one and year two that produces the delayed effect? What are the schools learning? What is the evidence that this learning is occurring?

The rhetorical version strips the mechanism. It simply asserts that effects will emerge later, without specifying what needs to change to produce them or what evidence would falsify the prediction. Khan Academy's April 2026 pivot toward "investing in human systems alongside the tool" has legitimate features — human-system integration is a real variable, and classrooms where teachers actively scaffold AI-tool use do produce different outcomes than classrooms where students encounter AI tools alone. The move becomes rhetorical if the timeline extension is presented without the specific theory of how human-system investment produces the transformation that Khanmigo's three-year arc did not produce. Without such a theory, "investing in human systems" becomes an indefinite deferral — a next horizon that can always be pushed further when the current one fails to produce what was claimed.

The fifth move is **narrowing the claim post-hoc**. The original claim is revised to match current evidence, with the revision framed as if it had been the claim all along. *Khanmigo was always intended as a teaching assistant, not a replacement for teachers. The tool's primary value has always been in supporting teachers with administrative tasks. We never claimed every student would become a Rhodes scholar; we claimed to expand access to personalized support.* The linguistic signature is the recasting of what was said in the past to align with what present evidence supports.

The Khanmigo arc contains clean examples. The 2023 framing was about transformation of education on a civilizational scale — "an amazing personal tutor for every student on the planet," "the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen." By 2025 and into 2026, the public framing had shifted substantially toward Khanmigo as a "teaching assistant" — a "sidekick for teachers" that helped with lesson planning, rubric generation, and administrative support, and that provided supplementary help to students who actively sought it. *Brave New Words*, published in May 2024, already contained the narrower framing, describing Khanmigo as a tool that would "unstick" students during independent practice — a meaningfully different claim from the 2-sigma tutoring revolution of April 2023. The narrowing can be framed honestly — *we have learned that the teaching-assistant use case is where the tool produces its most reliable value* — or it can be framed as if the narrower claim was always the claim, which relocates the original transformation framing as either a misunderstanding by the audience or an aspirational frame that was never meant literally.

The ITS tradition, which Essai 7 of this volume examined, provides the paradigmatic historical case of post-hoc claim narrowing in EdTech. ITS was launched with the expectation that it would approach Bloom's 2-sigma human-tutoring benchmark. When VanLehn's 2011 meta-analysis showed ITS producing effects in the 0.76 sigma range — less than half of Bloom's target — the field's rhetoric narrowed accordingly. ITS, it was now said, had always been intended to support homework and seatwork rather than to replace classroom instruction. It worked within its "limited area of expertise." The narrowing was gradual, deniable at each individual step, and ultimately decisive. The claim that ITS would approach human tutoring was retired, and the claim that ITS would supplement classroom instruction took its place, without any moment of explicit retraction.

The sixth move is **shifting the standard of comparison**. When the tool does not outperform the gold-standard baseline, the baseline is changed. *Khanmigo performs comparably to Google Search. Our tool produces effects similar to other widely-used EdTech interventions. Compared to no intervention at all, the tool produces meaningful gains.* The linguistic signature is the introduction of a new comparator that the tool can outperform, replacing the original comparator against which it could not.

Recent research on Khanmigo has begun invoking Google Search as a comparator — students using Khanmigo perform comparably to students using Google Search for homework help, with students reporting they "perceived it positively." This is a meaningful finding; Google Search is a widely-available tool, and a dedicated tutor producing comparable outcomes has a specific value proposition. It is also not the comparator the 2023 framing invoked. The 2023 claim was that Khanmigo would approach expert human tutoring — the 2-sigma benchmark Bloom established and that Khan himself invoked in the TED framing. The 2026 comparator is a free search engine. The two are not on the same axis. Moving between them is a rhetorical move, and the audience for the move often does not notice that the comparator has shifted.

This move also recurs in the ITS history. When ITS failed to reach Bloom's 2-sigma level of expert human tutoring, the field did not retract the comparison; it reframed it. Two moves were performed in parallel. First, the ITS effects were repositioned as "comparable to human tutoring" on the grounds that most human tutors were not actually expert tutors performing at 2 sigma. Second, the comparison itself was repositioned — ITS was now compared against no-tutoring control conditions rather than against expert human tutoring, and the smaller sigma against the weaker comparison was presented as the relevant finding. Both moves shifted the standard of comparison downward. Both preserved the rhetorical viability of ITS claims without requiring retraction of what had originally been claimed.

---

There is a seventh move, which the essai has been enacting by describing the first six, and which is the field's most honest response pattern, though it is the rarest. It is the move Khan Academy made in April 2026. The move is to describe the gap between claim and evidence in plain language, without performing the six deflections, and to credit that gap as real rather than as a problem to be rhetorically managed.

DiCerbo's statement — "So far I am not seeing the revolution in education" — is an example of this move. So is Sal Khan's description of Khanmigo as a "non-event" for most students. So is the *Chalkbeat* interview's title: *Why Sal Khan's AI revolution hasn't happened yet*. The statements are striking in the context of the six rhetorical moves because they do not perform any of them. They do not blame implementation, users, or UX. They do not extend the timeline or narrow the claim to match current evidence. They do not shift the comparator. They state what the evidence shows, in language the audience can understand, and let the statement stand.

The comparison case in EdTech history that most closely parallels this move is Sebastian Thrun's 2014 statement about Udacity. After MOOC completion rates proved what they proved, Thrun said publicly that Udacity had "a lousy product, one that did not provide a good service to [its] students." This is a specific kind of acknowledgment — the founder of a heavily-funded, much-publicized educational-technology company saying out loud that his own product had not worked. The 2014 Thrun moment was followed, within months, by a narrowing of Udacity's scope — pivot to corporate vocational training, abandonment of the degree-replacement framing — and the organization's subsequent trajectory did not hold to the honesty of the original acknowledgment. But the moment itself was real, and it had the same structure as DiCerbo's and Khan's moment in April 2026: the person most responsible for the claim describes the claim's failure to hold in the language of the failure rather than in the language of the rhetorical deflection.

This is the move the essai wants to credit specifically. Most organizations do not make it when their tools underperform. The institutional incentives run strongly the other direction: philanthropic funders, district partners, media coverage, Microsoft's cloud-computing credits, and organizational reputation all reward the performance of the six moves and penalize the honest description that Khan Academy provided. DiCerbo and Khan chose, publicly, to describe what they had seen, knowing the implications for Khan Academy's position in the AI-tutor conversation. This is a disciplinary move in the sense the whole volume is concerned with. It is the move the book's form is trying to enact — specific findings held openly, without the rhetorical machinery that protects claims from evidence.

The credit does not need to be unqualified. Khan Academy continues to operate Khanmigo, and the organization's April 2026 pivot toward "human systems integration" has legitimate features but could become an indefinite-deferral move if not accompanied by specific theory and evidence. What happens after April 2026 — whether Khan Academy continues to describe the evidence honestly as it accumulates, whether the organization commits to the kinds of measurement practices Essai 10 specified as necessary, whether Khanmigo continues or retires — will determine how the full arc ultimately reads. But the April 2026 moment was itself significant. An organization with every reason to deploy the six rhetorical moves chose, for a single interview, to describe what was happening without them. This is worth naming.

---

I want to name the other substantial act of honesty from April 2026, which came from inside the EdTech industry rather than from inside Khan Academy. Dan Meyer, the mathematics-education researcher who now serves as Chief Academic Officer at Amplify, published a piece in April 2026 titled *RIP Khanmigo & Edtech Industry Dreams of AI Tutors*. Meyer's piece is significant because it offers an industry-level diagnosis of the Khanmigo arc from a position that is inside the EdTech industry rather than outside it — Meyer is a product leader at a competitor, not an academic critic writing from a neutral stance.

Meyer's core observation is what he calls the "silver spoon" argument. Khanmigo, he notes, had every advantage the EdTech industry could offer: early OpenAI access before competitors could reach GPT-4 at scale, Microsoft backing on cloud computing, Sal Khan's existing brand recognition and public platform, the goodwill of decades of Khan Academy's genuinely good work on free educational content. If a product with every conceivable advantage could only achieve "non-event" status, Meyer argues, the problem lies with the underlying theory — the theory that an AI chatbot can serve as the personal tutor Bloom's 2-sigma evidence pointed toward — rather than with any specific organization's execution.

Meyer's second observation concerns what he calls the *Ouroboros of Tech* — the pattern of using technology solutions to address problems that were created by earlier technology solutions. Khanmigo was positioned as the answer to the problems of self-paced digital learning, problems that earlier waves of educational technology had themselves introduced. The pattern, Meyer argues, prevents the field from engaging with the underlying socioeconomic factors that actually shape learning outcomes — teacher support, student motivation, institutional coherence, neighborhood stability, family resources. A technology that addresses a problem another technology created is often not addressing the actual problem.

Meyer's third observation is specific and worth noting: he describes the "increasingly aggressive way Khanmigo inserted itself into the student experience" across the 2024-2026 deployment period, pushing prompts and nudges and reminders on students who had not initiated engagement. The aggressive insertion, Meyer argues, was itself a rhetorical attempt to force engagement where there was no student purpose — to produce the metric of use even when the underlying behavior that would generate learning was absent. The move is what happens when a tool has to appear to be working because its founding claim requires that it work, even when the actual interactional evidence suggests that it is not.

I quote Meyer because his piece is a specific kind of April 2026 document — not the institutional acknowledgment of Khan Academy itself, but the industry-level diagnosis of what the Khan Academy acknowledgment means for the broader EdTech industry. Together, the two April 2026 pieces — the *Chalkbeat* interview and Meyer's *RIP Khanmigo* — form a diagnostic moment in the field. The question is what happens in the subsequent months and years: whether the diagnostic moment becomes a genuine turning point in how the EdTech industry handles efficacy claims, or whether it is absorbed into the next hype cycle without substantive change.

---

I want to dwell on one further distinction before this essai closes, because it is crucial to how the essai should be read.

Each of the six moves has a legitimate version and a rhetorical version. Implementation does matter; some schools genuinely implement better than others, and effects depend on it. Users do vary; students with different levels of self-regulation engage with tools differently, and that variation is real. UX does affect outcomes; good interfaces produce different results than bad ones. Timelines do sometimes extend; some educational interventions produce delayed effects. Claims do sometimes refine appropriately; understanding a tool's specific value is what careful research does. Comparison standards do sometimes change appropriately; the right comparator for a given deployment may become clearer as the deployment evolves.

The distinction between legitimate and rhetorical uses of each move is what the essai is trying to teach the reader to draw. The distinction is not always clean in any single instance, but it has markers that become recognizable with practice.

The legitimate version of each move is accompanied by three features: a specific mechanism connecting the move to the outcome it explains; a specification of what evidence would falsify the move's claim; and an engagement with the implications of the move for the original claim, including potential retraction of the original claim if the move does not hold up. The rhetorical version typically lacks one or more of these features. It invokes the move without specifying how it works, what would disprove it, or whether the original claim would have to change if the move turns out to be wrong.

The Pane RAND 2014 Cognitive Tutor finding is a legitimate implementation-timeline move because Pane's team specified the mechanism (school-level institutional learning about deployment), specified what evidence would falsify the prediction (no effect in year two would have falsified the first-year-learning-curve claim), and engaged with the implications (if year two had also shown no effect, the first-year null finding would have constituted the complete finding, not a partial one). DiCerbo's "IDK IDK" observation is, similarly, a legitimate user-engagement observation because it described what was specifically observed, invited a diagnostic response rather than a defensive one, and was accompanied by DiCerbo's acknowledgment that the original claim had not held.

The rhetorical versions of these moves often share a diagnostic linguistic signature: they tend to be offered without the three features above. Implementation blame without mechanism — *what specifically* about the poor implementations produced the failure? Timeline extension without falsifier — *what evidence* in year three would finally show the move was wrong? Claim narrowing without engagement — *how does the narrower claim relate* to what was said at the beginning? When a move is offered without these features, it is probably performing rhetorical work rather than analytical work.

---

Before the closing, a brief observation about why these rhetorical moves are used so consistently across the EdTech industry. The answer is not primarily about individual character or organizational virtue. It is about institutional incentives.

EdTech organizations — whether non-profits like Khan Academy or venture-backed companies like Knewton — depend on sustained narratives of transformative impact to maintain their funding environments. Philanthropic funders, investors, district partners, and media coverage all reward confident claims about impact and penalize acknowledgments of underperformance. Microsoft's cloud-computing credits, partnerships with OpenAI, district purchasing decisions, teacher adoption rates — all are shaped by the narrative the organization can sustain. An organization that publicly acknowledges that its revolutionary product is a non-event is an organization whose next funding cycle, next district partnership, next media appearance will be harder to secure. The incentives do not reward honesty; they reward the maintenance of the narrative.

What Cass Sunstein has called "availability entrepreneurs" — actors whose institutional position depends on their ability to sustain attention to a particular issue or claim — face specific pressure to continue promoting the claim even as evidence moves against it. For Khan Academy, Khanmigo is not merely a product; it is the organization's claim on the AI-in-education conversation. Retreating from that claim has consequences that extend beyond the specific evaluation of the specific tool. The rhetorical moves are not failures of character; they are responses to a structure that rewards their deployment. Which is why honest acknowledgments like April 2026's are rare, and why they matter when they do occur.

Larry Cuban documented this pattern across the full twentieth-century history of educational technology in *Oversold and Underused* (2001). Each wave of technology — radio, instructional television, computer-assisted instruction, educational computing, online learning — followed the same cycle: inflated initial claims, deployment gap between promise and practice, rhetorical management of the gap through implementation blame or user blame or claim narrowing, and eventual replacement by the next wave without substantive resolution of whether the previous wave had worked. The pattern Khanmigo's 2023-2026 arc exemplifies is not new. It is, as the historical precedents this essai has documented demonstrate, the EdTech field's ordinary response to disappointing evidence. What is unusual is the specific April 2026 moment when Khan Academy resisted the pattern and named the gap honestly.

---

Here is what I would like you to carry out of this essai.

When you next encounter an EdTech organization responding to underperformance of its claims — in an annual report, in a press interview, in an academic publication, in a product-update announcement — you have a diagnostic apparatus for the response. Watch for the six moves. Notice which ones appear. Notice whether the move is accompanied by the three features that distinguish its legitimate form from its rhetorical form: specific mechanism, specified falsifier, engagement with implications for the original claim.

If a response performs the six moves without the three features, the response is doing rhetorical work. The organization is protecting its claim from evidence. This is not necessarily dishonest; it is structurally rewarded by the institutional incentives of the EdTech industry. But it is also not evidence that the original claim is being honestly revised in light of what the research has shown. A reader who recognizes the move can calibrate their belief in the organization's current framing accordingly.

If a response instead performs the seventh move — the honest description of the gap between claim and evidence, without deployment of the six deflections — the response is doing something different, and something rarer. It is the kind of response Khan Academy offered in April 2026. It is the response Thrun offered in 2014 before Udacity's subsequent trajectory moved in other directions. It is the response the book is trying to model in its own form, across every essai. It is not a move that is guaranteed to produce good outcomes for the organization that makes it; it may well be punished by funders and partners who prefer the maintained narrative. But it is the response that makes the organization's subsequent claims more rather than less credible. An organization that can describe its current failures openly is an organization whose next claim can be evaluated against its track record of honesty rather than against a track record of protected claims.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai is the volume's most delicate balance: characterizing a rhetorical pattern that the Khan Academy arc exemplifies, while also crediting Khan Academy specifically for an honesty that distinguishes their April 2026 response from the typical pattern. I want to name where I am uncertain whether this balance holds.

I am not sure I have given Khan Academy enough credit. The April 2026 acknowledgment was genuinely unusual in the field. DiCerbo and Khan, as senior organizational figures with substantial institutional interests at stake, chose to describe what they had seen in language that did not perform the six rhetorical moves. This is a significant disciplinary act, and the essai names it as such. But the essai also uses the Khanmigo arc as the anchor for a broader critique of the rhetorical pattern, which means the arc is both the book's best example of the honest response and the book's vehicle for examining the dishonest ones. A reader sympathetic to Khan Academy might say the essai's dual use of the arc undercuts the credit, even when the credit is explicitly given. If this concern lands, tell me where.

I am not sure about the boundary between legitimate and rhetorical versions of each move. The three features I specified — mechanism, falsifier, engagement with implications — are a plausible set of markers, but they are not an algorithm. In any specific case, the reader has to judge whether the mechanism is genuinely explanatory or whether it is post-hoc; whether the falsifier is genuinely specified or whether it is an indefinite future; whether the engagement with implications is substantive or whether it is defensive. The essai provides the framework; the reader does the application. A reader who finds the framework insufficient — who thinks the legitimate-rhetorical distinction requires more than three markers, or different markers — has a legitimate methodological concern.

I am not sure about the historical precedents. The essai treats Knewton, MOOCs, ITS, and Cuban's documentation of earlier waves as comparable cases with the six moves appearing across them. This is compressed historical analysis; a longer treatment of any one of these cases would produce a fuller picture. The MOOC disillusionment period in particular is rich with specific examples of user blame and claim narrowing that the essai treats briefly. A reader who works specifically on one of these historical moments may find the treatment too thin. The alternative is to extend the essai substantially, which the volume's length constraints do not accommodate.

I am not sure about the Dan Meyer passage. Meyer's piece is specific and pointed, and the essai quotes his core observations directly. But Meyer is, as the essai notes, a product leader at a competitor to Khan Academy. A reader might reasonably ask whether his critique carries the same weight as a critique from a neutral academic observer, or whether the essai's use of Meyer risks importing his commercial positioning into the book's register. I have kept the Meyer passage because his industry-internal perspective offers something a neutral observer could not — a specific characterization of what the Khanmigo arc looks like from inside the EdTech industry itself. But the judgment is contestable.

Finally, I am not sure the essai avoids sounding cynical about the field. The risk is that a careful description of six rhetorical moves, across many organizations and many decades, lands as a claim that the entire EdTech field is operating in bad faith. This is not the essai's argument. The argument is that the field has developed a specific set of rhetorical responses to disappointing evidence, that these responses have both legitimate and rhetorical versions, that the rhetorical versions are often deployed even by well-meaning people because institutional incentives favor them, and that honest responses like Khan Academy's April 2026 acknowledgment are significant precisely because they resist the pressure to deploy the moves. A reader who finds the essai drifting toward cynicism — toward a blanket "EdTech claims are all false" framing — should tell me. The essai is trying to describe a pattern, not to condemn a field.

The next essai examines what the claims have cost to produce, and what cost-blind comparison conceals. I hope you will continue with me.
