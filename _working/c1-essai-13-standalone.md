# /c1 — Essai 13 Standalone Specification

## *On What We Measure and What We Care About*

### Why this essai gets its own artifact

Essai 13 is the book's deepest essai. It carries the Integrate-level posture shift at the book level — the distinction between a measure and what the measure is a proxy for, held as an active working tool in every future efficacy claim the reader encounters. It is the essai that, done well, makes the rest of the book retroactively more valuable; done poorly, it makes the book feel like twelve close readings without a unifying framework.

The essai has three structural jobs that no other essai carries alone:

1. **It must name at the deepest level what the first twelve essais have been circling.** Every specific close reading the book performed — Bloom's measure of aligned-item mastery, ITS's measure of step-level response patterns, adaptive learning's measure of item sequencing, engagement as usage proxy, cost as hidden denominator — is an instance of one deeper pattern: the gap between what the field's apparatus measures and what the field claims learning systems are about. Essai 13 makes that pattern visible as a pattern, not as the twelfth instance.

2. **It must carry the book's constructive moment without prescribing.** The Revise-level posture shifts pointed here: the reader can articulate what more adequate apparatus would provisionally require. But Thesis A committed to the Montaignian register — examination, not reform. The essai holds both. It names what better evidence would require, provisionally, while refusing the reformer's voice.

3. **It must be the essai that makes the book portable.** A reader who has finished Essai 12 has a set of moves — close-reading techniques for specific claim types. A reader who has finished Essai 13 has a framework that generalizes beyond the specific claim types. The generalization is what makes the book useful in five years when the specific products and studies are different.

The essai is high-risk and high-reward. Getting it right is what makes the book the book the reader hoped for. Getting it wrong produces a book that feels as if its ambitions exceeded its delivery at the last moment.

### 1. Number, working title, one-line descriptor

**Essai 13. *On What We Measure and What We Care About*.** The reader comes to hold the distinction between a measure and what the measure is a proxy for as the book's central analytical tool — recognizing the distinction across every claim the book examined, applying it to claims the book did not examine, and articulating provisionally what more adequate evidence would require.

### 2. Keyword audit + title justification

Passes all three tests.

*Justification:* The title states the essai's subject cleanly. *What We Measure* names the apparatus's output; *What We Care About* names the construct the measurements purport to be evidence for; the conjunction names the gap. The title is plainer than some essai titles (*On the Afterlife of the Teaching Machine*, *On What Examination Licenses*) because the essai's subject is structural and benefits from directness. No metaphor, no irony, no flourish — just the distinction the essai operationalizes.

### 3. The problem this essai solves for the reader

After twelve essais, the reader has watched the apparatus applied to particulars. They have a collection of specific readings. What they do not yet have — what the spiral has been preparing them to see, essai by essai — is the single deepest observation the book has been building toward: *every concealment the apparatus performs is an instance of one deeper concealment, which is the distance between measurement and construct.* The reader needs to see this deepest observation clearly, once, explicitly. Without this essai, the reader leaves with techniques. With it, they leave with a framework that generalizes — to learning systems the book did not cover, to educational technology that does not yet exist, to research fields other than education where the same structure recurs.

The essai's failure mode is that it feels like a summary. It is not a summary — the twelve prior essais do not sum to the thirteenth. They *converge on* it. The essai names the convergence point and the structural observation the convergence reveals, which is different from collecting what the earlier essais showed.

### 4. Posture shifts (from `/l1`)

1. **Integrate:** The reader holds the distinction between a measure and what the measure is a proxy for as an active working tool in every efficacy claim they encounter from this point forward.
2. **Disassemble:** The reader can name the specific proxy relationship in a given efficacy claim — *"this item-level performance measure is a proxy for durable transferable understanding, and the proxy relationship is assumed, not demonstrated"* — and can explain what would be required to establish it rather than assume it.
3. **Articulate:** The reader understands why the field's claim-making vocabulary (*effectiveness, mastery, learning*) outruns what its evidence apparatus can support.
4. **Revise:** The reader can articulate what a more adequate apparatus would provisionally require — multiple outcome measures, converging evidence across paradigms, durability testing, transfer testing, honest cost reporting — even while accepting that no one study will implement all of it.

This is the only essai delivering Integrate as its highest level (together with Essai 14, the closing). The Integrate move is what makes the book's work portable beyond the specific cases it examined.

### 5. Opening strategy

The essai's opening is its hardest craft problem. The essai cannot open on a single specific claim — it is not reading one thing, it is naming what all the previous readings share. But it also cannot open abstractly, without a concrete anchor, or it reads as philosophy rather than as examination.

The solution: open on **the gap itself, named through a single comparison**. Best move: **place side by side, briefly, two moments from previous essais**. Bloom's 2-sigma — a measure of aligned-item performance under tutoring conditions, standing in as evidence for *instruction as effective as one-on-one tutoring*. Kestin's physics finding — a measure of aligned-item performance on a same-day post-test in a selective Harvard population, standing in as evidence for *AI tutoring outperforms active-learning instruction*. Both are specific findings. Both are real. Both report on what they measured. Both are cited as evidence for constructs — *tutoring effectiveness, AI tutoring effectiveness* — that are not what the measurements index.

*"The 2-sigma gap in tutoring. The Harvard AI-tutor finding. Forty years apart; different technologies; different methodologies; different research traditions. What they share is a structure: a specific measurement standing in as evidence for something it is not, strictly, a measurement of. The book has traced this structure across twelve essais. Named directly, it is the single deepest observation the book has built toward."*

Brief pause. The essai names its own project: making the structure visible as structure, not as yet another instance. Sets up the essai's work: walk the gap, show what the field has tried to do about it, show why the attempts have not taken, name what would be required for them to take.

This opening satisfies two constraints simultaneously: it anchors concretely (Bloom, Kestin — claims the reader has worked through) and it names structurally (the gap as the book's deepest observation). It also operates the spiral one last time: the apparatus, now, examines itself.

### 6. Core movement blocks

**Block A — The structure, named (4–5 pages).** Opens on Bloom and Kestin as described above. Names the structure: *a specific measurement standing in as evidence for something it is not, strictly, a measurement of.* The measurement is not false. The construct the measurement is cited as evidence for is not trivial. The gap is the thing. The essai walks through this structure once, carefully, making it visible not as critique of any specific claim but as the general shape under which the field operates. Acknowledges that proxy relationships are unavoidable in any empirical research — no measurement is the construct it indexes. The question is not whether proxies are used (they must be), but whether the proxy relationship has been established or assumed. Across twelve essais, it has almost always been assumed. That is the observation.

**Block B — What the field claims to care about (4–5 pages).** The essai names the constructs the field's claim-making vocabulary invokes. *Effectiveness.* *Learning.* *Mastery.* *Personalized learning.* *Intelligent tutoring.* *Engagement as pathway to understanding.* Each of these is a construct the field treats as something the apparatus can measure. Each is, in the cognitive science and learning-science literatures, a richly articulated construct with substantial theoretical and empirical treatment — *learning* in Bjork's sense involves storage strength, retrieval strength, durability, transfer to novel contexts, metacognitive awareness of what one knows. *Mastery* in Bloom's sense (and in the cognitive-load literature) involves not just performance on trained items but structural reorganization of knowledge. *Understanding* in a dozen traditions involves the capacity to apply, not just reproduce. The essai names these constructs carefully — not exhaustively, but enough that the reader sees what the field's vocabulary claims to be evidence for. Then names what the apparatus actually measures: aligned-item performance at short timescales. The gap between *what the apparatus measures* and *what the vocabulary claims* is not subtle. It is structural. It has been the book's subject across every essai.

**Block C — What the field has tried (5–6 pages).** The essai is honest about the history of attempts to close the gap. Transfer testing — advocated by Bransford, Brown, Cocking in *How People Learn*, and earlier by Thorndike, and never routinely adopted. Durability testing — Bjork's decades of work on the distinction between performance at training and performance after a delay, never routinely adopted. Multi-paradigm convergence — work across psychometrics and cognitive science on using multiple outcome measures to triangulate on constructs, used sometimes in research settings, almost never in deployed-product efficacy reporting. Situated assessment — Lave, Wenger, Greeno on learning as participation in practices rather than individual acquisition, influential in some learning-sciences corners, almost never reflected in efficacy claims for deployed systems. The essai walks through these traditions with respect and brevity. Each has existed for decades. Each has produced methodology that could be adopted. Each remains exceptional rather than routine in the evidence base that accompanies EdTech and adjacent claims. The essai names this, not as a complaint, but as the question the essai has been building toward: *why do these traditions not take?*

**Block D — Why the apparatus persists (4–5 pages).** The essai proposes, provisionally, what the previous twelve essais have accumulated evidence for. The apparatus persists because it serves the production conditions of the field. Researcher-designed aligned-outcome short-timescale studies produce citeable numbers at low cost in reasonable timeframes. Transfer testing is harder to design, more expensive, produces smaller and noisier effects, and gets fewer papers published. Durability testing requires follow-up over months or years that exceeds the grant cycle and the product-release cadence. Multi-paradigm convergence requires teams with methodological range that most programs do not have. Situated assessment does not fit the randomized-controlled-trial template that funding agencies and policy bodies prefer. The field's apparatus is not an accident of intellectual laziness. It is an equilibrium produced by the specific incentives around publication, funding, product release, policy adoption, and reputational reward. The essai is careful here — this is not a conspiracy claim, it is a structural observation. Individual researchers working within the apparatus are not cynical; they are responsive to the incentives that govern whether their work gets funded, published, and cited. The apparatus is not what any individual chose. It is what the incentives produced. That the reader now can see it as an apparatus, rather than as the neutral background against which research occurs, is the book's central deliverable.

**Block E — The Revise move (3–4 pages).** What would more adequate evidence look like, provisionally? The essai resists the prescriptive register — *this is what the field must do* — and stays in the examinational register — *this is what we can now see would be required, while acknowledging that seeing what is required does not produce it.* The sketch: multiple outcome measures on the same intervention, reported openly rather than selectively. Durability testing at three, six, twelve months as baseline expectation rather than exceptional addition. Transfer testing as standard in any claim about learning (as distinct from claims about training on specific items). Cost reporting as first-class variable in any comparative claim. Pre-registration of analytic plans, with reported deviations. Convergence across developer-designed and standardized measures. Population diversity beyond the initial research setting. None of this is novel. All of it has been advocated for, across decades, in specific research traditions. The essai names this list not as a reform program but as the specific shape of what the apparatus does not currently produce — which is what the reader can now see. What the field does with this shape is the field's question. The book's work is to make the shape visible.

**Block F — The reader, integrated (2–3 pages).** Short closing block. The reader now holds the distinction between measure and construct as a working tool. They hold it against the twelve essais they have read; they can apply it to claims the book did not cover; they can articulate, provisionally, what would close the gap. The apparatus the book examined is now visible to the reader as apparatus — not as the neutral way learning research happens, but as a specific equilibrium of measurement choices that can be examined as choices. That shift — from seeing what the apparatus produces as the findings, to seeing the apparatus itself as the thing produced — is what the book has delivered. Bridge to Essai 14: the closing essai does not add to this. It releases the reader with the apparatus as theirs.

### 7. Anchor material

- **Primary anchor (structural):** Bloom 1984 and Kestin 2024, placed side by side at the opening. Both are claims the reader has worked through in prior essais; the essai does not re-read them but uses them to name the structure they share.
- **Primary anchor (construct literature):** Robert Bjork on storage strength vs. retrieval strength (Bjork & Bjork 1992 and subsequent work); the distinction between training performance and post-training durability. Used in Block B to illustrate what *learning* means in a serious construct tradition.
- **Primary anchor (methodological tradition):** John Bransford, Ann Brown, Rodney Cocking, eds., *How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School* (National Academies Press, 1999, expanded edition 2000). Used in Block C for transfer-testing and multi-paradigm convergence advocacy.
- **Secondary anchors:** Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger, *Situated Learning* (1991), and subsequent situated-cognition work, for situated assessment. Thorndike's early-20th-century work on transfer for historical depth. Recent work on multi-measure convergence in psychometrics (specific source to select).
- **Tertiary anchors:** Brief references across Block C to specific learning-science traditions the book has gestured toward but not developed — generative-learning research (Mayer, Fiorella), interleaving and spaced-practice work (Rohrer, Taylor, Kornell), deliberate-practice research (Ericsson). One-sentence mentions.
- **No new specific EdTech claims cited.** The essai uses the twelve prior essais as its evidence base. Adding new efficacy claims here would make the essai an additional close reading rather than a convergence point.

**Anchor discipline:** The essai is the only one in the book that cites construct-literature seriously. That is deliberate. Earlier essais could not do this without seeming to demand the reader have learning-sciences background. By Essai 13, the reader has earned access to this material — they have the apparatus, they have watched specific cases, they are ready to see that the construct literature has been present across the whole book even when unnamed.

### 8. Essai closing / bridge to Essai 14

The essai closes by handing the reader the apparatus as their own. *"What remains is not further examination by this book, but the reader's examination of whatever claim they encounter next. The closing essai does not extend the examination. It names what has been delivered and releases the reader to it."* Direct bridge to Essai 14.

### 9. Contributor notes

This is the book's most craft-demanding essai. The failure modes are specific and each is load-bearing.

- **Do not summarize the previous twelve essais.** The essai's discipline is to name the convergence point the essais have built toward, not to review what they showed. A draft that restates the book is a draft that has failed this essai's assignment. The reader has read the book. The essai names what the book was for.

- **Do not become philosophical.** The essai is abstract by the standards of the book — it is about structure rather than cases — but it is not philosophy. It does not engage philosophy-of-science literature at length. It does not invoke Kuhn or Popper or Cartwright or Hacking. The essai stays in the examinational register: what the apparatus does, what it does not do, what more adequate apparatus would require. A draft that turns this essai into an epistemology chapter has drifted.

- **Do not prescribe.** The Revise move in Block E is provisional and sharply constrained. The essai does not say *the field must adopt X, Y, Z.* It says *what more adequate evidence would require is visible now; what the field does with that visibility is its own question.* Watch every sentence in Block E for drift into prescription. The register difference between *"this is what would be required"* (examinational) and *"this is what the field must do"* (prescriptive) is narrow but decisive.

- **Do not indict the field's workers.** Block D's structural point — that the apparatus persists because it serves production conditions — is load-bearing and easy to overstate. Individual researchers are not the target; the equilibrium of incentives is. A draft that reads as *researchers are intellectually captured by the apparatus* has gone wrong. The correct register is *researchers, responding rationally to incentives, reproduce an apparatus that no individual researcher designed and no individual researcher can change alone.* That is a different kind of claim.

- **The construct literature in Block B must be accurate.** Bjork, Bransford, Lave, the others — the essai cites these to establish that the gap between measurement and construct is not a novel observation, it has been named in specific traditions for decades. If the citations are inaccurate, the essai loses its credibility at the most important moment. The author or contributor drafting this block should be familiar with the primary sources, not summaries of them.

- **The essai's length discipline matters.** The essai is long — the longest in the book outside Essai 10. But every page must be earning its place, because the reader is near the end and fatigue is high. Watch for paragraphs that restate what the previous sentence said in slightly different words. Watch for repetition across blocks. Watch for the essai answering objections that no reader would raise. The essai's power depends on compression.

- **The Integrate move must land.** Block F is short — three pages — but it is where the book's Integrate-level posture shift is delivered or missed. The reader must finish Block F holding the measure-vs-construct distinction as theirs, not as the book's claim about the world. The difference is experiential: if the reader finishes Block F thinking *the apparatus is broken*, the essai has produced a critique-level shift. If the reader finishes thinking *I now see the apparatus as apparatus*, the essai has produced the Integrate-level shift. The latter is the assignment. The phrasing of Block F is where that lands or fails.

- **Do not end on a flourish.** The essai's end bridges to Essai 14. Essai 14 is the closing. Essai 13 is not the closing — it is the deepest essai, which is different. A draft that tries to end Essai 13 with a memorable closing line has confused the essai with the one that follows. The bridge is plain, functional, and clear: *the examination concludes; the closing releases the reader to what the examination has left them.*

**Primary sources to have at hand:** Bloom 1984; Kestin 2024; Bjork & Bjork 1992 on desirable difficulties and storage/retrieval; *How People Learn* (2000 edition); Lave & Wenger 1991; a selection from the construct-validation literature in psychometrics (Messick 1989 is canonical); Thorndike on transfer; the author's own drafts of Essais 1–12 for continuity and accurate cross-reference.

### 10. Projected length

**28–32 pages.** Longest essai in the book outside Essai 2. Essai 2 installs the apparatus; Essai 13 names what the apparatus was suppressing all along. Both require room. The essai should not exceed 32 pages; 30 is the target. Anything longer suggests the essai has started summarizing or philosophizing rather than converging.

## Relationship to the rest of the book

A few specific observations about how Essai 13 sits with the others:

**Essai 2 and Essai 13 are the book's structural anchors.** Essai 2 installs the apparatus; Essai 13 names what the apparatus was suppressing. Together, these two essais contain the book's analytical core. Essais 3–12 are the spiral that builds the reader's habit; Essai 14 is the release. A reader who read only Essais 2 and 13 would have much of the book's intellectual content, but without the habit the spiral builds — which is why the spiral is there.

**The construct literature in Block B is the book's only sustained engagement with learning-science theory.** Across Essais 1–12, the book has mostly held learning-science theory in the background, cited in passing, respected but not developed. Essai 13 brings it forward. This is not a late reveal; it is the moment where the previous essais' careful restraint pays off. The reader is ready to see the construct literature because they have watched, across twelve essais, exactly the kinds of claims the construct literature was built to evaluate more carefully.

**Block D's "apparatus persists because of incentives" argument is the book's strongest structural claim.** It is also the claim most vulnerable to misreading. If Block D reads as cynical, the book has failed its register commitment. If it reads as sociological — describing how the field's incentives shape the apparatus — the book has done its hardest structural work honestly.

**The Revise move in Block E is the whole book's Revise moment.** No other essai delivers Revise-level posture shift (Essais 10 and 12 deliver Revise at local scope; Essai 13 delivers it at the whole-apparatus scope). The move must be provisional, specific, and short.
