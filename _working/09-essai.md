# On Engagement as Evidence

*Essai 9 — Draft*

---

In April of 2026, Matt Barnum published a piece in *Chalkbeat* titled *Why Sal Khan's AI revolution hasn't happened yet*. The piece was an interview with Sal Khan and Kristen DiCerbo, Khan Academy's Chief Learning Officer, about the three-year arc of Khanmigo — the AI-powered tutor Khan had announced from the TED stage in 2023 as "probably the biggest positive transformation that education has ever seen." By April 2026, the public framing had changed. "For a lot of students," Khan told Barnum, "it was a non-event. They just didn't use it much." DiCerbo, speaking alongside him, offered her own observation. "So far," she said, "I am not seeing the revolution in education."

Then she made the observation that this essai is built around. Students who did engage with Khanmigo, DiCerbo said, often responded to its questions with *"IDK IDK"* — *I don't know, I don't know*. The tool was designed around Socratic dialogue, around the idea that an AI tutor could ask the kinds of prompting questions a skilled human tutor asks, helping students work out answers rather than supplying answers directly. What DiCerbo observed was that students, faced with the AI's questions, frequently declined to engage with the thinking the system was trying to prompt. They typed "IDK IDK" and moved on. The platform recorded them as active users. The platform's engagement metrics were, in their own terms, functioning. What was not functioning was whatever the metrics were supposed to be evidence of.

I want to pair DiCerbo's observation with a different kind of statement from a different company. Luis von Ahn, CEO of Duolingo, has said for over a decade that his goal is to make language learning as addictive as a mobile game. He has said this from the TED stage, in press interviews, in podcast appearances, in the company's own blog posts. He has been admirably transparent about what Duolingo optimizes for. The company's business model is built on sustained daily engagement. The metrics it reports to investors lead with daily active users, monthly active users, streak holders, lessons completed, questions answered, total minutes. These are the numbers the company's valuation rests on. They are the numbers that have made Duolingo into one of the largest publicly traded education companies in the world. They are numbers about engagement.

What I want to examine in this essai is the relationship between these two things — the engagement metrics Duolingo reports as evidence of its success, and DiCerbo's observation of what engagement can actually look like when you watch it happen. Both companies are reporting on user engagement with their platforms. One is reporting engagement as evidence of effectiveness. The other is reporting engagement as insufficient evidence of effectiveness. Both are being honest, in their different ways, about what they see. The difference between them is the subject of this essai.

---

I want to say something carefully before going further, because the essai I am about to write can easily drift into a mode I want to avoid. I am not arguing that engagement metrics are meaningless. A product with zero engagement is doing nothing — nobody is using it, and whatever the product was designed to do, it is not doing it. A product with high engagement is doing *something* — people are returning to it, spending time on it, treating it as part of their routine. These are real facts. Companies are right to measure them, to track them, to respond to changes in them. Engagement matters.

What engagement does not do, and what the rest of this essai examines, is answer the question that educational technologies routinely position themselves as answering. That question is: *do people learn from this?* A product can have extraordinarily high engagement and produce no learning, and another product can have modest engagement and produce substantial learning, and both possibilities are consistent with ordinary observations of how humans interact with media. The relationship between engagement and learning is not zero, but it is also not one, and the gap between these two propositions is where this essai lives.

The rhetorical move I want to examine is the substitution: engagement metrics reported as if they answered the learning question. It is a specific move, performed in specific contexts, with specific consequences. It is not fraud. It is not even dishonesty in most cases. It is a reporting choice that becomes analytically meaningful because what is left unreported — harder evidence of what users actually learned — happens to be harder to produce, easier to dispute, and therefore less attractive to report. The substitution works precisely because engagement metrics are real and measurable, and because the harder evidence is scarce and contested, and because the audience for these reports often does not notice that one has stood in for the other.

---

Consider how the substitution actually works in a company like Duolingo.

The company has, according to its own public reporting, tens of millions of daily active users. It reports lessons completed in the billions. It reports learners across hundreds of languages and nearly every country in the world. The scale of engagement Duolingo has produced is genuinely extraordinary; nothing else in the history of language education has reached this number of users in this way. The company's investor communications report these numbers quarterly, track their growth, and position them as evidence of the company's continued relevance and effectiveness.

What does the company say about learning outcomes?

The most widely cited finding is the one from a 2012 study commissioned by Duolingo and conducted by Roumen Vesselinov and John Grego. The study found that approximately 34 hours of Duolingo use produces the equivalent of one semester of college Spanish. This finding has circulated for more than a decade as evidence of Duolingo's effectiveness, cited in the company's own marketing, in press coverage, in academic discussion. It has become, effectively, the canonical piece of evidence that Duolingo teaches language.

Read carefully, the study supports a narrower claim than the one that has circulated. The outcome measure was the WebCAPE — the Web-Based Computer Adaptive Placement Exam. WebCAPE is a validated placement test, designed to assess where students should be placed in a sequence of college Spanish courses. It is not a comprehensive measure of language proficiency. It measures, in particular, receptive skills — reading comprehension, grammatical recognition at the sentence level — rather than productive skills like speaking and writing. A student who performs well on WebCAPE has demonstrated specific kinds of Spanish knowledge; they have not demonstrated that they can hold a conversation, write a paragraph, understand a native speaker in natural context, or navigate the cultural and pragmatic dimensions of actual language use. The study's finding was a specific finding on a specific instrument: Duolingo users, after approximately 34 hours, tested at a WebCAPE level comparable to students who had completed one semester of college Spanish.

There are additional specifications worth noting. The 34 hours was a point estimate; the 95 percent confidence interval around it ran from 26 to 49 hours. The study's sample was restricted to native English speakers in the United States, excluding anyone of Hispanic origin and anyone with prior Spanish knowledge — a specific demographic whose results may not generalize to the broader population of learners Duolingo's marketing addresses. The study was commissioned by Duolingo, conducted by researchers selected by Duolingo, and the study's methodology was designed in consultation with Duolingo. This is not a scandal; industry-commissioned research is standard in many fields. But it does mean the finding should be read as the company's own evidence about itself, not as an independent validation.

More recent Duolingo research follows similar patterns. A 2021 study published in *Foreign Language Annals* by Jiang and colleagues — with several authors affiliated with Duolingo — reported that learners who completed particular unit thresholds on Duolingo tested at proficiency levels comparable to students who had completed multiple semesters of university study, on reading and listening assessments. The authors did not comprehensively test speaking and writing. The findings, like Vesselinov-Grego, are finding about receptive skills on specific instruments, conducted at least in part by Duolingo-affiliated researchers.

What is missing from this evidence base is the kind of study that would answer the question Duolingo's marketing implicitly claims to answer. That study would be: an independent researcher, not commissioned or funded by Duolingo, evaluates a representative sample of Duolingo users against comprehensive independent proficiency measures (CEFR-aligned oral proficiency interviews, independent writing assessments, listening comprehension in naturalistic contexts), at a delayed follow-up long enough to distinguish durable learning from short-term performance. Studies of this character exist for some educational interventions. For Duolingo, at the time of this writing, they largely do not. The company's learning-outcome evidence base is, in the strict sense, thinner than its engagement-metric evidence base by multiple orders of magnitude.

This is the substitution. Daily active users measured continuously and reported quarterly. Learning outcomes measured in a small number of commissioned studies over more than a decade, on receptive-skill instruments, often by researchers with company affiliations. The first category of evidence is vast and well-funded; the second is narrow and selective. When Duolingo's public communications describe the company's success, they lead with the first and reference the second as supplementary. When the audience for these communications — investors, journalists, policymakers, educators — takes the communications at face value, they are accepting a substitution they may not notice is being performed.

---

I want to pause on something specific before going further.

Duolingo has not, to my knowledge, ever claimed that its engagement metrics *are* learning outcomes. The company's public communications are more careful than that. What Duolingo does is report engagement metrics prominently, report learning-outcome findings narrowly, and allow the audience to draw the inference that the two are closely connected. This is a rhetorical choice, not a factual claim. A factual claim can be wrong; a rhetorical choice can be challenged but cannot be falsified in the same way.

This is why the essai is called *On Engagement as Evidence* rather than *On Engagement as Deception*. The pattern the essai examines is not that companies lie about what their engagement metrics show. The pattern is that companies report engagement metrics in contexts where the audience expects learning-outcome evidence, and allow the audience to treat the former as a reasonable approximation of the latter. The audience is not being lied to. The audience is being given what is easiest to measure, in a format that fits the audience's expectations for what evidence looks like, in a way that the audience often does not interrogate.

This pattern is not unique to Duolingo. It is close to universal across consumer educational technology. Khan Academy has reported engagement metrics as evidence of reach and impact for more than a decade. Quizlet and Kahoot and Chegg and Course Hero all report substantial engagement metrics. K-12 district-purchased platforms produce dashboards full of engagement data that administrators review as evidence of the platforms' value. The pattern is the genre, not a defect within it. What is unusual about Duolingo is not that it reports engagement metrics; what is unusual is the scale of the engagement the company has produced, which makes the substitution particularly legible.

---

Now return to DiCerbo.

What DiCerbo observed with Khanmigo is what the inside of the substitution looks like when someone is willing to describe it honestly. Students used the AI. They typed characters into its chat interface. They appeared as engaged users in the platform's analytics. If Khan Academy had chosen to, it could have reported to its funders and its public that Khanmigo had reached a certain number of students, that students had initiated a certain number of sessions, that the platform had processed a certain number of interactions. All of these numbers would have been true. None of them would have answered the question Khan Academy was asking.

Instead, DiCerbo looked at what the students were doing inside those interactions. They were typing "IDK IDK." They were declining to engage with the thinking the tool was designed to prompt. The engagement, measured behaviorally, was there. The engagement, measured cognitively — in terms of the mental work the tool was supposed to support — was not. DiCerbo could have reported the behavioral engagement and called it a success. She did not. She said, publicly, that the tool was not producing what it was designed to produce, and that she and Khan had not yet figured out how to solve the problem.

This is a specific kind of disciplinary move. Khan Academy could have done what most EdTech companies do in situations like this: pivot the marketing, narrow the claims, emphasize sub-groups where engagement was higher, report the engagement metrics as evidence of reach without making claims about outcomes. DiCerbo chose to describe, in public, what the inside of Khanmigo's engagement actually looked like. She named the gap between behavioral engagement and cognitive engagement in the specific case of her own organization's product. She did this without being forced to, without public pressure that had reached a threshold requiring a statement, without regulatory action, without litigation. She did it because it was true and because someone should say so.

I want to credit this clearly. In a field where the structural incentives run entirely the other direction — where the reputational cost of admitting that your product is not producing what you claimed is large, and the reputational cost of letting engagement metrics stand as effectiveness evidence is small — DiCerbo did the harder thing. She said the metrics were not the thing. She said the engagement was not the evidence. This essai is not about her. But her willingness to describe what she observed is the specific example of what the disciplinary move looks like when someone chooses to make it.

---

There is, in the learning-sciences research literature, a distinction that helps make sense of what DiCerbo observed.

Roger Azevedo and a substantial line of research on self-regulated learning distinguish between two kinds of engagement: *behavioral* and *cognitive*. Behavioral engagement is what platforms can measure — the clicks, the sessions, the time on task, the lessons completed, the questions attempted. It is observable from outside. Cognitive engagement is what happens inside the learner: the active construction of understanding, the monitoring of one's own comprehension, the deployment of effortful strategies for making sense of material, the metacognitive attention to whether one's understanding is actually developing. It is not directly observable from outside. It has to be inferred from indirect evidence — from the quality of responses rather than the presence of them, from later performance on transfer tasks, from what the learner can do days or months after the engagement occurred.

The distinction matters because behavioral engagement and cognitive engagement can dissociate. A student can be behaviorally engaged — clicking through a lesson, answering questions, collecting points — without being cognitively engaged. This is what DiCerbo observed: students behaviorally present in Khanmigo's interface, cognitively absent from the thinking the tool was designed to prompt. The platform's metrics captured the behavioral presence; they could not capture the cognitive absence. The substitution of engagement for learning is partly the substitution of behavioral engagement for cognitive engagement — the measurement of the observable proxy for the unobservable thing the proxy is supposed to index.

This distinction also helps make sense of the Duolingo pattern. High daily active user numbers are evidence of sustained behavioral engagement; what Duolingo's marketing invites the audience to infer is that the behavioral engagement is producing cognitive engagement — active, effortful construction of language competence. Whether it is doing so is the question the engagement metrics cannot answer. The answer to that question would require studies of what users can actually do with the language after extended Duolingo use, measured independently, against proficiency standards that reflect actual language use rather than aligned item performance. Those studies are the ones the evidence base is thin on.

---

There is one more dimension of this pattern worth naming before the essai closes.

The substitution of engagement for learning is not only a reporting choice. It is, in many cases, a design choice. Products designed to maximize engagement are designed, whether intentionally or not, around mechanisms that reliably produce engagement — loss aversion (streak-loss penalties), social comparison (leaderboards), variable-ratio reinforcement (XP, badges, surprises), and structured push notification cadences. These mechanisms are genuinely effective at producing their target outcome, which is continued use of the product. They are not the same mechanisms that produce learning, which tend to involve effortful retrieval, desirable difficulty, spaced practice, and occasional genuine failure. The research on desirable difficulties, particularly Robert Bjork's work, has shown consistently that short-term performance and long-term learning can dissociate in ways that make engagement-optimized products specifically ill-suited to learning production.

This is not a charge of incompetence or of bad intent. Most educational-technology products are built by people who genuinely want students to learn, and who reasonably believe that a product students will not use cannot teach them anything. The engagement-first design philosophy follows from a sensible premise. The problem is that the optimization for engagement can, in its mature form, produce products that are extremely effective at maintaining usage and only modestly effective at producing learning — and that the evidentiary apparatus around such products tends to report the engagement-effectiveness without reporting the learning-effectiveness, because the first is easy to measure and the second is not.

When a product has been optimized for engagement over many years, with the full machinery of contemporary behavior-design research applied to making it maximally sticky, the engagement metrics it produces should be read not as neutral indicators of the product's value but as indicators of the product's success at a specific task. That specific task is not, necessarily, teaching. It is keeping users using. These are not the same task. The evidentiary apparatus of educational technology, by reporting engagement metrics as if they were neutral indicators of learning value, elides this distinction at scale.

---

What, then, is the alternative? What does harder evidence look like?

The research on high-dosage tutoring provides one answer. Programs like Saga Education and MATCH Corps and others have been evaluated using independent outcome measures — standardized assessments, state test performance, subsequent course grades, college enrollment rates — over sustained periods, with delayed follow-ups, by researchers not employed by the tutoring organizations. These evaluations have produced findings that are, by educational-research standards, substantial: measurable effects on learning outcomes that persist past the end of the intervention, with effect sizes in the 0.15 to 0.40 standard-deviation range depending on specifics. These findings cost something to produce — the evaluations themselves are expensive, and the underlying tutoring interventions are expensive. What they produce is evidence about learning, not evidence about engagement.

The contrast with the consumer EdTech evidence base is stark. Saga Education's evaluation record is a few dozen studies, most of them independently conducted, using external outcome measures, over periods of months to years. Duolingo's evaluation record on learning outcomes is a handful of studies, most of them commissioned or authored by Duolingo, using aligned instruments, at relatively short timescales. The cost structures match: Saga's tutoring costs on the order of thousands of dollars per student per year; Duolingo is free at the consumer level. The evidence structures match the cost structures. More rigorous evidence is more expensive to produce, and the interventions for which such evidence is produced tend to be the interventions expensive enough to justify the investment. The cheap interventions tend to be evaluated cheaply, which means evaluated against the metrics their infrastructure already captures — which means evaluated on engagement.

This is the structural observation the essai wants to end on. The substitution of engagement for learning in EdTech's evidentiary apparatus is not solely a rhetorical move. It is also an economic one. Rigorous learning-outcome evaluation is expensive, requires independent researchers, takes years, and produces findings that are often disappointing. Engagement measurement is cheap, requires only the platform's own analytics, produces findings in real time, and is essentially guaranteed to produce some number the company can report. The field has arranged itself to favor the evidence it is cheaper and easier to produce over the evidence it is expensive and difficult to produce, and it has arranged its rhetorical conventions to allow the easier evidence to travel in contexts where the harder evidence is what is actually being claimed.

---

Here is what I would like you to carry out of this essai.

When you next encounter an educational technology's effectiveness claim and the evidence offered is engagement data — daily active users, sessions per week, lessons completed, time on platform, streak counts, badges earned — ask yourself two questions.

The first: is this engagement data, or is it learning-outcome data? The answer is often obvious once you ask. Engagement data describes what users do with the platform. Learning-outcome data describes what users can do afterward, outside the platform, measured independently. They are not the same category. If what is being claimed is that the product produces learning, and what is being offered as evidence is that users engage with the product, the evidence is in a different category from the claim. The substitution has been performed.

The second question: if this product were not producing learning, would anyone notice? For products whose evidentiary apparatus rests on engagement metrics alone, the answer is often no. Users who are behaviorally engaged and not cognitively engaged still show up in the analytics. Platforms can maintain high engagement metrics for years without producing the learning outcomes the metrics implicitly promise. Khan Academy's willingness to notice, through DiCerbo's "IDK IDK" observation, that its AI tutor was not producing what it was designed to produce, is unusual because most organizations cannot see this from inside their own metrics. If the product's only feedback loop is engagement, the product can only see what engagement measures.

These two questions do not tell you whether any specific product works. They tell you what kind of evidence you have about it, and they tell you where to look for the evidence that would answer the question the engagement metrics cannot. Usually, the harder evidence either does not exist or is much narrower than the product's public claims imply. This fact is itself information. A product whose public claims substantially outrun its learning-outcome evidence base is not necessarily a bad product; it is a product whose claims you should calibrate accordingly.

The next essai examines a specific wave of claims that has generated substantial new evidence in the last four years — the AI-tutor studies of Kestin, Eedi, Rori, and others, which have been cited as indicating that this generation of systems is finally producing learning-outcome evidence worth attention. The essai reads that evidence through the apparatus the first nine essais have built, and asks what, specifically, the current moment's AI tutors have demonstrated.

### What I am not sure about, and what I am asking from you

This essai is, in many ways, the essai in the volume most in tension with itself. Its subject is a rhetorical substitution that the essai is also trying to avoid performing in reverse — critiquing engagement metrics without dismissing engagement as a phenomenon, crediting DiCerbo's honesty without letting the credit become sentimental, naming Duolingo's pattern without accusing the company of fraud. I want to name specifically where I am unsure I have held these balances.

I am not sure I have given enough credit to what engagement does accomplish. A substantial portion of this essai treats engagement as a proxy that has been substituted for something more important. There is a different reading in which engagement, for large and diverse populations of learners, is itself a non-trivial accomplishment. Reaching tens of millions of people in hundreds of languages is a real thing, even if the depth of what each of those people learns is modest. A reader who believes Duolingo has produced genuine value at extraordinary scale, even without the learning-outcome evidence base I am asking for, has a case to make. The essai does not engage that case in its strongest form. If this absence matters, tell me.

I am not sure the Duolingo treatment lands where I want it to. I have tried to describe the company's pattern without indicting the company, to treat von Ahn's transparency about his engagement-first philosophy as credit rather than as ammunition, to characterize the substitution as rhetorical rather than as fraudulent. Whether this balance holds under the specific paragraphs is something a reader of Duolingo's work — or someone who works at the company, or who has taught with it, or who uses it and has learned a language from it — is better positioned to judge than I am. If the essai has drifted into polemic, it will not have accomplished what I intended.

I am not sure about the Azevedo behavioral-vs-cognitive distinction. The general distinction is well-established in the self-regulated-learning literature, but the specific characterization I have given may not match Azevedo's own formulation precisely. The structural point — that platforms can measure behavioral engagement and cannot directly measure cognitive engagement, which is the thing actually of interest for learning — stands independent of attribution, but I have attached Azevedo's name to it, and a reader who knows the literature better than I do should correct me if I have oversimplified.

Finally, I am not sure the closing two questions — *is this engagement data or learning-outcome data* and *if this product were not producing learning, would anyone notice* — are the right two. They are the questions that fall out of the essai's argument. They are practical. But a reader who applies them will find, I think, that they apply to almost every educational-technology effectiveness claim in circulation, which raises a question the essai does not answer: *what should we do, given that nearly every such claim fails these questions?* The essai gestures at the answer (look for harder evidence, calibrate claims against the evidence available) but does not develop the response. A reader who wants a fuller answer has a fair case for asking for it.

The next essai examines the current-moment AI tutor studies that many readers will be most interested in. I hope you will continue with me.
